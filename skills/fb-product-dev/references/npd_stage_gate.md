# F&B New Product Development: Stage-Gate Process

## Overview

The stage-gate model is the dominant NPD framework in consumer packaged goods (CPG) food and beverage. It imposes structured decision points (gates) between phases of increasing investment, ensuring that only commercially viable projects advance. A typical F&B company running 20-30 active concepts will kill 60-70% before Stage 3 -- this is the system working correctly.

**Typical timelines:**
- Line extension (new flavor, size): 8-12 months concept to shelf
- New product within existing brand: 12-18 months
- New brand / novel ingredient platform: 18-30 months
- Products requiring novel food / GRAS notification: 24-36+ months

---

## Stage 0: Discovery & Ideation

**Objective:** Generate a pipeline of commercially promising concepts grounded in consumer need, market white space, and organizational capability.

### Activities

| Activity | Detail |
|---|---|
| Trend analysis | Monitor SPINS/IRI/Circana data, trade publications (BevNET, Food Navigator), trade shows (Expo West, NACS, Fancy Food), social listening |
| White space mapping | Plot existing category offerings on attribute maps (e.g., sugar content vs. functionality, price tier vs. format) to identify unoccupied positions |
| Consumer insight | Qualitative (focus groups, ethnography, online communities) and quantitative (surveys, MaxDiff preference ranking) research |
| Competitive audit | SKU-level analysis of competitors -- pricing architecture, claims, distribution (ACV), velocity, promotional cadence |
| Internal ideation | Cross-functional workshops (R&D, marketing, sales, ops) using frameworks like SCAMPER, jobs-to-be-done, occasion mapping |
| Technology scouting | Ingredient supplier innovation days, university partnerships, patent landscape review |

### Outputs
- Concept briefs (1-2 pages each): target consumer, occasion, key benefit, preliminary positioning, estimated price point
- Prioritized pipeline ranked by strategic fit, market size, technical feasibility
- Typical pipeline: 15-30 concepts entering Stage 1 screening

### Common Failure Points
- Over-indexing on trends without validating consumer willingness to pay
- Ignoring supply chain feasibility ("great idea, but the ingredient doesn't exist at scale")
- Skipping competitive audit and entering a saturated sub-segment

---

## Stage 1: Scoping

**Objective:** Rapidly screen concepts to a short list using lightweight analysis. Spend days, not weeks.

### Activities

| Activity | Detail |
|---|---|
| Concept screening | Quantitative concept test (n=200-400) measuring purchase intent, uniqueness, relevance, believability |
| Preliminary business case | Top-down market sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM), rough COGS estimate, target margin range |
| Technical feasibility | R&D assessment: Can we formulate this? What are the critical unknowns? Any novel ingredients requiring GRAS? |
| Regulatory scan | High-level review: any claims that require clinical substantiation? Organic certification needed? State-specific issues (Prop 65)? |
| IP review | Freedom-to-operate check on product name, claims, packaging format |

### Gate 1 Review Criteria

| Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Purchase intent (top-2 box) | >= 40% among target consumers |
| Estimated market size (SOM) | >= $10M retail in year 3 |
| Technical feasibility | Green or yellow (no red flags requiring novel food petition) |
| Strategic fit | Aligns with brand architecture and channel strategy |
| Competitive differentiation | Clear point of difference vs. top 3 competitors |

### Kill Criteria
- Top-2 box purchase intent < 30%
- No viable path to target gross margin (typically 40-55% for premium beverages)
- Regulatory blocker with no workaround (e.g., unapproved health claim essential to positioning)
- Cannibalization > 30% of existing portfolio revenue

### Outputs
- 5-8 concepts advancing to Stage 2
- Killed concepts documented with rationale (feed back into ideation)

---

## Stage 2: Business Case

**Objective:** Build a rigorous financial and operational case for each surviving concept. This is the last "cheap" gate -- Stage 3 requires real R&D and co-man investment.

### Activities

| Activity | Detail |
|---|---|
| Detailed financial model | 3-year P&L pro forma: revenue build by channel/retailer, COGS (ingredients, packaging, co-man tolling, freight), gross margin, trade spend, marketing spend, contribution margin |
| Retail pricing architecture | SRP positioning relative to segment (value / mainstream / premium / super-premium), margin stacks for retailer and distributor |
| Trade spend modeling | Introductory allowances (typically $1-3/case), scan-back promotions, MCB (manufacturer chargeback) rates, demo/sampling budget |
| Channel strategy | Prioritized channel roadmap: natural/specialty first vs. conventional grocery vs. convenience/gas vs. e-commerce (DTC, Amazon) |
| Co-manufacturer identification | Issue RFIs to 3-5 qualified co-mans; assess capabilities, capacity, location, pricing, minimums, quality certifications (SQF/BRC/FSSC 22000) |
| Packaging exploration | Structural design concepts, substrate selection (can, glass, PET, Tetra Pak, pouch), preliminary cost estimates from packaging suppliers |
| Brand / design brief | Creative brief for agency or in-house design: brand identity, visual language, flavor cues, shelf impact requirements |

### Financial Benchmarks (Premium RTD Beverages)

| Metric | Target Range |
|---|---|
| SRP (12 oz single serve) | $2.49 - $4.99 |
| Gross margin (at FOB) | 45-60% |
| COGS per unit | $0.55 - $1.20 |
| Trade spend (% net revenue) | 15-25% (year 1, heavy for distribution build) |
| Marketing spend (% net revenue) | 10-20% (year 1) |
| Contribution margin | 15-30% (year 1); 25-40% (year 3) |
| Payback period | 18-36 months |

### Gate 2 Review Criteria

| Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---|
| 3-year NPV | Positive at hurdle rate (typically 12-15%) |
| Year 1 revenue forecast | Supported by retailer/distributor pipeline |
| Co-man identified | At least 2 qualified candidates with capacity |
| Packaging feasible | Structural design validated, tooling costs budgeted |
| Brand/design | Creative direction approved by brand team |
| Regulatory path | Clear claim strategy; no unresolved blockers |

### Kill Criteria
- Negative NPV under base case assumptions
- No co-manufacturer willing to run the product at required volume
- Packaging cost makes COGS unviable
- Competitive launch during scoping that eliminates differentiation

## Stage 3: Development

**Objective:** Create the product, package, and label. This is where R&D, regulatory, and design execute in parallel.

### Activities

| Activity | Detail |
|---|---|
| Bench-top formulation | Iterative formula development: target Brix, pH, flavor profile, color, mouthfeel, stability. Typically 15-50 iterations. |
| Sensory panels | Internal trained panel for descriptive profiling; consumer panel (n >= 100) for acceptance and optimization (see `sensory_evaluation.md`) |
| Packaging development | Structural design finalization, print-ready artwork, die lines, color proofs, pre-production packaging samples |
| Shelf life initiation | Real-time and accelerated shelf life studies; sensory, micro, chemical, physical stability at 0, 30, 60, 90, 180, 270, 365 days |
| Production trial planning | Detailed trial protocol with co-man: ingredients sourced, equipment configured, sampling plan, acceptance criteria |
| Regulatory review | Full label review: Nutrition Facts, ingredient statement, claims, allergens, organic certification, Prop 65, state-specific (see `labeling_claims.md`) |
| Label development | Nutrition Facts panel generation, ingredient statement drafting, front-of-pack claims, back-panel copy, UPC assignment |
| Process authority engagement | For acidified/low-acid beverages: engage process authority to develop scheduled process, file FDA Form 2541 |

### Resource Requirements
- R&D: 0.5-1.0 FTE for 3-6 months (formulation scientist + technician)
- Regulatory: 40-80 hours (in-house or consultant at $150-300/hr)
- Design: $15K-$75K (agency) or 0.25 FTE (in-house) for 2-3 months
- Shelf life: $5K-$15K (third-party lab) or in-house lab time
- Sensory: $15K-$50K per consumer CLT; $2K-$5K per trained panel session

### Gate 3 Review Criteria

| Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Formula locked | Gold standard sample approved by cross-functional team |
| Consumer acceptance | Overall liking >= 7.0 on 9-point hedonic scale; top-2 box purchase intent >= 50% |
| Shelf life on track | Accelerated data supports target shelf life (typically 9-12 months for ambient beverages) |
| Label approved | Regulatory and legal sign-off on all claims and compliance elements |
| Production trial scheduled | Co-man confirmed, ingredients on order, trial date set |
| Packaging ready | Print-ready files approved, packaging on order or at printer |

### Kill Criteria
- Consumer acceptance below 6.0 overall liking after 3+ reformulation cycles
- Shelf life failure with no viable reformulation fix (e.g., protein precipitation, color degradation)
- Regulatory finding that invalidates core claims
- COGS escalation pushing gross margin below minimum threshold

## Stage 4: Testing & Validation

**Objective:** Prove the product can be manufactured at scale and confirm consumer and trade acceptance.

### Activities

| Activity | Detail |
|---|---|
| Pilot / production trial | First run at co-man (typically minimum run: 5,000-50,000 units). Validate process parameters, yields, quality specs. |
| Shelf life confirmation | Continue real-time study; confirm accelerated predictions hold |
| Consumer testing (if not done in Stage 3) | CLT or HUT with production samples (not bench samples) |
| Retailer presentations | Category review decks, sell sheets, broker presentations. Content: market data, consumer insights, margin story, promotional plan, planogram recommendations. |
| Broker alignment | Engage regional or national broker network; set distribution targets by account and timeline |
| Trade show planning | Booth, samples, sell sheets, meeting schedule for Expo West/East, NACS, regional shows |
| Logistics setup | Warehouse/3PL selection, freight lane pricing, order management system configuration, EDI setup with key retailers |

### Production Trial Protocol

1. **Pre-production meeting** (2-4 weeks before): Review formula, process parameters, packaging specs, sampling plan, acceptance criteria
2. **Ingredient staging** (1-2 weeks before): All ingredients received, CoAs reviewed, stored per spec
3. **Line setup** (day of): Equipment calibration, CIP verification, first-article inspection of packaging
4. **Startup sampling**: First 50-100 units -- check fill volume, Brix, pH, cap torque, seam integrity, label placement
5. **Steady-state sampling**: Every 15-30 minutes during run -- full QC panel
6. **End-of-run sampling**: Final units, composite retention samples (store at ambient + accelerated conditions)
7. **Post-trial review**: Yield analysis, quality data review, corrective actions, go/no-go for commercial production

### Gate 4 Review Criteria

| Criterion | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Production trial success | All quality parameters within spec; yield >= 90% |
| Shelf life | Real-time data supports labeled shelf life at >= 6 months actual data |
| Retail commitments | Written (PO or authorization) from >= 3 key accounts or >= 500 doors |
| Sales forecast | Bottom-up forecast by account; inventory plan aligned with production schedule |
| Marketing plan | Activation plan funded and scheduled (sampling, digital, in-store) |
| Logistics ready | 3PL, freight, EDI operational |

### Kill Criteria
- Production trial failure on 2+ attempts (quality, yield, or process issues not resolvable)
- Shelf life failure at real-time checkpoints
- Insufficient retail interest to support minimum production runs
- Competitive landscape shift making launch economics unviable

## Stage 5: Launch & Post-Launch

**Objective:** Execute the commercial launch and monitor performance against plan.

### Activities

| Activity | Detail |
|---|---|
| Production ramp | First 2-3 commercial production runs; build launch inventory (typically 8-12 weeks of supply) |
| Distribution build | Ship to DCs, authorize at retail, confirm shelf sets, verify pricing and UPC scanning |
| Marketing activation | Sampling programs, digital media (social, programmatic, influencer), in-store POS, demo crews |
| Velocity monitoring | Weekly SPINS/IRI/Circana reads after ACV builds to measurable level (typically 8-12 weeks post-launch) |
| Post-launch reviews | Structured reviews at 90, 180, and 365 days |

### Post-Launch Review Framework

| Timeframe | Focus |
|---|---|
| 90 days | Distribution vs. plan, initial velocity reads, fill rates, quality complaints, retailer feedback, marketing performance metrics |
| 180 days | Velocity trends (growing/stable/declining), repeat purchase rates, trade spend efficiency, margin actuals vs. pro forma, competitive response |
| 365 days | Full P&L reconciliation, brand health metrics, consumer awareness/trial/repeat, category share, line extension or reformulation decisions |

### Velocity Benchmarks (Natural/Specialty Channel)

| Category | Good | Great |
|---|---|---|
| RTD tea/kombucha | $4-6/SKU/store/week | $8+/SKU/store/week |
| Functional beverage | $3-5/SKU/store/week | $7+/SKU/store/week |
| Juice/smoothie | $5-8/SKU/store/week | $10+/SKU/store/week |
| Sparkling water | $3-5/SKU/store/week | $6+/SKU/store/week |

## Common NPD Failure Points (Ranked by Frequency)

| Rank | Failure Point | Stage | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Insufficient consumer validation before development investment | 1-2 | $50-200K wasted |
| 2 | Shelf life failure requiring reformulation | 3-4 | 3-6 month delay, $30-100K |
| 3 | Production trial failure (process doesn't scale) | 4 | $25-75K per failed trial |
| 4 | Labeling/claims error discovered post-print | 4-5 | $50-200K reprint + delay |
| 5 | Missed category review window | 4 | 6-12 month delay (annual resets) |
| 6 | Underestimating trade spend requirements | 5 | Margin erosion, cash flow stress |
| 7 | Co-man capacity conflict (bumped for larger customer) | 4-5 | 2-4 month delay |
| 8 | Ingredient supply disruption | 3-5 | Variable; can kill product |
| 9 | Over-SKU'ing at launch (too many flavors) | 5 | Diluted velocity, retailer discontinuation |
| 10 | No post-launch course correction | 5 | Slow death over 12-18 months |

## Key Principles

1. **Front-load the learning.** Spend more time in Stages 0-2 (cheap stages) to reduce rework in Stages 3-5 (expensive stages).
2. **Kill early, kill often.** A healthy innovation pipeline kills more projects than it launches. The ratio should be roughly 5:1 concepts entering Stage 1 to products launching.
3. **Cross-functional gates, not functional handoffs.** Every gate decision involves R&D, marketing, sales, operations, finance, and regulatory. No single function owns the decision.
4. **Time-box, don't open-end.** Each stage has a maximum duration. If the team cannot produce gate deliverables within the time box, the project is paused or killed.
5. **Separate the "should we?" from the "can we?"** Stage 1-2 answer "should we?" (market, consumer, financial). Stage 3-4 answer "can we?" (technical, operational). Both must be yes.
