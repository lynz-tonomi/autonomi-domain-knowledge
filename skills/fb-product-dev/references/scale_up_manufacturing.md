# Beverage Scale-Up: Bench to Commercial Manufacturing

## Overview

Scale-up is the process of translating a bench-top formula into a commercially manufacturable product. It is the most common source of NPD delays in beverage -- what works perfectly in a 1-liter beaker frequently fails at 500-gallon batch scale. Mixing dynamics, heat transfer, ingredient behavior, and equipment geometry all change with scale, and the food scientist must anticipate and manage these differences.

---

## Scale-Up Phases

| Phase | Volume | Purpose | Typical Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Bench-top | 0.5-5 L | Formula development, initial stability, sensory screening | $500-$2,000 per iteration |
| Pilot plant | 5-50 gal (20-200 L) | Process parameter development, small-batch sensory, initial shelf life | $3,000-$15,000 per trial |
| Production trial (minimum run) | 500-5,000 gal (co-man minimum) | Validate commercial process, generate production samples for shelf life and consumer testing | $15,000-$75,000 per trial |
| Commercial production | 2,000-20,000+ gal per run | Ongoing manufacturing | Variable (tolling fee per unit) |

## Scale-Up Challenges

### Mixing Dynamics

| Factor | Bench | Commercial | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Vessel geometry | Cylindrical beaker, magnetic stir bar | Jacketed tank, top-entry agitator with baffles | Dead zones, vortexing, air incorporation |
| Reynolds number | Low (laminar flow) | High (turbulent flow) | Different dissolution and dispersion behavior |
| Mixing time | Seconds to minutes | Minutes to tens of minutes | Ingredient degradation if shear-sensitive; insufficient blending if underpowered |
| Tip speed | Low | Must be controlled to avoid foaming or shear damage | Excessive tip speed destroys hydrocolloid structure, denatures proteins |
| Powder incorporation | Easy manual addition | Requires eductor, powder induction system, or pre-slurry | Clumping, fisheyes, undissolved solids |

**Key principle:** Scale by constant tip speed or constant power per unit volume, not by constant RPM. RPM-based scaling produces dramatically different mixing intensity at larger vessel diameters.

### Heat Transfer

| Factor | Bench | Commercial | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heating rate | Rapid (small thermal mass) | Slow (large thermal mass) | Flavor compounds degrade with extended heat exposure (Maillard browning, vitamin loss) |
| Temperature uniformity | Near-perfect | Gradients exist (especially near jacket walls) | Hot spots causing localized scorching or caramelization |
| Cooling rate | Rapid (ice bath) | Limited by jacket capacity and cooling water temperature | Extended time in "danger zone" if cooling is slow |
| CIP interaction | Not applicable | Previous product residue, CIP chemical residue | Off-flavors, contamination, allergen cross-contact |

**Key principle:** Calculate and compare F0 (lethality) and C0 (cook value) between bench and commercial processes. Matching the time-temperature integral is more important than matching the peak temperature.

### Ingredient Behavior at Scale

| Ingredient Category | Common Scale-Up Issue | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| Hydrocolloids (gum, pectin, CMC) | Incomplete hydration, fisheyes, viscosity overshoot | Pre-hydrate in water, use high-shear mixer, adjust addition temperature |
| Proteins (whey, pea, collagen) | Foaming, gelation, precipitation at pH extremes | Control addition temperature, add after pH adjustment, use defoamer |
| Sweeteners (stevia, monk fruit, allulose) | Crystallization, flavor perception shift at dilution | Verify solubility at batch concentration, confirm sensory at production Brix |
| Colors (natural: turmeric, beet, anthocyanin) | Degradation under heat/light/pH, inconsistent hue | Protect from light/O2, add post-pasteurization if possible, specify color input CoA |
| Flavors (natural, WONF) | Volatilization during hot process, scalping into packaging | Add post-pasteurization, verify flavor retention through process, test packaging compatibility |
| Acids (citric, malic, phosphoric) | pH overshoot, localized protein precipitation | Add slowly with agitation, verify final pH at temperature (pH is temperature-dependent) |
| Carbonation (CO2) | Inconsistent volumes, foaming at filler | Maintain cold product temperature (<4C), deaerate before carbonation, calibrate carbonator |
| Vitamins/minerals (fortification) | Degradation (especially C, B1, B12), off-flavors (iron, zinc) | Overformulate to account for process loss (15-30%), add late in process, use chelated mineral forms |

### Foaming Issues

Foaming is the #1 nuisance problem in beverage scale-up. Sources include:

- Protein-containing formulas (whey, soy, pea)
- High-shear mixing of surfactant-containing systems
- Air entrainment during transfer and filling
- Carbonation in the presence of foam-stabilizing ingredients

**Mitigations:**
- Defoaming agents (silicone-based, typically 5-20 ppm; must be declared on label)
- Vacuum deaeration before filling
- Low-turbulence transfer (minimize drops, elbows, splashing)
- Adjusting fill temperature and fill valve type

## Trial Run Protocols

### Pre-Production Meeting (2-4 Weeks Before Trial)

| Topic | Detail |
|---|---|
| Formula review | Final formula card with exact ingredient specs, lot codes, CoAs |
| Process parameters | Target temperatures, hold times, Brix, pH, carbonation volumes, fill weight/volume |
| Packaging specs | Can/bottle/closure specs, label placement, case configuration |
| Sampling plan | What to sample, when, how many, who performs analysis |
| Acceptance criteria | Spec ranges for every measured parameter (see Quality Parameters below) |
| Retention samples | Quantity (typically 24-48 units), storage conditions, labeling protocol |
| Roles and responsibilities | Who from brand team attends? Who from co-man leads? Decision authority? |
| Contingency plan | What if pH is out of spec? What if fill volume is low? Pre-agree on decision tree. |

### Day-of-Trial Execution

**Phase 1: Startup (first 15-30 minutes)**
1. Verify CIP completion records
2. First-article inspection: packaging components, label placement, code date
3. Start-up product divert to waste until steady-state parameters achieved
4. First production sample pull: Brix, pH, temperature, fill volume, visual inspection
5. If out-of-spec: HOLD line, troubleshoot, adjust, re-sample before proceeding

**Phase 2: Steady-State Production**
1. Sample every 15-30 minutes: Brix, pH, fill volume/weight, cap torque or seam integrity
2. Sample every 60 minutes: dissolved O2, color (if applicable), carbonation volume
3. Continuous monitoring: line speed, reject rate, filler downtime
4. Sensory check: brand team member evaluates product every 30-60 minutes for organoleptic defects

**Phase 3: End of Run**
1. Final samples pulled from last units produced
2. Composite retention samples assembled: early, middle, late run
3. Yield calculation: total gallons batched vs. total saleable units produced
4. Waste accounting: startup waste, end-of-run waste, rejected units, rework
5. Post-trial debrief with co-man team: what worked, what didn't, corrective actions

## Quality Parameters to Monitor

### In-Process Parameters

| Parameter | Method | Typical Spec (Example: Flavored Sparkling Water) | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Brix (soluble solids) | Digital refractometer | 0.0-1.0 (flavored water) or 10.0-12.0 (juice) +/- 0.3 | Every 15 min |
| pH | pH meter (temperature-compensated) | 3.0-3.5 +/- 0.1 | Every 15 min |
| Titratable acidity (TA) | Titration to pH 8.2 | Spec depends on formula | Every 60 min |
| Temperature (fill) | Thermocouple / IR | Hot fill: >= 185F (85C); Cold fill: <= 40F (4C) | Continuous |
| Fill volume/weight | Graduated cylinder / scale | 12.0 fl oz +/- 0.2 fl oz (or by weight) | Every 15 min |
| Dissolved oxygen | DO meter | < 1 ppm (for O2-sensitive products) or < 50 ppb (aseptic) | Every 60 min |
| Carbonation (CO2 volumes) | Zahm-Nagel or in-line CO2 meter | 2.5-3.5 volumes +/- 0.3 | Every 30 min |
| Cap torque (application/removal) | Torque meter | Application: 12-18 in-lbs; Removal: 8-16 in-lbs | Every 15 min |
| Can seam integrity | Seam teardown (double seam dimensions) | Per co-man seam spec and can supplier spec | Every 30 min (first head) |
| Headspace | Measurement gauge | Per spec (critical for hot fill inversion) | Every 30 min |
| Color | Spectrophotometer or visual match | Delta E < 2.0 from standard | Every 60 min |
| Code date / lot code | Visual inspection | Legible, correct date, correct format | Every 15 min |

### Finished Product Release Testing

| Test | Method | Turnaround |
|---|---|---|
| Microbiological (APC, yeast/mold, coliform) | Plating / Petrifilm | 3-5 days |
| Nutritional analysis (for Nutrition Facts verification) | Accredited lab (Eurofins, Merieux, Covance) | 5-10 business days |
| Heavy metals (if applicable -- juice, botanical extracts) | ICP-MS | 5-7 business days |
| Shelf life pull-point analysis | Micro + chemistry + sensory at each time point | Ongoing over shelf life |
| Packaging integrity (burst test, vacuum, seal strength) | Mechanical testing | 1-2 days |

## Process Authority Requirements

### Acidified Beverages (21 CFR 114)

An acidified beverage is a low-acid food (pH > 4.6 in nature) that has been acidified to pH <= 4.6. Most flavored waters, many teas, and some functional beverages fall into this category.

**Requirements:**
1. **Process authority:** A competent person (typically with a Better Process Control School certificate and advanced food science degree) must establish the scheduled process
2. **Scheduled process:** Documented thermal process (time-temperature) and critical factors (pH, Brix, preservatives, fill temperature) that achieve commercial sterility or adequate shelf stability
3. **FDA Form 2541:** File the Food Canning Establishment Registration (Form FDA 2541) before initial production; file the Scheduled Process (Form FDA 2541a, 2541c, or 2541g depending on process type)
4. **pH monitoring:** Every batch must be pH-tested and documented; equilibrium pH must be <= 4.6
5. **Critical factor monitoring:** All critical factors identified in the scheduled process must be monitored and recorded during production
6. **Deviation procedures:** Documented procedures for what to do when critical factors are out of range (hold, evaluate, rework, or destroy)
7. **Operator training:** All personnel involved in acidification and thermal processing must complete Better Process Control School (BPCS)

### Low-Acid Beverages (21 CFR 113)

Low-acid canned foods (LACF) have pH > 4.6 and water activity > 0.85. Most beverages are acidified below 4.6, but some (e.g., certain plant milks, protein shakes) may be LACF requiring retort or aseptic processing.

**Additional requirements beyond acidified:**
- Full thermal process validation (heat penetration studies)
- Container integrity testing (incubation studies)
- More stringent FDA Form 2541 filing requirements
- Typically requires aseptic processing or retort -- significantly more complex and expensive

## Equipment Considerations

### Batch vs. Continuous Mixing

| Attribute | Batch Mixing | Continuous (In-Line) Blending |
|---|---|---|
| Flexibility | High -- easy to adjust | Low -- requires precise metering |
| Consistency | Good within batch; batch-to-batch variation possible | Excellent uniformity when calibrated |
| Volume | Suited for <10,000 gal runs | Suited for >10,000 gal runs |
| Capital cost | Lower | Higher |
| Typical co-man setup | Most small-mid co-mans | Large co-mans and captive plants |
| Best for | Low-volume, high-complexity formulas | High-volume, simple formulas (flavored water, CSDs) |

### CIP (Clean-in-Place) Systems

- CIP is critical for allergen management, flavor carry-over prevention, and food safety
- Standard CIP cycle: pre-rinse (water) -> caustic wash (NaOH 1-3%, 70-80C) -> intermediate rinse -> acid wash (phosphoric/nitric, 0.5-1.5%) -> final rinse -> sanitize (peracetic acid or hot water)
- Changeover validation: ATP swab testing, allergen swab testing (if applicable), sensory rinse water evaluation
- CIP failures are a common source of off-flavor complaints ("tastes like soap," "chemical taste," "tastes like the last product")

## Yield and Efficiency

### Yield Calculation

```
Yield (%) = (Saleable Units Produced x Fill Volume) / (Total Batch Volume) x 100
```

**Typical yield targets:**
- First production trial: 85-92% (learning curve, startup/shutdown waste)
- Second production trial: 90-95%
- Steady-state commercial: 93-97%

**Waste sources:**
- Startup divert (first 50-200 units until steady state): 1-3%
- End-of-run (line drains, tank heels): 1-3%
- Rejected units (fill, seam, label defects): 1-5%
- Sampling (QC pulls, retention): 0.5-1%
- Transfer losses (piping, hoses, tanks): 0.5-2%

### OEE (Overall Equipment Effectiveness)

```
OEE = Availability x Performance x Quality

Availability = (Planned Production Time - Downtime) / Planned Production Time
Performance = (Actual Output / Maximum Possible Output)
Quality = (Good Units / Total Units Produced)
```

| OEE Level | Benchmark | Typical For |
|---|---|---|
| World class | >= 85% | Large captive beverage plants (Coca-Cola, PepsiCo) |
| Good | 70-84% | Well-run co-manufacturers |
| Acceptable | 60-69% | Small co-mans, complex products |
| Needs improvement | < 60% | Frequent changeovers, old equipment, training gaps |

## Scale-Up Decision Checklist

Before approving a production trial, verify:

- [ ] Formula card finalized and signed off by R&D and QA
- [ ] All ingredients sourced, CoAs reviewed, stored at co-man
- [ ] Packaging components on-site, first-article inspection complete
- [ ] Process authority engaged (acidified/LACF products)
- [ ] Scheduled process filed or filing in progress
- [ ] Trial protocol reviewed and agreed by co-man and brand team
- [ ] Sampling plan and acceptance criteria documented
- [ ] Retention sample plan defined (quantity, storage, labeling)
- [ ] Post-trial shelf life study plan in place (pull points, lab identified)
- [ ] Contingency plan for out-of-spec conditions documented
- [ ] Brand team travel and attendance confirmed
- [ ] Production insurance / liability coverage verified
