## FDA Ingredient & Product Requirements

### GRAS (Generally Recognized as Safe)

**Regulatory Foundation:** 21 CFR 170.30, 170.35; FD&C Act Section 201(s)

GRAS status is the gold standard for ingredient approvals in the US food market. An ingredient achieves GRAS status through one of two pathways:

**Self-Affirmed GRAS (21 CFR 170.30(h))**
- Manufacturer conducts internal GRAS determination without FDA involvement
- Requires: (1) adequate published scientific evidence demonstrating safety, (2) expert panel review of that evidence, (3) qualified experts reaching consensus that evidence supports safe use
- Expert panel: typically 3-5 food safety scientists with relevant expertise; must be independent (no financial conflict of interest with company)
- Timeline: panel assembly and review typically 3-6 months
- **When to use:** ingredient has long established history of safe use in food, robust published safety literature exists, potential for FDA scrutiny is low
- Documentation: company retains GRAS determination on file; no notification requirement but must be prepared to defend if FDA challenges

**FDA-Notified GRAS (21 CFR 170.30(h), 170.35)**
- Manufacturer submits GRAS determination to FDA for acknowledgment
- Submission includes: data and literature summary, identification of expert panel, opinion of expert panel, statement of basis for GRAS conclusion
- FDA review period: FDA has 180 days to respond; in practice, FDA often extends through additional information requests (ADR), extending timeline to 6-12 months
- FDA response options: acknowledgment (no regulatory issue), objection letter (FDA disputes GRAS status), or no closure letter issued (ambiguous; interpreted as withdrawal/advisory)
- **When to use:** novel ingredient with limited use history, ingredient chemistry differs from prior-approved analogs, anticipated FDA interest due to intended use or consumer population
- Advantage: creates formal record of FDA acknowledgment; provides stronger legal position if future challenge emerges

**Critical Distinction:** The presence of an ingredient in "natural" form or botanical source does NOT confer automatic GRAS status. Turmeric root is "natural"; curcumin isolate from turmeric may require separate GRAS determination depending on processing and safety data. This distinction is source of frequent regulatory missteps.

### Food Additive Petition (21 CFR 171; FD&C Act Section 409)

**Regulatory Requirement:** Required when ingredient is neither GRAS-recognized nor prior-sanctioned (21 CFR 181-186)

- Submission to FDA includes: proposed regulation, data supporting safety (toxicology studies, metabolism studies, intended use conditions, analytical methods)
- FDA review is mandatory; no "acknowledgment" pathway exists
- Typical approval timeline: 12-24 months minimum; often extends to 3+ years if FDA requests additional studies
- Toxicology requirements: typical petition includes 90-day feeding studies in rodents, 1-year chronic studies, genotoxicity assessment, reproductive/developmental toxicity studies
- Cost: regulatory consultants, expert panel, toxicology studies typically total $200,000-$500,000+
- **Strategic implication:** if food additive pathway is identified late in development (post Gate 2), project timeline typically extends 18-24 months. This is project-critical and must be surfaced at Gate 1.
- Prior-sanctioned substances (grandfathered additives approved before 1958): listed in 21 CFR 181-186; common examples include certain artificial colors, BHA, BHT, calcium silicate

### New Dietary Ingredient (NDI) Notification — Dietary Supplements Only (21 CFR 190.6)

**Regulatory Foundation:** Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA), 1994; 21 CFR 190.6

- Applies ONLY to dietary supplement products, not conventional foods
- Required for any dietary ingredient not marketed in the US prior to October 15, 1994
- Definition of "dietary ingredient": vitamin, mineral, amino acid, herb, other botanical, or concentrate/metabolite/constituent of these (21 CFR 190.1)
- Submission timeline: must submit to FDA at least 75 days before product marketing begins
- Submission contents: information related to reasonably expected use, statement of basis for reasonable expectation of safety, evidence from research, and identification of prior use in the US or identity of similar substances
- "Reasonable expectation of safety": lower bar than GRAS or food additive approval; generally requires evidence (published literature, use history) that ingredient is safe under conditions of use
- FDA review: 75-day holding period is mandatory; FDA may issue an objection letter or no objection letter. No objection does NOT equal affirmative approval; it means FDA has no basis to object at that time.
- Timeline including preparation: 4-8 months total (includes dossier assembly, literature compilation, expert review)
- Note: DSHEA does not apply to conventional food products; supplement product classification is critical regulatory determination

## Nutrition Labeling

### FDA Nutrition Facts Panel (21 CFR 101.9; FDA Final Rule December 2016, effective January 1, 2020 for companies >$10M revenue, January 1, 2021 for smaller companies)

**Format & Content Requirements**

- Updated format (as of 2020): larger, bolder "Calories" declaration (minimum 1.5x font size of other nutrients), separate line for "Added Sugars" with %DV, updated Reference Daily Values (RDVs) based on 2016 Dietary Guidelines
- Mandatory nutrients: Calories, Total Fat, Saturated Fat, Trans Fat, Cholesterol, Sodium, Total Carbohydrate, Dietary Fiber, Total Sugars (including Added Sugars as sub-category), Protein, Vitamin D, Calcium, Iron, Potassium (now mandatory; was voluntary pre-2020)
- Dual-column format required if product has multiple serving sizes on package (e.g., "per serving" and "per container")
- Rounding rules (21 CFR 101.9(c)): Calories ≤50 round to nearest 5 (if <5 cal declared as 0), >50 round to nearest 10; Total Fat, Saturated Fat, Cholesterol round to nearest 0.5g (if <0.5g declared as 0); Sodium round to nearest 5mg; Sugars round to nearest 1g; Protein round to nearest 1g
- Zero declaration rules: nutrient at or below rounding threshold can be declared as "0" or rounded value (e.g., 0.4g fat can be declared as "0g Fat")

**Serving Size Determination (21 CFR 101.12)**

- Serving size must reflect Reference Amounts Customarily Consumed (RACC) per serving occasion
- RACCs published in FDA regulations by product category (21 CFR 101.12(b)); examples: 55g for bread, 240mL for beverage, 28g for nuts
- If package contains less than RACC, entire package constitutes one serving
- If package contains 200% or more of RACC, may have option to declare "per serving" or "per container"
- Serving size determination is often source of dispute; FDA issued guidance (FDA Guidance on Serving Size Determinations, May 2016) addressing consumer consumption data

**Nutrient Analysis Methods**

- **Laboratory analysis** (most accurate): samples tested by FDA-recognized method (typically AOAC Official Methods or other FDA-recognized compendial methods). Cost: $500-$1,500 per SKU for full panel. Turnaround: 2-4 weeks. Required for label claims (cannot use database/recipe calculation if making claim).
- **Database calculation**: use USDA FoodData Central or ingredient manufacturer nutrient data; acceptable for statements of identity but not recommended if making claims
- **Recipe calculation**: manual calculation based on ingredient nutrient content; least accurate, generally acceptable only for products with simple, unchanged formulations
- Regulatory requirement: nutrient values declared on label must reflect actual product composition within allowable label tolerance (see below)

**Label Tolerances (21 CFR 101.9(c), FDA Compliance Guidance)**

- For naturally occurring nutrients: actual nutrient level must be ≥80% of declared value and ≤120% of declared value
- For calories, total fat, saturated fat, sodium, cholesterol, sugars: stricter tolerance applies, depending on nutrient
- Practical implication: if lab analysis shows 8.2g fat but label declares "8g Fat," this passes tolerance (8.2 is >80% of 8 and <120% of 8). If lab shows 6.4g, this FAILS tolerance (<80% of 8).
- Tolerance testing: best practice is to test finished product annually or with any formulation change

### Ingredient Declaration (21 CFR 101.4, 101.22)

**Descending Order of Predominance**

- Ingredients listed by weight in descending order at time of manufacture
- Water added as ingredient (not evaporated off) must be listed in order of predominance, even if water evaporates later during storage or preparation
- For spice, natural flavor, artificial flavor, color additives: special declaration rules apply (see below)

**Sub-Ingredient Declaration**

- Sub-ingredients (ingredients within ingredients) must be declared in parentheses following the parent ingredient
- Example: "Whole Wheat Flour (Whole Wheat, Vitamin E)" or "Natural Vanilla Flavor (Vanilla Extract, Gum Arabic)"
- Exception: sub-ingredients that are GRAS for their technical function (e.g., lactic acid as preservative in bacterial culture) may not require parenthetical listing per FDA guidance, though best practice is to include

**Standardized Common Names (FDA 2016 Guidance, "Use of 'Evaporated Cane Juice' and Similar Misleading Terms")**

- Ingredients must use standard common names, not marketing-friendly alternatives
- "Evaporated cane juice" must be declared as "sugar"; "coconut palm sugar" as "sugar"; "cane crystals" as "sugar"
- This 2016 guidance effectively eliminated marketing-motivated ingredient renames
- Herbal or botanical ingredients: use common name (e.g., "turmeric" not "golden root extract")
- Color additives must be declared by specific name (21 CFR 101.22(k)): "Red No. 40" not "artificial color"; "Beta-Carotene" (if GRAS) or "Yellow No. 5" if synthetic

**Spice, Flavor, Color Blanket Declarations**

- "Spice" can be declared generically if ingredient is a single spice or spice blend with no other functional ingredients
- "Natural Flavor" and "Artificial Flavor" can be declared as blanket terms without identifying specific flavor source, unless flavor components trigger separate labeling (e.g., if natural flavor contains an allergen)
- Colorants: each color additive must be listed by name; colorants from plant sources (e.g., "Beta-Carotene," "Annatto Extract") declared by specific name

## Allergen Labeling

### FALCPA (Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act of 2004) + FASTER Act (2021)

**Regulatory Foundation:** 21 CFR 101.22(d)-(j); FD&C Act Section 201(qq)

**Major Allergens Covered (9 as of January 1, 2023)**

- Milk, eggs, fish, crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, sesame
- Sesame added January 1, 2023 per FASTER Act (Food Allergy Safety, Treatment, Education, and Research Act of 2021)
- Note: "Tree nuts" and "fish" must be declared with SPECIFIC type: "almonds" not just "tree nuts," "salmon" not just "fish"

**Declaration Options**

- **Parenthetical declaration in ingredient list:** allergen name in parentheses following ingredient. Example: "Milk Protein Isolate (Milk), Wheat Starch (Wheat), Sesame Seeds (Sesame)"
- **Separate "Contains:" statement:** after ingredient list, statement "Contains: Milk, Wheat, Sesame" using common names of major allergens
- If using "Contains:" statement, ALL major allergens present must be listed there; cannot selectively list only some allergens
- Either method acceptable; practical choice depends on label design and allergen complexity

**Cross-Contact ("May Contain")**

- Voluntary labeling; not required by FDA but if used, must be truthful and not misleading
- "May contain" statements are frequently used but should be based on quantitative risk assessment, not blanket disclaimers
- Best practice: use VITAL (Voluntary Incidental Trace Allergen Labelling) framework to assess risk and justify precautionary labeling
- VITAL framework (published by ICPNA and adopted by FDA): categorizes allergen risk by processing step (shared equipment, production line, facility, etc.) and recommends whether precautionary labeling is necessary
- FDA enforcement: if "may contain" statement is not truthful (e.g., no genuine cross-contact risk exists), FDA may challenge as misleading

**Tree Nuts & Fish Specificity**

- Tree nuts: must specify type (e.g., "almonds," "cashews," "walnuts"). "Tree nuts" alone is insufficient.
- Fish: must specify species (e.g., "salmon," "cod," "tuna"). "Fish" alone is insufficient.
- Crustacean shellfish: may specify type (e.g., "shrimp," "crab") but "crustacean shellfish" is acceptable
- Rationale: different tree nuts and fish species have different allergen proteins; cross-reactivity is not universal

## Claims Substantiation

### Nutrient Content Claims (21 CFR 101.13, 101.54-101.67)

**Defined Terms Framework**

FDA has established specific regulatory definitions for common nutrient claims. Use of these terms triggers compliance with specific numerical criteria:

- **"Free"** (e.g., "sugar-free," "fat-free"): nutrient present in negligible amounts. Calorie-free = <5 cal per RACC, Fat-free = <0.5g per RACC, Sugar-free = <0.5g per RACC
- **"Low"** (e.g., "low sodium," "low fat"): Low Sodium = ≤140mg per RACC, Low Fat = ≤3g per RACC, Low Cholesterol = ≤20mg per RACC, Low Saturated Fat = ≤1g per RACC
- **"Reduced"** (e.g., "reduced sugar"): at least 25% less nutrient compared to reference food; must declare reference food on label. Example: "Reduced Sugar (50% less sugar than original formula)"
- **"Light/Lite"** (e.g., "light in sodium"): at least 50% reduction in calories or fat compared to reference food; or sodium reduced by 50%; must identify reference food
- **"High"** (e.g., "high in fiber"): at least 20% or more of Daily Value per RACC
- **"Good Source"**: 10-19% of Daily Value per RACC
- **"More"**: 10% or more additional Daily Value compared to reference food; must identify reference food
- **"Lean"** (meat products): <10g fat, <4.5g saturated fat, <95mg cholesterol per 100g
- **"Extra Lean"** (meat products): <5g fat, <2g saturated fat, <95mg cholesterol per 100g

**Claim Substantiation Requirements**

- Nutrient content claims require analytical data demonstrating product meets the criterion
- Lab analysis mandatory; database/recipe calculation insufficient for claims
- Tolerance testing required if claim is central to marketing (best practice: test annually or with formulation change)
- Documentation: retain analytical reports, test method validation, reference food comparison data

### Health Claims (21 CFR 101.72-101.83)

**Authorized Health Claims (FDA-Approved Diet-Disease Relationships)**

FDA has evaluated and authorized specific health claims based on significant scientific agreement. These claims require compliance with:

- **Specific language requirement:** claim must use exact language FDA approved or FDA-authorized variation
- **Disqualifying nutrient levels:** even if product qualifies for claim, disqualifying nutrient levels invalidate use. Example: cannot claim "low in sodium may reduce risk of high blood pressure" if product has >400mg sodium per RACC
- Examples of authorized claims:
  - "Diets low in sodium may reduce the risk of high blood pressure" (sodium-hypertension claim)
  - "Adequate calcium as part of a healthful diet may reduce the risk of osteoporosis" (calcium-osteoporosis claim)
  - "Adequate intake of potassium may reduce blood pressure" (potassium claim)
  - "Diets low in saturated fat and cholesterol and rich in fruits, vegetables, and grain products may reduce the risk of heart disease" (fruit/vegetable/grain claim)

- Authorized claims list maintained by FDA at 21 CFR 101.72-101.83 and on FDA website; complete listing of 12 authorized health claims

**Qualified Health Claims**

- Lower evidentiary standard than authorized claims; based on "credible scientific basis" rather than "significant scientific agreement"
- Require FDA petition (IND pathway) or notification before marketing
- Must include qualifying disclaimer language: "This claim is not FDA-approved" or "Based on limited scientific evidence"
- FDA has recognized qualified claims for: walnuts and heart disease, calcium and cancer risk, omega-3 fatty acids and cardiovascular disease, and others
- Timeline: 6-12 months for FDA review if submitted via petition; notification pathway often faster (60 days)

**Health Claims NOT Allowed**

- Any drug claims (e.g., "cures," "treats," "prevents disease") are prohibited for food; such language triggers FDA classification as unapproved new drug
- Structure/function claims must include required disclaimer (see below)
- Unqualified claims lacking significant scientific agreement (these may trigger warning letter)

### Structure/Function Claims (21 CFR 101.36, 101.93)

**Regulatory Framework**

- Describe role of nutrient or dietary ingredient in maintaining normal structure or function of body
- Do NOT require FDA pre-approval (unlike health claims)
- Examples: "Calcium builds strong bones," "Vitamin C supports immune function," "Fiber promotes healthy digestion"
- **CRITICAL:** Structure/function claims trigger MANDATORY DISCLAIMER requirement (21 CFR 101.36(b)(2)): "These statements have not been evaluated by the FDA. This product is not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any disease."
- Disclaimer font size must be at least 1/16 inch (approximately 7-8 points)
- Manufacturer must submit Structure/Function Notification to FDA within 30 days of product marketing (21 CFR 101.93)
- FDA notification includes: claim, product identity, ingredient list, brief literature summary supporting claim

**Common Structure/Function vs. Health Claim Distinction Pitfalls**

- "Supports heart health" = structure/function claim (allowed, needs disclaimer, needs notification)
- "Reduces risk of heart disease" = health claim (must be authorized, requires significant scientific agreement)
- "Promotes bone strength" = structure/function claim
- "Reduces osteoporosis risk" = health claim
- Line between these is contentious; when in doubt, use disclaimer + notification to be safe

### "Natural" Claims (FDA Guidance December 2015; FTC Authority)

**Regulatory Status**

- FDA has NO formal regulatory definition of "natural" for food
- December 2015 FDA Guidance (informal; not final rule) states: FDA will not object to use of term "natural" if nothing artificial or synthetic has been included in, or added to, a food that would not normally be expected to be in that food
- Guidance explicitly notes this is FDA's longstanding policy, not a new rule
- FDA requested comments on potential regulation of "natural" claims in 2018; as of 2026, no final rule has been issued
- **FTC Authority:** FTC regulates advertising claims and has brought enforcement actions against companies making deceptive "natural" claims (e.g., products with synthetic ingredients, artificial colors, or pesticide residues)

**Practical Substantiation Requirements**

- If claiming "natural," substantiation file should document:
  - Complete ingredient list with supplier affidavits confirming no artificial additives
  - Processing methods (minimal processing, no synthetic methods)
  - Any pesticide residue testing (if relevant)
  - Absence of artificial colors, flavors, preservatives
- "Natural" is among most litigated F&B claims; class action litigation common
- Recommendation: document everything. A well-maintained substantiation file minimizes litigation risk.

### "Organic" Claims (USDA National Organic Program — 7 CFR 205)

**Regulatory Framework**

- USDA (not FDA) regulates organic claims under National Organic Program (NOP)
- Mandatory certification required; self-declaration insufficient
- Four label categories:
  - **"100% Organic":** all ingredients (excluding water and salt) must be certified organic; can use USDA Organic seal
  - **"Organic":** ≥95% organic ingredients by weight (excluding water and salt); can use USDA Organic seal
  - **"Made with Organic [ingredient]":** 70-94% organic ingredients; cannot use USDA Organic seal but can list up to 3 organic ingredients on front panel
  - **<70% Organic:** cannot claim "organic" on front label; can list organic ingredients in ingredient statement only
- Multi-ingredient products: percentage calculated by weight of organic ingredients

**Certification Requirements (7 CFR 205.1-205.20)**

- USDA-accredited certifying agent must review and approve:
  - Organic System Plan (OSP): detailed description of production system, inputs, handling procedures
  - Supplier documentation: organic certificates for purchased organic ingredients
  - Annual inspection: unannounced facility inspection
  - Ongoing compliance: record-keeping requirements
- Initial certification timeline: 3-6 months from complete application submission to issuance of certificate
- **Critical for development timeline:** organic certification must be completed BEFORE marketing begins; cannot claim "organic" while certification pending
- Annual re-certification required; costs typically $500-$2,000/year depending on certifying agent and operation complexity

**Common Pitfalls in Product Development**

- Starting organic certification process too late (in Phase 3 instead of Phase 1)
- Failing to verify organic status of purchased ingredients and suppliers
- Using non-certified ingredient in product claiming organic percentage
- Insufficient supplier documentation for multi-ingredient products

### "Non-GMO" Claims

**Non-GMO Project Verification (Most Recognized US Standard)**

- Non-GMO Project is third-party verification program; most trusted non-GMO claim in US market
- Enrollment process: submit product dossier including ingredient sourcing, supply chain documentation, test protocols
- Enrollment to verification: typically 3-6 months
- Cost: enrollment + annual verification fees, typically $500-$3,000/year depending on product complexity
- High-risk crops requiring testing: corn, soy, canola, sugar beet, papaya, alfalfa, zucchini
- Low-risk crops (typically not tested): wheat, rice, oats, beans (unless sourced from regions where GMO cultivation occurs)
- Ongoing compliance: annual re-verification, documentation of ingredient GMO status, testing for high-risk ingredients

**FDA Position on Non-GMO Claims**

- FDA does not have formal definition of "non-GMO" for labeling
- FDA guidance: non-GMO claims must be truthful and not misleading (21 CFR 101.4)
- FDA has challenged some "non-GMO" claims as unsubstantiated if no testing or documentation provided
- Best practice: combine Non-GMO Project verification with supply chain documentation and testing of high-risk ingredients

**National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard (NBFDS, 7 CFR 66; Compliance Deadline January 1, 2022)**

- Federal requirement for disclosure of "bioengineered" ingredients (≥5% bioengineered content)
- Disclosure options: text label, symbol, QR code linking to disclosure website, or 1-800 number
- Applies to conventional foods derived from bioengineered crops (not to GRAS bioengineered ingredients)
- Interacts with "non-GMO" claims: non-GMO Project Verified products automatically comply with NBFDS

## State-Level Requirements

### California Prop 65 (Safe Drinking Water and Toxic Enforcement Act of 1986, Cal. Code Section 25249.5)

**Regulatory Overview**

- California maintains list of 900+ chemicals known to cause cancer or reproductive toxicity
- Products sold in California containing any listed chemical above "safe harbor" level must display warning label
- Applies to any product sold in California (includes most national brands, online sales to CA residents)
- Enforcement: combination of government agencies (CA OEHHA, Attorney General) and private citizens (qui tam actions)

**Food & Beverage Relevance**

- **Common F&B exposures:** lead, cadmium, acrylamide (in heated/baked foods), arsenic (in rice products, juices), methylmercury (in fish)
- **Packaging:** BPA from polycarbonate, phthalates from plastics
- **Natural contaminants:** lead in certain minerals and botanical supplements

**Warning Label Format (Updated 2018)**

- Label must include: California Prop 65 warning symbol (⚠), "WARNING" heading, specific chemical name(s), statement of exposure route ("WARNING: This product contains chemicals known to the State of California to cause cancer," or "birth defects or other reproductive harm" as applicable)
- Font size: minimum 6-point font; specific sizing requirements if part of other warning labels
- Placement: on product label or package insert where consumer will see before purchase

**Testing & Compliance**

- Analytical testing for heavy metals (ICP-MS method): cost typically $200-$500 per test per substance; turnaround 1-2 weeks
- For acrylamide in baked goods: may require HPLC testing, cost $300-$600 per sample
- "Safe harbor" levels published by CA OEHHA; if product tests below safe harbor, no warning required (though labeled should verify this)
- Best practice: screen formulation and packaging at Gate 1; budget for testing if known Prop 65 exposure

**Strategic Implication**

- Prop 65 warnings can negatively impact consumer perception; companies often reformulate to eliminate chemical exposure rather than apply warning label
- If California sales are strategic market, Prop 65 compliance screening should occur early in development

### Other State Requirements

**Vermont Act 120 (Repealed; Superseded by Federal NBFDS)**

- Previously required GMO labeling at state level
- Repealed January 1, 2022 upon implementation of federal National Bioengineered Food Disclosure Standard
- NBFDS now provides federal floor for bioengineered disclosure

**NBFDS Implementation (7 CFR 66; Compliance Deadline January 1, 2022)**

- Federal requirement for disclosure of "bioengineered" ingredients (derived from bioengineered crops, ≥5% bioengineered content)
- Applies nationally; preempts most state-level GMO labeling requirements
- Disclosure methods: text disclosure, symbol, QR code, or 1-800 number
- Products subject to NBFDS labeling must use "bioengineered" terminology (not "GMO")

**State Weights & Measures Requirements**

- Net quantity declarations vary by product category and state
- Federal baseline: FTC Fair Packaging and Labeling Act (FPLA, 15 U.S.C. § 1451) requires net quantity in both metric and English units
- Some states require additional disclosure (e.g., California Code of Regulations Title 4, Division 6)
- Best practice: verify net quantity declaration compliance with FPLA and all target state requirements at label design phase

**Other State Organic Programs (California, Oregon)**

- Some states have organic programs that exceed federal NOP requirements
- California Certified Organic Farmers (CCOF) and similar programs may require additional certification
- If targeting these states, verify whether additional state-level organic certification required beyond federal NOP

## International Regulatory Considerations

### European Union (EU)

**Novel Food Regulation (EU 2015/2283, updated by Regulation (EU) 2023/2571)**

- Any ingredient not significantly consumed in the EU before May 15, 1997 classified as "Novel Food"
- Novel Food requires authorization before marketing; self-affirmation NOT permitted
- Assessment pathway: food business operator submits dossier to national competent authority or directly to European Commission
- Timeline: 12-24 months typical (often longer if scientific questions arise)
- This regulatory pathway applies to many ingredients that are GRAS in the US (e.g., certain probiotics, botanical extracts)
- Cost: technical dossier preparation, toxicology consultation, often €30,000-€100,000+

**Nutrition & Health Claims (Regulation (EC) 1924/2006)**

- Nutrition labeling mandatory under Regulation (EU) No 1169/2011: must declare per 100g/100mL in addition to per serving (per serving optional)
- Format differs from US Nutrition Facts Panel; different DVs, different nutrient list
- Health claims: far more restrictive than US. EU maintains Register of Nutrition and Health Claims; only pre-authorized claims permitted
- Example: "High in Vitamin C" not permitted in EU unless it meets specific nutritional criterion AND appears on authorized register
- "Natural" claims: EU has stricter guidance; "natural" ingredient claims frequently challenged by European Food Safety Authority (EFSA)

**Allergen Labeling**

- EU Big 14 allergens (more expansive than US Big 9):
  - Cereals containing gluten (wheat, rye, barley, oats)
  - Crustaceans, molluscs, fish
  - Peanuts, tree nuts
  - Milk, eggs, soybeans
  - Celery, mustard, sesame, sulphites, lupin
- Mandatory listing in ingredient list or separate "Contains" statement
- Precautionary labeling ("may contain"): strongly regulated; vague "may contain" statements discouraged; must be justified by VITAL or similar risk assessment

**Clean Label Expectations**

- Consumer expectations in EU are higher than US for "clean" ingredient lists
- Artificial colors, certain preservatives (e.g., sodium benzoate, potassium sorbate) face consumer skepticism and regulatory proposals to phase out
- Natural flavor preference strong; artificial flavors increasingly restricted via private retailer policies

### Canada (Health Canada)

**Natural Health Products (NHPs) — Dietary Supplements & Functional Foods**

- Regulated under NHP Regulations (SOR/2007-154)
- Requires Natural Product Number (NPN) or Exemption Number (EN) for marketing
- Submission includes: ingredient list, dosage form, recommended use, safety and efficacy evidence, manufacturing information
- Timeline: NPN application review 6-12 months (significant government backlog); EN approval often faster
- Different from FDA dietary supplement pathway: more rigorous safety & efficacy evidence required
- Cost: consultant preparation of NHP application typically $5,000-$15,000+

**Nutrition Facts Table**

- Mandatory for most packaged foods; format distinct from US
- Requirements per Canadian Food and Labelling Regulations (C.R.C., c. 870)
- Bilingual English/French required
- Must declare per 100g serving (serving size not based on RACC but on "reasonable quantity")
- Different nutrient list than US (e.g., includes "Other Carbohydrate" category; trans fats mandatory)
- Different Daily Values than US

**Allergen Labeling**

- Priority allergens similar to US but includes additional: mustard, sulphites
- Specific listing requirement similar to FALCPA
- Precautionary labeling ("may contain"): increasingly scrutinized; Health Canada guidance recommends VITAL methodology

### Codex Alimentarius

**Role in International Trade**

- International food standards developed by FAO/WHO joint body
- Not directly enforceable but referenced in WTO trade dispute procedures
- Relevant Codex standards: General Standard for the Labelling of Prepackaged Foods (CXS 1-1985), Guidelines on Nutrition Labelling (CXG 2-1985)
- Codex provides baseline for many national regulations globally

**Codex Compliance for Export Strategy**

- If developing product for international export, Codex compliance provides foundational baseline
- However, individual markets often exceed Codex requirements (EU is restrictive; Canada is restrictive; China has different framework)
- Strategy: Codex compliance necessary but not sufficient; must layer in target market-specific requirements

## Regulatory Screening Decision Framework

Use this methodology at Gate 1 and Gate 2 to identify regulatory blockers and timeline impacts.

### Step 1: Ingredient Screen

For each ingredient (including sub-ingredients), confirm regulatory status:

- **Is ingredient GRAS?** If yes, self-affirmed or FDA-notified? If FDA-notified, do we have acknowledgment letter on file?
- **If not GRAS, is it an approved food additive?** Check 21 CFR 70-82 (color additives), 21 CFR 180-189 (direct/indirect food additives)
- **If dietary supplement product:** Is ingredient pre-DSHEA (marketed before October 15, 1994) or does it require NDI notification?
- **For novel ingredients:** Has Novel Food authorization been obtained (if EU sales contemplated)? Is ingredient on Health Canada NHP licensed list (if Canada sales contemplated)?

**Action:** Create ingredient compliance matrix with GRAS status, food additive approval status, and timeline for any required determinations.

### Step 2: Claims Screen

Map each marketing claim to regulatory pathway:

- **"Natural"** → Document ingredient sourcing, processing, absence of artificial additives; plan substantiation file
- **Nutrient content claims** (e.g., "Low Fat") → Identify criterion (21 CFR 101.54-101.67), plan lab analysis
- **Health claims** (e.g., "reduces risk of") → Identify if authorized (21 CFR 101.72-101.83) or qualified; if qualified, plan FDA notification or petition; if unauthorized, RED FLAG
- **Structure/function claims** (e.g., "supports bone health") → Plan disclaimer language, 30-day FDA notification filing
- **Organic claims** → Plan USDA organic certification timeline (3-6 months, must complete before marketing)
- **Non-GMO claims** → Plan Non-GMO Project verification (3-6 months) or supply chain documentation

**Action:** Create claims compliance matrix with each claim mapped to regulatory pathway, required evidence, timeline, and risk level (green/yellow/red).

### Step 3: Labeling Screen

Review label design for compliance:

- **Nutrition Facts Panel:** Can lab-verified panel be created? Any suspected out-of-tolerance nutrients (use calculation tools to estimate)?
- **Allergen declaration:** Are all major allergens properly identified? Any cross-contact risks requiring precautionary labeling?
- **Ingredient list:** Are all ingredients declared in descending order? Any ingredients using non-standard names that require correction?
- **Prop 65 (California):** Does formulation or packaging contain any Prop 65-listed chemicals? If yes, has testing been budgeted?

**Action:** Create labeling compliance checklist; flag any nutrients or ingredients requiring testing or reformulation.

### Step 4: Geographic Screen

If selling outside the US, check each target market:

- **EU:** Is each ingredient GRAS equivalent in EU, or does it require Novel Food authorization? Are any claims unauthorized under EU health claims register?
- **Canada:** Are any ingredients requiring NHP authorization? Is nutrition facts table format compliant?
- **Other markets:** Verify country-specific requirements (China has entirely different regulatory framework; Middle East may have Halal certification requirements; etc.)

**Action:** Create geographic compliance matrix by target market; identify any market-specific regulatory gaps.

### Step 5: Timeline Screen

For each regulatory requirement identified in steps 1-4, assign timeline estimate:

- GRAS self-affirmation: 3-6 months
- GRAS FDA notification: 6-12 months
- Food additive petition: 12-24 months minimum (often 3+ years)
- NDI notification: 4-8 months total
- Organic certification: 3-6 months
- Non-GMO Project verification: 3-6 months
- Novel Food (EU): 12-24 months
- NHP (Canada): 6-12 months
- Prop 65 testing: 2-4 weeks (testing only, not legal pathway)
- FDA structure/function notification: 30 days post-marketing (not blocking)
- Nutrition Facts Panel lab analysis: 2-4 weeks

**Action:** Create regulatory timeline chart. Any requirement exceeding 6 months is a critical gate issue that must be surfaced to project leadership.

### Output: Regulatory Risk Matrix

Create matrix mapping each requirement to risk level and timeline:

| Requirement | Status | Timeline | Risk Level | Gate Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Ingredient X GRAS determination | Not initiated | 6-12 months | RED | Gate 1 blocker; delay 6 months |
| "Low Sodium" claim | Pending lab analysis | 2 weeks | GREEN | Minor; complete before Phase 3 |
| Organic certification | Not initiated | 3-6 months | YELLOW | Must initiate at Gate 1 approval |
| EU Novel Food (for export) | Not assessed | 12-24 months | RED | Gate 1 decision: develop EU pathway or exclude EU |
| Prop 65 testing | Not initiated | 2-4 weeks | YELLOW | Budget testing for Phase 2 |

**Gate Review Actions:**

- RED items: decide go/no-go on product or market; assign dedicated resource; set milestone dates
- YELLOW items: assign to Phase owner; establish completion deadline
- GREEN items: assign to standard QA process; monitor for slippage
