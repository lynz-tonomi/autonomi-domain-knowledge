# Commodity Procurement — F&B Beverage Manufacturing

## Overview

Commodity procurement in beverage manufacturing is a specialized discipline that combines agricultural market knowledge, futures hedging, supplier relationship management, and quality specification control. Unlike finished-goods purchasing, ingredient procurement requires understanding of crop cycles, weather impacts, geopolitical supply risks, and the interaction between raw commodity markets and processed ingredient pricing.

---

## 1. Juice Concentrate Markets

### Frozen Concentrated Orange Juice (FCOJ)

FCOJ-A is the benchmark contract traded on ICE Futures U.S. The contract unit is 15,000 pounds of orange solids (equivalent to approximately 2,160 gallons of 65-Brix concentrate). Key pricing dynamics:

| Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| Florida citrus greening (HLB) | Structural decline in FL production since 2005; FL now produces <20% of historic peak |
| Brazil (Sao Paulo state) | Dominant global supplier (~70% of world FCOJ trade); crop reports from Fundecitrus drive pricing |
| Spain/Mediterranean | NFC-focused; Valencia and blood orange; smaller volumes but premium quality for European-style products |
| Crop reports | USDA NASS Florida citrus forecast (monthly Oct-Jul); Fundecitrus crop estimate (semi-annual) |
| Futures curve | Typically backwardated during crop uncertainty; contango when supply is comfortable |

**NFC (Not-From-Concentrate) Pricing**: NFC orange juice trades at a significant premium to reconstituted FCOJ — typically 40-80% higher on a per-gallon basis. NFC pricing is less transparent than FCOJ futures; most transactions are negotiated bilaterally between processors (Cutrale, Citrosuco, Louis Dreyfus) and buyers. Contracts are typically 6-12 month terms with volume commitments.

**Other Juice Concentrates**:

| Concentrate | Key Origins | Pricing Benchmark | Typical Lead Time |
|---|---|---|---|
| Apple juice concentrate (AJC) | China (70%), Turkey, Poland, US (WA) | No futures; index pricing via Foodnews/Mintec | 8-12 weeks (China) |
| Grape juice concentrate | Argentina, Chile, Spain, US (CA) | Negotiated; tracks wine grape surplus | 6-10 weeks |
| Lemon juice concentrate | Argentina, Italy, Spain | Negotiated; seasonal availability | 6-10 weeks |
| Pineapple juice concentrate | Thailand, Philippines, Costa Rica | Negotiated; limited spot availability | 10-14 weeks |
| Cranberry juice concentrate | US (WI, MA), Canada, Chile | Negotiated; Ocean Spray influences market | 4-8 weeks domestic |

### Crop Report Calendar (Critical Procurement Dates)

| Report | Source | Timing | Relevance |
|---|---|---|---|
| FL Citrus Production Forecast | USDA NASS | Monthly, Oct-Jul | Sets FCOJ market tone for US crop |
| Fundecitrus Crop Estimate | Fundecitrus (Brazil) | May (preliminary), Dec (final) | Dominant global supply signal |
| USDA World Markets & Trade | USDA FAS | Semi-annual | Global citrus supply/demand balance |
| EU Short-Term Outlook | European Commission | Quarterly | Mediterranean citrus production |

## 2. Sweetener Procurement

### Sugar

Sugar #11 (ICE) is the world raw sugar futures benchmark. Contract unit is 112,000 pounds. Domestic US sugar pricing is significantly higher than world price due to US sugar program (tariff-rate quotas, price support loans).

| Sweetener | Pricing Mechanism | Typical Contract | Key Considerations |
|---|---|---|---|
| Refined cane sugar | US #16 futures or negotiated | Quarterly/semi-annual | US price 60-100% above world price; domestic sourcing preferred for "cane sugar" label claims |
| Refined beet sugar | Negotiated; tracks US #16 | Annual contracts common | Functionally equivalent to cane in most applications; some brands require cane for marketing |
| Organic cane sugar | Premium over conventional | Semi-annual; volume committed | 30-60% premium; limited certified mills (Brazil, Paraguay, Colombia); 8-12 week lead |
| HFCS 42 / HFCS 55 | Negotiated with ADM, Cargill, Ingredion, Tate & Lyle | Annual contracts with quarterly pricing adjustments | HFCS 55 standard for beverages; contracting is oligopolistic — 4 major producers |
| Liquid sucrose | Negotiated; tracks refined sugar | Quarterly | Delivered by tanker; requires receiving infrastructure |

### Specialty Sweeteners

Specialty and high-intensity sweeteners are the fastest-growing procurement category in functional beverages. Lead times and supply chain complexity are significantly higher than commodity sweeteners.

| Sweetener | Primary Origin | Lead Time | MOQ (typical) | Key Risks |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Stevia (Reb A 95%+) | China (Shandong, Anhui) | 12-16 weeks | 500 kg | Quality variability; bitter aftertaste at high purity requires blending expertise |
| Stevia (Reb M, Reb D) | China; enzymatic conversion | 12-16 weeks | 200 kg | Premium pricing ($80-200/kg vs $20-40 for Reb A); limited producers |
| Monk fruit extract | China (Guangxi province) | 16-20 weeks | 200 kg | Single-origin risk (>95% from Guangxi); crop yield variability; mogrosides content varies |
| Allulose | China, US (Ingredion, Tate & Lyle) | 8-12 weeks | 1,000 kg | Regulatory status varies by country; not approved in EU; US: not counted as sugar on label |
| Erythritol | China (fermentation) | 10-14 weeks | 1,000 kg | Bulk pricing volatile; some negative health press in 2023 created demand uncertainty |

**Hedging note**: Specialty sweeteners have no futures market. Risk management is purely through contract structure — fixed-price forwards, cost-plus with caps/floors, or strategic inventory builds.

## 3. Organic Ingredient Premiums & Availability

Organic ingredients command structural premiums driven by limited certified acreage, lower yields, and certification costs. For procurement planning:

| Ingredient Category | Organic Premium (over conventional) | Availability Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| Citrus juices (OJ, lemon) | 40-70% | Limited certified groves; Brazil organic FCOJ is thin market |
| Apple juice concentrate | 50-80% | Turkey and China dominate; US organic apple supply tight |
| Cane sugar | 30-60% | Brazil (Usina Sao Francisco, Native) and Paraguay primary sources |
| Flavors (natural organic compliant) | 60-120% | Must use organic-compliant solvents and carriers; limited flavor house capability |
| Stevia/monk fruit | 20-40% above conventional | Organic certified stevia farms are scarce; audit trail complexity |
| Dairy (for dairy-based beverages) | 80-120% | Organic milk supply tight; regional processor relationships critical |

**USDA Organic labeling tiers** and their procurement implications:
- **"100% Organic"**: Every ingredient must be certified organic — extremely limiting for beverages with functional ingredients
- **"Organic"**: 95%+ organic by weight (excl. water and salt) — standard target; allows 5% from National List
- **"Made with Organic [ingredient]"**: 70%+ organic — easier to achieve; less marketing impact
- Below 70%: Can list organic ingredients in ingredient panel only

## 4. Natural Flavor Sourcing

### Citrus Oils

Citrus essential oils are critical flavor components for beverages. The market is highly concentrated:

| Oil | Key Origins | Pricing Driver | Volatility |
|---|---|---|---|
| Orange oil (cold-pressed) | Brazil, US (FL) | Co-product of juice processing; supply tied to juice production | Moderate; tracks OJ crop |
| Lemon oil | Argentina, Italy, US (CA) | Argentine crop dominates; crop year Jan-Jun | High; weather-dependent |
| Lime oil (distilled) | Mexico, Peru | Mexican lime crop; cartel-like market structure | Very high; 2-3x swings in 12 months |
| Grapefruit oil | US (FL, TX) | Small market; FL citrus greening reducing supply | High; structural decline |

### Vanilla

Vanilla bean and extract pricing is among the most volatile of any food ingredient:

- **Madagascar** supplies ~70% of world vanilla; Bourbon vanilla (V. planifolia)
- Pricing has ranged from $20/kg to $600/kg over the past decade
- Extract (single-fold, 13.35 oz vanilla beans per gallon per FDA standard) pricing tracks bean cost
- **Procurement strategy**: Lock 12-24 month supply when prices dip below $100/kg; maintain 6-month safety stock minimum; consider synthetic vanillin (from guaiacol or lignin) as cost hedge for non-"natural vanilla" applications

### Botanical Extracts

| Extract | Origin | Lead Time | Standardization Challenge |
|---|---|---|---|
| Green tea extract | China, Japan | 8-12 weeks | EGCG content varies 40-98%; must spec to target |
| Turmeric extract (curcuminoids) | India | 10-14 weeks | Curcumin content 85-95%; solubility is formulation challenge |
| Ginger extract | China, India | 8-12 weeks | Gingerol content standardization; heat sensitivity |
| Ashwagandha (KSM-66, Sensoril) | India | 10-14 weeks | Branded ingredients have sole-source risk; withanolide content |
| Elderberry extract | Europe (Austria, Denmark), US | 8-12 weeks | Anthocyanin content varies; limited organic supply |

## 5. Packaging Material Procurement

### PET Resin

PET (polyethylene terephthalate) resin pricing is tied to crude oil via the paraxylene/PTA/MEG chain. Key dynamics:

- **Contract structures**: Quarterly pricing with formula tied to PET resin index (ICIS, PetroChem Wire)
- **rPET premium**: Recycled PET commands 5-20 cent/lb premium over virgin; availability constrained by collection infrastructure; California PCR mandates (AB 793) driving demand
- **Preform vs bottle**: Many co-packers blow their own bottles from preforms; procurement may be preforms not finished bottles
- **Lead times**: 4-6 weeks for standard preforms; 8-12 weeks for custom molds; 16-24 weeks for new mold tooling

### Aluminum Cans

| Format | Typical MOQ | Lead Time | Key Suppliers |
|---|---|---|---|
| 12 oz sleek (211 x 604) | 1 truckload (~204,000 cans) | 6-10 weeks printed | Ball, Ardagh, Crown |
| 12 oz standard (211 x 413) | 1 truckload | 4-8 weeks | Ball, Ardagh, Crown |
| 16 oz (211 x 610) | 1 truckload | 6-10 weeks | More limited availability |
| Brite (unprinted) + shrink sleeve | Lower MOQ (1/4 truckload possible) | 2-4 weeks brite + 4-6 weeks sleeves | Multiple; sleeves from CCL, Fort Dearborn |

**Aluminum market**: LME Aluminum futures set the metal cost component; Midwest Premium (MWP) adds US delivery premium; can sheet conversion cost is separate negotiation with can makers.

### Glass Bottles

- Lead times: 8-16 weeks for stock molds; 20-40 weeks for custom molds
- MOQs: Typically 1-2 truckloads minimum for stock; much higher for custom
- Weight reduction trend: Lightweighting saves freight and material cost
- Domestic vs import: Mexican glass is price-competitive but longer lead and quality variability

### Labels and Cartons

| Material | Lead Time | MOQ | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pressure-sensitive labels | 3-5 weeks | 10,000-50,000 labels | Most flexible for short runs |
| Shrink sleeves | 4-6 weeks | 50,000-100,000 sleeves | Full-body graphics; mandatory for irregular containers |
| Printed cartons (folding) | 4-6 weeks | 5,000-10,000 units | For multi-packs; tray + shrink wrap alternative |
| Corrugated cases | 2-4 weeks | 1,000-5,000 cases | RSC (regular slotted container) standard; custom print adds time |

## 6. Contract Structures

| Structure | Best For | Risk Profile | Typical Duration |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Spot purchase** | Small volumes, testing new ingredients, emergency buys | Full price exposure | Single PO |
| **Fixed-price forward** | Budget certainty; when market is favorable | Locked in — risk of overpaying if market drops | 3-12 months |
| **Cost-plus (formula)** | Long-term relationships; when both parties want fairness | Margin is fixed; input cost floats | 12+ months |
| **Cost-plus with cap/floor** | Hybrid — budget protection with some market participation | Bounded risk for both parties | 6-12 months |
| **Consignment** | Packaging materials; when supplier has inventory management capability | Supplier carries inventory cost; buyer pays on use | Ongoing |
| **Toll processing** | When buyer owns raw material but needs processing (e.g., own oranges, toll for juice extraction) | Buyer takes commodity risk; processor takes conversion margin | Seasonal/annual |

## 7. Hedging Strategies

For mid-scale F&B brands, direct futures trading is often impractical. Practical hedging approaches:

1. **Layered forward buying**: Buy 30% of annual needs 6 months ahead, 30% at 3 months, 40% spot — averages out price volatility
2. **Supplier-managed hedging**: Negotiate fixed-price contracts where the supplier hedges on their side; this embeds a premium but shifts complexity
3. **Inventory hedging**: Build strategic inventory when prices are favorable (requires warehouse capacity and working capital)
4. **Formula-based contracts with collars**: Set a price floor and ceiling tied to commodity index; share risk between parties
5. **Diversification across origins**: Source from multiple geographies to reduce single-origin supply shock risk

## 8. Landed Cost Calculation

A complete landed cost model for imported ingredients:

| Cost Component | Example (FCOJ from Brazil) |
|---|---|
| FOB Santos price | $2,200/MT |
| Ocean freight (reefer container) | $3,500-5,500 per 20' container |
| Marine insurance | 0.3-0.5% of CIF value |
| US Customs duty | FCOJ: 7.85 cents/liter (specific duty) |
| Harbor maintenance fee | 0.125% of cargo value |
| Merchandise processing fee | 0.3464% of cargo value (min $31.67, max $614.35) |
| Drayage (port to warehouse) | $500-1,500 depending on distance |
| Warehouse receiving | $35-75 per pallet |
| Quality hold / lab testing | $200-500 per lot |
| **Total landed cost** | **FOB + 15-30% typically** |

## 11. Supplier Diversification Strategy

**Rule of thumb for critical ingredients**: Maintain at least two qualified suppliers for any ingredient representing >10% of COGS or any ingredient where supply disruption would halt production.

**Diversification matrix**:

| Risk Level | Strategy | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Critical, sole-source** | Aggressively qualify backup; maintain safety stock | Branded ashwagandha extract (KSM-66) |
| **Critical, dual-source** | Maintain 70/30 or 60/40 split; keep both active | FCOJ from two Brazilian processors |
| **Important, available** | Primary + approved backup; spot-buy backup annually to keep qualification active | Citric acid (multiple global producers) |
| **Commodity, fungible** | Market-buy; no lock-in needed | Filtered water treatment chemicals |

**Single-source risk factors to evaluate**: Geographic concentration, financial health of supplier, regulatory risk (e.g., import restrictions), weather/climate exposure, and political instability in origin country.
