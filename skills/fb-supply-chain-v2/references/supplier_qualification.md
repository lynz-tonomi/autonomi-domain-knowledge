# Supplier Qualification — F&B Beverage Manufacturing

## Overview

Supplier qualification in food and beverage manufacturing is a regulatory obligation, a food safety imperative, and a business continuity discipline. Every ingredient, packaging material, and processing aid that enters a beverage production facility must come from a supplier that has been evaluated, approved, and monitored. The consequences of failure range from product recalls costing millions to criminal liability under FSMA for adulterated products. This reference covers the complete supplier qualification lifecycle from initial assessment through ongoing monitoring.

---

## 1. Approved Supplier Program (ASP) — Structure

### Program Requirements

An Approved Supplier Program is a prerequisite for GFSI certification (SQF, BRC, FSSC 22000) and a regulatory expectation under FSMA Preventive Controls (21 CFR 117 Subpart G — Supply-Chain Program).

| Program Element | Requirement | Documentation |
|---|---|---|
| Written ASP procedure | Documented policy for evaluating, approving, and monitoring suppliers | SOP in food safety management system |
| Risk assessment by material | Categorize materials by food safety risk (high, medium, low) | Risk matrix; updated annually |
| Approval criteria | Defined minimum standards for each risk level | Decision tree or checklist |
| Approved supplier list | Master list with approval status, date, next review date | Controlled document; accessible to receiving |
| Receiving verification | Procedures to verify incoming materials meet specs | Inspection SOPs; COA review procedure |
| Monitoring and reassessment | Ongoing performance tracking; annual reassessment | Supplier scorecards; re-audit schedule |
| Non-approved supplier procedure | Emergency-use protocol with enhanced verification | Deviation/exception SOP |

### Material Risk Classification

| Risk Level | Definition | Examples | Approval Requirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| **High** | Direct food contact; potential for biological, chemical, or physical hazards; allergen-containing | Juice concentrates, dairy ingredients, flavors with allergens, processing aids | GFSI certification + COA per lot + annual assessment |
| **Medium** | Direct food contact; lower hazard risk; non-allergenic | Sugar, citric acid, ascorbic acid, water treatment chemicals | GFSI certification or supplier questionnaire + periodic COA |
| **Low** | Indirect food contact or non-food contact | Packaging (PET, cans, labels), cleaning chemicals, lubricants | Supplier questionnaire + food-grade certification/compliance letter |

## 2. GFSI Certification as Baseline

### Recognized GFSI Benchmarked Schemes

| Scheme | Full Name | Typical Sectors | Audit Body Examples |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SQF** | Safe Quality Food | Broad; dominant in US CPG | SQFI (managed by FMI) |
| **BRC** | Brand Reputation through Compliance (now BRCGS) | Global; strong in UK/EU; growing in US | BRCGS |
| **FSSC 22000** | Food Safety System Certification 22000 | Manufacturing; process-heavy | Foundation FSSC |
| **IFS** | International Featured Standard | Europe-dominant | IFS Management GmbH |
| **GLOBALG.A.P.** | Global Good Agricultural Practices | Primary production (farms, growers) | FoodPLUS GmbH |

**Practical policy**: Require GFSI certification for all high-risk ingredient suppliers. Accept SQF, BRC, FSSC 22000, or IFS — do not mandate a specific scheme. For small, artisanal, or specialty suppliers unable to achieve GFSI, conduct your own on-site audit or accept a third-party food safety audit to an equivalent standard, with documented risk acceptance.

### What GFSI Certification Tells You (and What It Does Not)

| GFSI Tells You | GFSI Does Not Tell You |
|---|---|
| Supplier has a food safety management system | Whether their product meets YOUR specifications |
| Hazard analysis and critical controls are in place | Whether their delivery performance is reliable |
| Facility is audited annually by accredited third party | Whether their quality is consistent lot-to-lot |
| Corrective action processes exist | Whether they can scale with your growth |
| Traceability and recall procedures are documented | Whether their financial health is stable |

## 3. Supplier Audit Protocols

### Audit Types

| Audit Type | Description | When Used | Cost | Duration |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Desktop / document review** | Review certifications, questionnaire responses, COAs, specs | Initial screening for all suppliers | Internal labor only | 2-4 hours |
| **Virtual audit** | Video walkthrough of facility + document review | Medium-risk suppliers; geographic distance | Internal labor + travel avoided | 4-8 hours |
| **On-site audit (your team)** | Your QA/food safety team visits supplier facility | High-risk suppliers; critical ingredients; new strategic suppliers | $2,000-5,000 (travel + labor) | 1-2 days |
| **Third-party certification audit** | Accredited CB audits to GFSI scheme standard | GFSI certification as ASP requirement | Supplier bears cost ($3,000-15,000) | 1-3 days |
| **Third-party custom audit** | Hired auditor evaluates to your checklist | When you cannot visit; need independent assessment | $1,500-4,000 | 1 day |

### On-Site Audit Checklist (Beverage Ingredient Supplier)

| Category | Key Observations | Scoring |
|---|---|---|
| **Facility & grounds** | Building integrity, pest exclusion, waste management, drainage, adjacent land use | Critical / Major / Minor |
| **Personnel practices** | Hygiene, handwashing, PPE, training records, illness reporting | Critical / Major / Minor |
| **Raw material control** | Incoming inspection, COA review, segregation, traceability | Critical / Major / Minor |
| **Process controls** | HACCP plan, CCP monitoring, deviation procedures, validation records | Critical / Major / Minor |
| **Allergen management** | Segregation, labeling, cleaning validation, changeover procedures | Critical / Major / Minor |
| **Sanitation** | Master sanitation schedule, CIP records, environmental monitoring, chemical controls | Critical / Major / Minor |
| **Pest management** | IPM program, pest activity trends, device placement, corrective actions | Critical / Major / Minor |
| **Traceability** | Lot coding, batch records, mock recall results (<4 hours, >95% recovery) | Critical / Major / Minor |
| **Laboratory** | Testing capabilities, method validation, calibration, retain samples | Critical / Major / Minor |
| **Storage & shipping** | Temperature control, FEFO, load condition, transportation hygiene | Critical / Major / Minor |

**Scoring rules**: Any Critical finding = fail (approval denied or suspended until corrected). More than 3 Major findings = conditional approval with corrective action deadline. Minor findings documented; tracked for trend.

## 4. Certificate of Analysis (COA) Requirements

### COA by Material Type

#### Juice Concentrates and NFC Juice

| Parameter | Specification Example | Test Method |
|---|---|---|
| Brix | 65.0 +/- 1.0 (FCOJ) or 11.5 +/- 0.5 (NFC) | Refractometer (AOAC 932.12) |
| Brix:Acid ratio | 12-16:1 (orange) | Calculated |
| Titratable acidity (% citric) | 3.0-4.5% (FCOJ) | Titration (AOAC 942.15) |
| pH | 3.2-4.0 | pH meter |
| Color (OJ score) | USDA color score 36-40 | USDA color comparator |
| Essential oil content | <0.035 mL/100 mL (for FCOJ per FDA standard) | Scott oil test |
| Total Plate Count (APC) | <1,000 CFU/mL (pasteurized) | AOAC 990.12 |
| Yeast & Mold | <100 CFU/mL | AOAC 997.02 |
| Coliforms | <10 CFU/mL | AOAC 991.14 |
| E. coli | Not detected in 25 mL | AOAC 998.08 |
| Salmonella | Not detected in 25 mL | AOAC 2004.03 |
| Patulin (apple) | <50 ppb (FDA limit); <10 ppb preferred | HPLC |
| Heavy metals (Pb, As, Cd, Hg) | Per California Prop 65 limits | ICP-MS |
| Allergen declaration | Tree nut free, soy free, etc. | Supplier declaration; ELISA if warranted |
| Pesticide residues | Below MRLs per 40 CFR 180 | Multi-residue screen (GC-MS/LC-MS) |

#### Sweeteners (Sugar, HFCS, Specialty)

| Parameter | Specification Example | Test Method |
|---|---|---|
| Purity / Assay | >99.5% sucrose; >95% Reb A | HPLC |
| Moisture | <0.1% (dry sugar) | Karl Fischer or loss on drying |
| Color (ICUMSA) | <45 IU (refined white sugar) | Spectrophotometric |
| Ash | <0.04% | Conductivity ash |
| Total Plate Count | <200 CFU/g (dry sugar) | Standard plate count |
| Yeast & Mold | <10 CFU/g | Standard methods |
| SO2 (sulfite) | <20 ppm (or declare if >10 ppm per FALCPA) | Monier-Williams |
| Heavy metals | Per USP/FCC limits; Prop 65 for CA | ICP-MS |
| Non-GMO status | Non-GMO Project Verified or IP declaration | Certificate or testing |

#### Packaging Materials (PET, Cans, Glass)

| Parameter | Requirement | Verification |
|---|---|---|
| FDA compliance letter | 21 CFR 177 (plastics) or 21 CFR 175 (coatings) | Supplier letter on file; renewed annually |
| Migration / extractables | Per FDA guidelines for food-contact materials | Third-party test report |
| BPA-free declaration (cans) | BPA-NI (non-intent) lining declaration | Supplier letter |
| Physical dimensions | Per agreed specification drawing | Receiving inspection; SPC data from supplier |
| Print quality | Color within Delta E tolerance; text legible; barcode scannable | Visual inspection + barcode verification |
| Functional testing | Seal integrity, closure torque, drop test | Supplier test data; periodic in-house verification |

## 5. Incoming Material Inspection and Testing

### Inspection Levels

| Level | What Happens | Applied To | Frequency |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Level 1 — Full inspection** | COA review + visual inspection + in-house or third-party lab testing | New suppliers; new materials; after quality incidents | Every lot |
| **Level 2 — Standard inspection** | COA review + visual inspection + targeted testing (Brix, pH, micro) | Established suppliers with good track record | Every lot; reduced testing panel |
| **Level 3 — Skip-lot** | COA review + visual inspection; lab testing every 3rd-5th lot | High-performing suppliers with 12+ months perfect history | Per skip-lot program schedule |
| **Level 4 — Vendor-managed** | Supplier tests and certifies; COA accepted without in-house testing | Only for highest-trust suppliers with verified lab capability | COA per lot; annual audit confirms |

### Skip-Lot Program Criteria

To qualify for skip-lot (reduced testing), a supplier must demonstrate:
1. Minimum 12 consecutive lots with zero nonconformances
2. Current GFSI certification with no critical or major findings
3. Supplier capability index (Cpk) >1.33 for key parameters
4. No customer complaints traced to the supplier in the past 12 months
5. Executed quality agreement with change notification clause

**Escalation back to full testing**: Any single lot failure immediately returns the supplier to Level 1 testing for a minimum of 5 consecutive conforming lots.

### Third-Party Laboratory Verification

| Lab Type | Examples | Use Case | Typical TAT |
|---|---|---|---|
| Accredited food testing lab | Eurofins, Merieux NutriSciences, SGS, Intertek | Micro, chemistry, allergens, heavy metals | 3-7 business days |
| In-house QC lab | Brand's own or co-man's lab | Brix, pH, titratable acidity, visual | Same day |
| Referee lab | Designated neutral lab in quality agreement | Dispute resolution on test results | 5-10 business days |

## 6. Supplier Scorecards

### Scorecard Dimensions and Weighting

| Dimension | Weight | Metrics | Scoring |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Quality** | 40% | COA conformance rate, nonconformance count, customer complaint trace-backs, audit findings | 100 = zero issues; deduct per incident |
| **Delivery** | 25% | On-time delivery rate (OTIF), lead time adherence, fill rate | 100 = 100% OTIF; deduct per late/short delivery |
| **Cost** | 20% | Price competitiveness vs market benchmark, total cost of ownership, cost-reduction initiatives | 100 = at or below market; deduct for above-market |
| **Responsiveness** | 15% | Response time to inquiries (<24 hr target), SCAR response timeliness, flexibility on urgent orders | 100 = consistently responsive; deduct per slow response |

### Scorecard Rating Scale

| Score | Rating | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 90-100 | **Preferred** | Eligible for increased volume share; skip-lot testing; partnership programs |
| 75-89 | **Approved** | Standard business relationship; routine monitoring |
| 60-74 | **Conditional** | Improvement plan required; 90-day corrective action window; increased inspection |
| Below 60 | **Probation** | Source reduction strategy; actively qualify replacement; exit within 6 months |

### Scorecard Cadence

- **Quarterly**: Issue scorecards to top 20 suppliers (by spend or risk)
- **Semi-annually**: Issue scorecards to remaining approved suppliers
- **Annually**: Full supplier base review; consolidation and rationalization decisions
- **Ad hoc**: Triggered by major quality event, recall, or delivery failure

## 7. Corrective Action Process (SCAR)

### SCAR — Supplier Corrective Action Request

A SCAR is issued when a supplier delivers nonconforming material or fails to meet a contractual obligation. The process:

| Step | Owner | Timeline | Deliverable |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Nonconformance identification | Buyer QA | Day 0 | NCR (Nonconformance Report) with evidence |
| 2. SCAR issuance | Buyer QA | Day 0-1 | Formal SCAR form sent to supplier with description, lot details, evidence |
| 3. Containment action | Supplier | Within 24 hours | Immediate action to prevent recurrence on in-process/in-transit material |
| 4. Root cause analysis | Supplier | Within 10 business days | Documented RCA using 5-Why, fishbone, or 8D methodology |
| 5. Corrective action plan | Supplier | Within 15 business days | Specific actions, responsible persons, target dates |
| 6. Verification of implementation | Supplier (with buyer review) | Within 30 business days | Evidence that corrective actions are implemented |
| 7. Effectiveness verification | Buyer QA | 60-90 days post-implementation | Confirm nonconformance has not recurred; close SCAR |

### SCAR Severity Levels

| Level | Criteria | Supplier Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Minor** | Cosmetic, documentation, or minor spec deviation with no food safety impact | SCAR required; 1 occurrence does not affect scorecard significantly |
| **Major** | Repeated minor issues; spec failure requiring rework; delivery failure causing production interruption | SCAR required; scorecard deduction; may trigger conditional status |
| **Critical** | Food safety hazard; adulterated material; undeclared allergen; fraud/misrepresentation | Immediate supplier suspension; SCAR + on-site audit required for reinstatement; may be permanent disqualification |

## 8. Ingredient Specifications and Tolerances

### Writing Effective Ingredient Specifications

A complete ingredient specification document includes:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Header** | Material name, supplier, spec number, revision date, approval signatures |
| **General description** | Physical form, color, odor, taste expectations |
| **Composition** | Ingredient statement (for multi-component ingredients); sub-ingredients listed |
| **Chemical specifications** | Brix, pH, acidity, moisture, water activity, specific gravity; with targets and acceptable ranges |
| **Microbiological specifications** | APC, yeast/mold, coliforms, E. coli, Salmonella, Listeria; limits and methods |
| **Physical specifications** | Particle size, viscosity, color value, clarity/turbidity |
| **Allergen status** | Contains/may contain declarations for Big 9 allergens (per FASTER Act) |
| **GMO status** | GMO, non-GMO IP, Non-GMO Project Verified, organic |
| **Packaging and labeling** | Container type, net weight, lot code format, label requirements |
| **Storage and shelf life** | Storage conditions, shelf life from date of manufacture |
| **Regulatory compliance** | FDA GRAS status, any state-specific requirements (CA Prop 65), country of origin |
| **Approved deviations** | Any known seasonal or source-dependent variations that are pre-approved |

### Tolerance Setting Guidelines

| Parameter Type | Typical Tolerance | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Brix (concentrates) | +/- 0.5 to 1.0 degree | Tighter for formulation-critical applications |
| pH | +/- 0.2 to 0.3 units | Critical for flavor and preservation |
| Titratable acidity | +/- 0.1 to 0.3% | Wider acceptable range for natural products |
| Color | +/- 2 Delta E (instrumental) | Tighter for clear beverages where color is visible |
| Moisture (dry ingredients) | +/- 0.5 to 1.0% | Affects reconstitution ratios and shelf life |
| Microbiological | Pass/Fail against limits | No tolerance on pathogen specs — zero tolerance for Salmonella, Listeria, E. coli O157 |

## 9. Change Notification Requirements

### Supplier Change Notification Policy

Suppliers must notify the buyer in writing a minimum of 90 days before any of the following changes:

| Change Type | Examples | Buyer Action Required |
|---|---|---|
| **Manufacturing location** | Moving production to a new facility; adding a second facility | Re-qualification audit; update ASL; possible re-registration |
| **Process change** | Changing pasteurization method, filtration, concentration process | Evaluation of impact on specifications; possible re-validation |
| **Raw material source** | Changing sub-supplier of a key sub-ingredient | Review impact on allergen status, GMO status, quality profile |
| **Formulation change** | Modifying ingredient ratios in a blended ingredient | Full re-evaluation; label review; possible reformulation |
| **Specification change** | Proposing to widen or narrow a spec range | Evaluate impact on finished product; approve or reject |
| **Packaging change** | Changing container material, liner, closure | Food-contact compliance review; shelf-life re-evaluation |
| **Key personnel change** | QA manager or food safety team leader departure | Assess impact on food safety system; may trigger interim audit |
| **Regulatory status change** | Loss of GFSI certification, FDA warning letter, recall | Immediate notification required; may trigger suspension |

**Contractual enforcement**: Change notification requirements must be embedded in the quality agreement (part of the supply agreement). Unapproved changes discovered after the fact are grounds for SCAR issuance and potential supplier suspension.

## 10. Backup Supplier Development

### Backup Supplier Qualification Timeline

| Phase | Duration | Activities |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Identification | 2-4 weeks | Market scan; RFQ to 3-5 candidates; preliminary assessment |
| 2. Document review | 2-4 weeks | GFSI certificate, COAs, specs, allergen questionnaire, food safety questionnaire |
| 3. Sample evaluation | 4-8 weeks | Request samples; lab testing against spec; formulation trial; sensory evaluation |
| 4. On-site audit (if high-risk) | 2-4 weeks | Schedule and conduct facility audit |
| 5. Trial production run | 2-4 weeks | Small-scale production run using backup supplier material; full QC on finished product |
| 6. Approval and documentation | 1-2 weeks | Add to approved supplier list; file all documentation |
| **Total** | **3-6 months** | From identification to approved status |

### Keeping Backup Suppliers Active

A qualified backup supplier that never receives orders will lose familiarity with your specifications and may deprioritize your business. Best practices:

1. **Annual test order**: Place at least one production-scale order per year to maintain commercial relationship and verify quality
2. **Spec maintenance**: Send updated specifications to backup suppliers whenever primary supplier specs are revised
3. **Market intelligence**: Maintain quarterly pricing contact even if not ordering — keeps relationship warm and provides market benchmark
4. **Dual-sourcing where practical**: Run 80/20 or 70/30 split between primary and backup to keep both operationally engaged

## 11. Sole-Source Risk Assessment

### Risk Assessment Matrix

| Factor | Low Risk (1) | Medium Risk (2) | High Risk (3) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Number of qualified suppliers** | 3+ qualified | 2 qualified | 1 (sole source) |
| **Geographic concentration** | Multiple countries/regions | Multiple sites, same country | Single site |
| **Financial stability** | Large, publicly traded, strong balance sheet | Mid-size, private, stable | Small, startup, overleveraged |
| **Material criticality** | Substitutable; multiple alternatives | Limited alternatives; requires reformulation | Irreplaceable; branded/patented ingredient |
| **Lead time** | <4 weeks domestic | 6-12 weeks | >12 weeks (import) |
| **Regulatory risk** | No regulatory concerns | Some regulatory exposure | Active regulatory issue or high-risk origin |

**Risk score**: Sum of all factors. Score 6-8 = low risk; 9-12 = moderate; 13-18 = high. High-risk materials require documented mitigation plan and executive acknowledgment.

### Sole-Source Mitigation Strategies

| Strategy | Description | Cost | Effectiveness |
|---|---|---|---|
| Safety stock build | Hold 60-90 days of inventory vs standard 14-21 | Working capital + storage cost | High short-term buffer |
| Qualify backup supplier | Full qualification of alternative source | $5K-25K + 3-6 months | Highest long-term protection |
| Alternative formulation | Develop formula variant using substitute ingredient | R&D cost + shelf life study | Moderate; requires separate production |
| Contract protection | Long-term supply agreement with penalty for non-delivery | Legal cost; potentially higher pricing | Moderate; doesn't prevent force majeure |
| Vertical integration | Invest in or acquire the supplier capability | Significant capital | Highest; but rarely justified for emerging brands |

## 12. FSMA 204 — Food Traceability Rule (Supplier Requirements)

### Overview

FDA's Food Traceability Rule (21 CFR Part 1, Subpart S) requires enhanced traceability records for foods on the Food Traceability List (FTL). Effective January 20, 2026.

### Foods on the FTL Relevant to Beverage Manufacturing

| FTL Category | Beverage Relevance |
|---|---|
| Fresh-cut fruits and vegetables | Fresh juice ingredients (cut fruit for cold-press) |
| Fresh herbs | Herb-infused beverages |
| Shell eggs | Egg-based beverages (eggnog) |
| Nut butters | Nut milk production (almond, cashew) |
| Fresh, frozen fruits and vegetables (not on exemption list) | Smoothie ingredients; frozen fruit for juice |
| Finfish (including smoked) | Fish-based broths (emerging category) |

### Key Data Elements (KDEs) Suppliers Must Provide

At each Critical Tracking Event (CTE) — growing, receiving, transforming, shipping — specific KDEs are required:

| CTE | Required KDEs from Supplier |
|---|---|
| **Shipping** | Traceability lot code, quantity/UOM, product description, ship-from location (GLN or address), ship-to location, date shipped, reference document (BOL, ASN) |
| **Transformation (if supplier processes)** | Input traceability lot codes, new output lot code, quantity, product description, location, date of transformation |

### Buyer Obligations at Receiving

| KDE | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Traceability lot code | Record the lot code from supplier label/documentation |
| Quantity and UOM | Verify and record quantity received |
| Product description | Commodity, variety, brand (if applicable) |
| Ship-from location | GLN or full address of immediate previous source |
| Receiving location | Your facility GLN or address |
| Date received | Actual receipt date |
| Reference document type and number | BOL, PO, ASN — link to your receiving record |
| Traceability plan reference | TLC (Traceability Lot Code) source reference |

**Record retention**: Maintain all traceability records for 2 years from creation date. Records must be available to FDA within 24 hours of request (electronic or paper).

## 13. Letters of Guarantee — Allergen Status

### Purpose

A Letter of Guarantee (LOG) is a formal written declaration from a supplier attesting that their product is free from specific allergens. It shifts some legal liability to the supplier for allergen misrepresentation.

### What a Valid LOG Must Include

| Element | Requirement |
|---|---|
| Supplier company legal name and address | Full legal entity name |
| Product identification | Specific product name, code, or specification number |
| Allergen declaration | Explicit statement of which allergens are absent (must reference Big 9 under FASTER Act: milk, eggs, fish, shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, sesame) |
| Basis for declaration | "Based on formulation review, supplier verification, and/or analytical testing" |
| Shared facility/line disclosure | Statement of whether product is made in a facility or on equipment that also processes any allergen |
| Authorized signature | Signed by QA manager, VP Quality, or equivalent with authority |
| Date and validity period | Issue date and expiration (typically annual renewal) |

### Limitations of Letters of Guarantee

- A LOG does not replace testing. For high-risk allergen situations (e.g., product marketed as "peanut-free" for allergic consumers), periodic ELISA or PCR verification testing is prudent.
- LOGs are only as reliable as the supplier's allergen management program. An on-site audit should verify the supplier's actual allergen controls before relying on the LOG.
- Advisory "may contain" statements on supplier products should be investigated — a LOG claiming "free from peanut" alongside a label saying "may contain peanut" is a red flag requiring resolution.
- LOGs must be renewed annually and any time the supplier changes formulation, manufacturing location, or shared equipment.

## 14. Quality Agreements

### Elements of a Supplier Quality Agreement

A quality agreement is a binding document (usually an exhibit to the supply agreement) that formalizes quality expectations:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| Specifications | Reference to current ingredient/material specification (by spec number and revision) |
| COA requirements | What parameters, frequency, and format for COAs |
| Change notification | 90-day minimum advance notice for any changes (see Section 9) |
| Nonconformance handling | SCAR process; timeline for response; cost responsibility for nonconforming material |
| Audit rights | Buyer's right to audit supplier facility with reasonable notice (24-72 hours) |
| Recall cooperation | Obligation to participate in recall activities; provide traceability data within 24 hours |
| Confidentiality | Protection of buyer's specifications and formulations |
| Regulatory compliance | Supplier warrants compliance with applicable FDA, state, and international regulations |
| Insurance requirements | Minimum product liability insurance ($2M-5M typical for ingredient suppliers) |
| Dispute resolution | Process for test result disputes; referee laboratory designation |
