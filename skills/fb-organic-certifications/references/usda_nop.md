## 1. Regulatory Framework

### Statutory Authority
- **Organic Foods Production Act of 1990 (OFPA):** 7 U.S.C. §§6501–6524
  - Established legal framework for organic agriculture
  - Mandated USDA to develop national standards for organic production and handling
  - Authorized civil penalties for willful violations: up to $11,000 per violation (§6519(c))

### Regulatory Authority
- **7 CFR Part 205 — National Organic Program**
  - Administered by USDA Agricultural Marketing Service (AMS)
  - Accredits certifying agents
  - Oversees compliance and enforcement
  - Maintains National List of Allowed and Prohibited Substances

### Key Implementation Dates
- **Final Rule published:** December 21, 2000
- **Effective date:** February 20, 2001
- **Strengthening Organic Enforcement (SOE) Rule:** Published January 19, 2023
  - **Initial effective date:** March 20, 2023
  - **Phased implementation through:** March 19, 2024

### Strengthening Organic Enforcement (SOE) Rule — Key Provisions
- **NOP Import Certificates:** Required for all organic products imported into the US (effective March 20, 2023)
- **Certifier oversight strengthened:** Minimum unannounced inspection rates increased to 5% annually (§205.406(a))
- **Supply chain traceability:** Handlers must maintain documented traceability for all organic ingredients and products
- **Fraud prevention measures:** Enhanced certifier monitoring; audits of high-risk suppliers; documentation of organic status of all inputs
- **Certification of operations requirements:** Expanded certifier responsibilities for verifying organic status of all ingredients/inputs at time of audit
- **Equivalency arrangement review:** Framework for evaluating international equivalency arrangements

## 2. Definitions and Scope (§205.2)

### Key Definitions

**Organic production (§205.2):**
- Production system managed in accordance with the Organic Foods Production Act of 1990 and NOP regulations
- Encompasses crop production, livestock production, and handling

**Handler (§205.2):**
- Any person engaged in selling, processing, or packaging agricultural products denoted or represented as "100% organic," "organic," or "made with organic" (specified percentage)
- Includes processors, retailers with in-store processing, exporters, importers, distributors, brokers

**Producer (§205.2):**
- Any person who engages in growing or producing food, fiber, feed, and other agricultural-based consumer products

**Certifying agent (§205.2):**
- Any entity accredited by the USDA Secretary to certify organic operations
- Must meet accreditation standards under 7 CFR Part 205, Subpart E

**Farming operation (§205.2):**
- All contiguous land under same management (for transition purposes)
- Can consist of separate fields if separated by buffer zones

**Excluded methods (§205.2):**
- Variety of methods used to genetically modify organisms or influence growth and development by means not possible under natural conditions
- **Included:** Cell fusion, microencapsulation, macroencapsulation, recombinant DNA technology
- **NOT included:** Traditional breeding, conjugation, fermentation, hybridization, in vitro fertilization, tissue culture

### Scope and Exemptions (§205.101)

**Excluded operations (§205.101(a)):**
1. Retail food establishments that handle organically produced agricultural products but do **not** process them (e.g., grocery stores selling fresh organic produce without further processing)
2. Operations with gross organic sales ≤$5,000 per year

**Important note on exemptions:**
- Exempt operations **must still comply** with all applicable production and handling standards
- **Cannot use** the USDA organic seal or make organic claims without becoming certified
- Exempt status applies only to certification requirement, not standard compliance

**Operations subject to NOP:**
- All producers making organic claims
- All handlers making organic claims
- Certifying agents (for compliance oversight)

## 3. Organic System Plan (OSP) Requirements (§205.201)

### Handler OSP Requirements (§205.201(b))

The OSP must include:

1. **Description of practices and procedures**
   - Production and handling practices for each product line
   - Identification of each production/handling step
   - Procedures for documenting compliance

2. **Complete input list (§205.201(b)(2))**
   - Every substance used as production or handling input
   - For each substance: brand name, manufacturer/source
   - Quantity and frequency of use
   - Point(s) of application in the production/handling system
   - Certification that all inputs comply with NOP standards

3. **Monitoring practices and procedures (§205.201(b)(3))**
   - How the operation will verify the OSP is being implemented
   - Frequency of verification activities
   - Records to be maintained

4. **Recordkeeping system description**
   - System for tracking inputs, outputs, and documentation
   - How records will be organized and maintained
   - System for demonstrating traceability

5. **Management practices to prevent commingling and contamination (§205.201(b)(4))**
   - Physical barriers and segregation protocols
   - Equipment sanitation procedures
   - Scheduling and production controls
   - Access controls to prevent contamination
   - Procedures specific to parallel production (if applicable)

6. **Additional information as required by certifying agent**
   - Certifying agent may request additional OSP details
   - Must be relevant to compliance determination

7. **Annual updates (§205.406(d))**
   - OSP must be updated annually
   - Updates must reflect any changes to operations, inputs, procedures
   - Updated OSP submitted with annual recertification

8. **SOE additions — Supply chain traceability and fraud prevention**
   - Documentation of all organic ingredient sources
   - Certificates of organic status from all suppliers
   - Procedures to verify organic status at point of purchase
   - Process for identifying and investigating high-risk inputs
   - Documentation of verification activities

### Producer OSP Requirements (§205.201(a))

In addition to handler requirements above, the producer OSP must include:

1. **Soil and water resource management**
   - Description of practices to maintain or improve natural resources
   - Soil organic matter management and monitoring
   - Water conservation and quality protection strategies
   - Erosion control measures

2. **Seed and planting stock sources**
   - Source of all seeds and propagating materials used
   - Documentation of organic certification of seed/planting stock, or
   - Justification if nonorganic seed/planting stock used (unavailability documentation)
   - Treatment history (prohibited treatments documented)

3. **Crop rotation plan (§205.201(a)(1)(iii))**
   - Specific rotation sequence for each field
   - Crops or cover crops to be grown in rotation
   - Timeline for rotation implementation
   - How rotation addresses soil fertility, pest/weed/disease management

4. **Soil fertility and crop nutrient management practices**
   - Fertilization sources: compost, manure, plant materials, minerals
   - Composting protocols if applicable (C:N ratio, temperature, duration, turning schedule)
   - Application timing and rates
   - Monitoring of soil fertility (testing schedule)
   - Micronutrient and trace element management

5. **Pest, weed, and disease management practices**
   - Identification of known pests, weeds, diseases in operation
   - Tiered approach documented: preventive → mechanical/physical → biological → allowed substances
   - Specific practices for each pest/weed/disease management need
   - Use of National List substances with restrictions and justification
   - Monitoring frequency and methods

6. **Monitoring practices and procedures (§205.201(a)(1)(vi))**
   - Regular field inspections and documentation
   - Testing programs (soil, pest populations, etc.)
   - Record maintenance for monitoring activities
   - How monitoring will identify deviations from the OSP

## 4. Producer (Crop) Standards (§205.202–§205.206)

### Land Requirements and Transition (§205.202)

**Organic land eligibility (§205.202(a)):**
- **No prohibited substances applied for 3 years** prior to harvest of organic crop
- Prohibited substances include: synthetic pesticides, synthetic fertilizers, genetic engineering, prohibited growth regulators
- Exception: substances allowed under National List are not considered "prohibited"
- "Three years" means 36 months from date prohibited substance was last applied to date crop is harvested

**Documentation required:**
- Field history (previous uses, operators, crops grown)
- Input records for 3-year period
- Affidavits from previous operators (if applicable)
- Maps showing field boundaries
- Neighboring land use documentation (potential drift/contamination sources)

**Buffer zones (§205.202(c)):**
- Distinct boundaries or barriers to prevent unintended contact with prohibited substances from adjacent land
- Must account for: topography, wind patterns, spray drift potential, water runoff, neighboring pesticide application
- **No specific minimum distance mandated by NOP** — adequacy determined on case-by-case basis by certifying agent
- Common practice: 10–25 feet for field crops, greater distances for tree crops or high-drift situations
- Vegetation barriers, terrain features, or physical structures acceptable

### Soil Fertility and Crop Nutrient Management (§205.203)

**General requirement (§205.203(a)):**
- Producer must manage soil fertility through crop rotations, cover crops, and application of plant and animal materials
- Primary focus on building soil organic matter and biological activity

**Approved soil-building practices:**
- Crop rotation with legumes for nitrogen fixation
- Cover crops (green manure)
- Compost (livestock or plant-based)
- Vermicompost
- Peat (limited — only where environmentally responsible)
- Composted seaweed and other plant materials
- Animal manures (from certified organic livestock or conventional sources with restrictions)

**Composting requirements (§205.203(c)):**
- **Hot composting (in-vessel or static aerated pile):**
  - Temperature: 131°F–170°F (55°C–76.7°C)
  - Duration: minimum 15 days
  - Minimum 5 turnings (static pile) or continuous aeration (in-vessel)

- **Windrow composting:**
  - Temperature: minimum 131°F (55°C)
  - Duration: minimum 3 days
  - Achievable only with active management and monitoring

- **Carbon-to-nitrogen (C:N) ratio:**
  - Must be managed to optimize decomposition
  - Typical target: 25:1 to 30:1
  - Feedstock materials must be documented

**Manure use restrictions:**
- Raw manure from conventional sources: must be aged 120 days before application to crops for human consumption (9 months for root vegetables)
- Composted manure: must meet hot-composting requirements above
- Manure from organic livestock: may be applied fresh if precautions taken to prevent pathogen transfer
- No prohibited substances in manure source operations

**Prohibited fertilizers:**
- Synthetic nitrogen fertilizers (except those on National List)
- Synthetic potassium chloride (muriate of potash)
- Urea (synthetic form)
- Sewage sludge

### Seeds and Planting Stock (§205.204)

**Organic seed/planting stock requirement (§205.204(a)):**
- **Must use organically grown seeds and planting stock** whenever available
- Organic status: grown under NOP standards in previous crop cycle
- Documentation: organic certificate from seed supplier or producer

**Exception for unavailability (§205.204(d)):**
- If organic seed/planting stock commercially unavailable in variety, quantity, or quality needed:
  - May use **untreated nonorganic seed/planting stock**
  - **Cannot use seed treated with prohibited substances**
  - Prohibited treatments include: synthetic fungicides, insecticides, growth regulators
  - Allowed seed treatments: botanical derivatives, elemental sulfur, copper (if on National List)
  - Must document availability search and maintain records

**Seed treatment restrictions:**
- All seed treatments must be documented and justify use
- Treated seeds purchased from supplier: supplier documentation required
- Proof of nonorganic source treatment status required

**Propagating material:**
- Applies to all vegetative propagation: cuttings, grafts, tissue culture, root divisions
- Must originate from organically managed plants
- Exception: minor perennial crops (small fruit bush propagation) — certifier may approve nonorganic source for specific cases

### Crop Rotation (§205.205)

**Requirement (§205.205(a)):**
- Crop rotation is a **required practice** for certified crop producers
- Exception: perennial crops; certifier may approve exemption with documented justification

**Purposes of crop rotation:**
1. Maintain and improve soil organic matter
2. Manage soil deficiencies and excesses (nitrogen, potassium, phosphorus)
3. Control pests, weeds, and diseases through breaking life cycles
4. Provide nitrogen through legume cultivation

**Documentation required in OSP:**
- Specific rotation sequence for each field/production area
- Crops or cover crops in rotation
- Timeline for rotation cycle
- How rotation addresses identified production issues

**Flexibility:**
- Rotation cycle length not mandated (typically 3–5 years)
- Field-by-field rotation: allowed; entire farm need not rotate simultaneously
- "Cash crop" years may alternate with cover crop years
- Certifier determines adequacy of planned rotation

### Pest, Weed, and Disease Management (§205.206)

**Tiered approach (§205.206(a)):**
Management strategy must follow this priority order:

**Tier 1 — Prevention (cultural practices):**
- Crop variety selection (resistant varieties)
- Crop rotation
- Sanitation (removal of crop residues, infected plants)
- Timing of plantings to avoid pest/disease peaks
- Spacing and density management
- Irrigation and drainage management
- Companion planting

**Tier 2 — Mechanical and physical controls:**
- Hand weeding
- Cultivation
- Mulching
- Flaming
- Traps and barriers
- Physical removal of pests/diseased plant material
- Exclusion netting or row covers

**Tier 3 — Biological controls:**
- Beneficial insects (predatory or parasitoid)
- Microbial biopesticides (e.g., Bacillus thuringiensis)
- Beneficial nematodes
- Pheromone disruption
- Herbivorous insects for weed control

**Tier 4 — Allowed substances (National List only):**
- Use only if Tiers 1–3 are insufficient
- Documentation required: what was tried in Tiers 1–3 and why insufficient
- Application must follow label and use restrictions
- Monitoring to verify effectiveness
- National List substances include: neem, sulfur, copper fungicides, pyrethrin, rotenone, spinosad (with restrictions), and others
- See `ingredient_compliance.md` for complete National List

**Documentation requirements:**
- Field-level pest/weed/disease monitoring records
- Scouting reports (dates, observations, population levels)
- Control actions taken and dates applied
- Effectiveness monitoring
- Justification for use of National List substances

## 5. Livestock Standards (§205.236–§205.242)

### Origin of Livestock (§205.236)

**Dairy cattle (§205.236(a)):**
- Must be under organic management for **12 months prior** to production of milk/milk products sold as organic
- Applies to all dairy cattle in the herd
- One-time herd transition allowed (exception to ongoing 12-month requirement)
- **SOE clarification:** Post-SOE Rule, dairy herd transition is stricter; producers must demonstrate all dairy animals were under organic management

**Poultry (§205.236(b)(1)):**
- Must be under organic management from **second day of life**
- Applies to all poultry producing eggs or meat sold as organic
- Poultry acquired post-hatch: day 2 required
- Chicks from organic-source hatcheries: documentation required

**Breeder stock (§205.236(b)(2)):**
- May be brought from nonorganic source
- Once brought into organic operation, must be managed organically
- Offspring from breeder stock: organic from **last third of gestation**
- Documentation of genetic origin required

**Slaughter stock (§205.236(b)(3)):**
- Must be raised under organic management from **last third of gestation**
- Applies to livestock purchased for slaughter
- Exception: emergency/disaster situations may allow shorter timeframe (certifier approval required)

**Breeding record documentation:**
- Livestock genetics/lineage documentation
- Dates of animal acquisition
- Dates of transition to organic management
- Herd health records showing organic management

### Livestock Feed (§205.237)

**Feed requirement (§205.237(a)):**
- **100% organic feed** required for all organic livestock
- Includes all grazing/pasture feed
- Applies from date of birth (or day 2 for poultry) through slaughter/production

**Approved feed sources:**
- Organically certified pasture and forage
- Organically produced grains and legumes
- Organically produced hay
- Permitted supplements (vitamins, minerals, trace elements) per National List
- Water (must be potable/clean)

**Allowed non-organic inputs in feed (§205.237(c)):**
- Vitamins and minerals (from approved sources)
- Trace elements (cobalt, copper, iodine, iron, manganese, selenium, zinc)
- Vitamin/mineral additives that are nonsynthetic or synthetically produced but approved
- Salt and salt combinations

**Prohibited in livestock feed (§205.237(d)):**
- Animal drugs (antibiotics, growth promotants, parasiticides — except as noted below)
- Hormones (growth hormones, sex hormones, synthetic estrogens)
- Antibiotic growth promotants
- Plastic pellets or roughage replacements
- Urea (synthetic nitrogen)
- Mammalian or poultry slaughter byproducts fed to mammals or poultry (includes meat and bone meal, blood meal fed to ruminants prohibited; poultry byproducts prohibited for poultry)
- Feed formulas containing any prohibited substance
- GMO or GE feed ingredients

**Parasiticide exception (§205.237(d)):**
- Parasiticides **allowed only for breeder stock** and only as needed
- Prohibited for production animals (dairy cows producing milk, laying hens, breeding stock in production, slaughter stock)
- Use requires documentation and justification

**Feed transition:**
- Animals must transition to 100% organic feed when entering organic management
- Transition period: typical 30–90 days (certifier determines)
- During transition: increasing percentage of organic feed required
- Complete documentation of feed transition required

### Livestock Health Care (§205.238)

**Preventive health care (§205.238(a)):**
- **Primary requirement:** Preventive management practices
- Includes: adequate shelter, exercise, sanitation, good nutrition, fresh water, access to outdoors
- Reduces disease incidence and need for medications

**Vaccines (§205.238(b)(1)):**
- Allowed and recommended
- Does not remove animal from organic status
- Common vaccines: rabies, clostridium, brucellosis (for cattle), fowl pox (poultry)
- Documentation required in herd health records

**Parasiticide use (§205.238(b)(2)):**
- **Allowed ONLY for:**
  - Breeder stock (not in production)
  - Pasture animals with documented parasite load
- **Prohibited for:** Production animals (lactating dairy, laying hens), slaughter stock
- Use requires documentation: parasite identification, population levels, control measures attempted
- After parasiticide use: quarantine period before slaughter for meat (typically 3 weeks minimum, per certifier requirements)

**Antibiotics — absolute prohibition (§205.238(c)):**
- Antibiotics **never permitted** in organic livestock
- Any animal treated with antibiotics **cannot return to organic production**
- If antibiotic treatment necessary for animal health/welfare: animal must be removed from herd and sold as conventional
- Includes: penicillin, tetracycline, macrolides, aminoglycosides, fluoroquinolones
- Synthetic and nonsynthetic antibiotics both prohibited
- Documentation: herd health records must document no antibiotic use

**Synthetic substances (§205.238(d)):**
- Limited to those on National List
- Uses allowed: treatment of specific illnesses/conditions with veterinary prescription
- Examples: certain vitamins, minerals, limited therapeutics
- Prohibited: routine use of synthetic substances as growth promotants or feed additives
- Certifier approval may be required for specific uses

**Veterinary care:**
- Relationship with veterinarian required (for cattle operations)
- Treatment recommendations must be documented
- Veterinary records showing organic status compliance required

### Living Conditions (§205.239)

**Outdoor access requirement (§205.239(a)):**
- **All livestock must have access to outdoors** during growing season
- Temporary confinement allowed only for: documented severe weather, animal illness/injury, stage of production (young animals, immediately post-parturition)

**Required outdoor features:**
- Shade (trees, structures, or natural shelter)
- Shelter (protection from weather)
- Exercise areas (space for natural movement and behavior)
- Fresh air and direct sunlight exposure
- Clean water (accessible in outdoor areas)

**Year-round outdoor access (§205.239(b)):**
- Livestock must have access to outdoors **throughout the year**
- Climate considerations apply: in harsh winter climates, temporary indoor shelter may be used during extreme conditions
- Documentation of outdoor access schedule required

**Ruminant pasture requirement — Pasture Rule (§205.240):**

**120-day minimum grazing season:**
- Ruminants must have access to pasture during **minimum 120 consecutive days** of grazing season
- Timing: typically May–September (varies by region)
- May not be extended through supplemental feeding in confined areas

**30% dry matter intake from pasture (§205.240(a)(2)):**
- During grazing season: ruminants must obtain **minimum 30% of dry matter intake from pasture**
- Pasture definition: land from which livestock obtain forage/graze
- Excludes: hay, stored feed, grain supplements (these are counted toward the remaining 70%)
- Documentation: pasture availability, grazing records, feed records showing percentage pasture vs. supplemental feed
- Calculation method: typically estimated based on pasture quality, stocking density, season length, supplemental feed records

**Pasture management:**
- Pastures must be organically managed (no synthetic pesticides, fertilizers for 3 years)
- Adequate forage availability (certifier may require pasture assessment)
- Grazing management to prevent overuse/degradation
- Documentation: pasture maps, forage testing, grazing logs

**Confined feeding (§205.239(d)):**
- Temporary confinement allowed only for: extreme weather, illness, stage of production (newborn through weaning)
- "Temporary" = not primary housing, not routine
- Feedlot operations: not compatible with organic requirements for ruminants (must have pasture access)

### Organic Livestock and Poultry Standards (OLPS) Rule

**Publication and effective dates:**
- Published: November 2, 2023
- Effective date: January 2, 2024
- Phased implementation for certain provisions through 2024–2025

**Key new requirements:**

**Poultry outdoor access (§205.239):**
- Laying hens: minimum **2 square feet outdoor per bird** (in addition to indoor space)
- Broilers: minimum outdoor space requirement (standards vary by density)
- Turkeys: minimum outdoor space per bird
- **Meaningful access:** Poultry must have usable outdoor areas with soil access (not just porches or minimal runs)
- Ramps, pathways must facilitate actual outdoor use
- Shade/shelter/windbreaks must be provided in outdoor areas

**Stocking density limits:**
- Indoor housing: maximum birds per square foot of floor space (varies by species)
- Outdoor run space: minimum per-bird requirements (varies by species)
- Poultry houses: maximum 6 birds per square meter indoor (typical)
- Nesting boxes: 1 per 3–4 hens
- Roosting bars: minimum linear feet per bird

**Beef cattle and swine:**
- Outdoor access and pasture/exercise area requirements
- Space allowances for confinement facilities (if used temporarily)
- Documentation of outdoor access and stocking density compliance

**Transportation and slaughter:**
- Animals must be transported humanely
- Slaughter facilities must meet animal welfare standards
- Documentation of transport and slaughter practices
- Minimal stress handling protocols

**Records and documentation:**
- Stocking density records
- Outdoor access logs
- Poultry/livestock welfare monitoring records
- Veterinary care and health records

## 6. Handler Standards (§205.270–§205.272)

### Handling Requirements (§205.270)

**Processing methods (§205.270(a)):**
- Processing must use **mechanical or biological methods only**
- Allowed: grinding, mixing, heating (conventional), fermentation, precipitation, separation, pH adjustment
- Prohibited: ionizing radiation, chemical solvents (except for specific approved uses)

**Allowed substances in handling:**
- National List substances only (§205.270(b))
- Approved processing aids and adjuvants
- Approved antimicrobial agents (for food safety purposes, not preservation)
- Approved additives for specific functional purposes (texture, color, flavor, etc.)
- See `ingredient_compliance.md` for complete National List of allowed handling substances

**Prohibited in handling (§205.270(b)):**
- Ionizing radiation (§205.105(f)): absolutely prohibited; no exceptions
- Sewage sludge (§205.105(g)): prohibited as fertilizer or soil amendment
- Synthetic substances not on National List
- Genetic engineering / excluded methods
- Fumigants except as approved (sulfonyl fluoride for stored products, only pre-harvest)

**Commingling and contamination prevention (§205.270(d)):**
- Must ensure organic products not commingled with nonorganic products during handling
- Physical separation or temporal separation required
- Dedicated equipment or thoroughly cleaned equipment required
- See §205.272 for detailed requirements

### Facility Pest Management (§205.271)

**Tiered approach (§205.271(a)):**
Handler must follow this priority order:

**Tier 1 — Prevention:**
- Sanitation: cleaning production areas, removing debris, sealing cracks
- Waste management: proper disposal of byproducts, adequate trash removal
- Temperature and humidity control
- Storage rotation (FIFO — first in, first out)
- Facility maintenance: repair of structural damage, exclusion of pests

**Tier 2 — Mechanical/physical controls:**
- Traps (insect traps, rodent traps)
- Screens and exclusion barriers
- Lighting (to attract/repel pests)
- Removal of infested product
- Freezing or other thermal treatment to control pests

**Tier 3 — Biological controls:**
- Beneficial insects (parasitic wasps for pest insects)
- Predatory insects (ladybugs, lacewings for aphid control in stored products)
- Microbial controls if applicable

**Tier 4 — National List substances (§205.271(c)):**
- Use only if Tiers 1–3 insufficient
- Allowed pesticides include: sulfur, diatomaceous earth (food-grade), botanicals (pyrethrin, neem), approved microbial controls
- Prohibited: synthetic pesticides, fumigants (except as noted above)
- Restrictions apply: must not contact organic product directly; used in traps or perimeter treatment only

**Lure and repel devices (§205.271(b)(2)):**
- Pheromone traps: allowed if do not contact organic product
- Electronic rodent repellent devices: allowed
- Light traps: allowed if organic product cannot be exposed

**Mandated pesticide application:**
- If pesticide application required by federal, state, or local authority (mandatory pest eradication program):
  - Affected product **loses organic status**
  - Product cannot be sold as organic
  - Must be segregated and labeled accordingly
  - Documentation of mandatory application required

**Documentation:**
- Pest monitoring records (frequency, observations, identification)
- Control measures attempted and results
- National List substances used, dates, locations, application rates
- Effectiveness monitoring

### Commingling and Contamination Prevention (§205.272)

**Physical segregation requirement (§205.272(a)):**
- **Organic and nonorganic products must be physically separated**
- Separate storage, production areas, or temporal separation (completely separate time periods)
- Clear identification and labeling of organic product areas
- Access controls to prevent mixing

**Contamination prevention measures (§205.272(b)):**
- Must maintain adequate measures to prevent contact with prohibited substances
- Documentation of measures required

**Equipment (§205.272(b)):**
- Dedicated organic equipment: ideal; eliminates need for cleaning validation
- Shared equipment: allowed if:
  - Thoroughly cleaned between organic and nonorganic production runs
  - Cleaning validation documentation (residue testing or approved cleaning protocol)
  - Scheduling ensures no cross-contamination
  - Records of cleaning date, time, method, person responsible

**Documentation requirements:**
- List of equipment used for organic production
- Cleaning procedures (if equipment shared)
- Cleaning log with dates, times, persons, results
- Segregation procedures and access controls
- Production scheduling to minimize cross-contact risk
- Records of any cross-contact incidents and corrective actions

**Parallel production (same facility, organic and nonorganic lines):**
- Allowed but requires heightened scrutiny
- Must include: dedicated equipment or validated cleaning, temporal separation, physical barriers, access controls, personnel designation
- Certifier audit includes verification of segregation measures
- Certifier may apply additional inspection frequency
- Higher risk for noncompliance findings; documented compliance essential

## 7. Wild-Crop Harvesting (§205.207)

### Land Eligibility (§205.207(a))

**Three-year requirement:**
- Land where wild crops are harvested must have **no prohibited substances applied for 3 years** prior to harvest
- "Prohibited substances" = same definition as for cultivated crops
- Difficult to document in wild/public lands; certifier must verify through historical research, land management records, etc.

### Harvesting Requirements (§205.207(b))

**Sustainability requirement:**
- Harvesting must not be **destructive to the environment**
- Harvesting rate must not exceed **sustainable harvest level** for that ecosystem
- Examples: plant regrowth intact, root systems preserved, long-term viability of species maintained

**Site designation and mapping (§205.207(c)):**
- Each harvesting site must be **specifically identified** in the OSP
- **Mapped** with GPS coordinates or physical landmarks
- Documentation: photographs, maps, boundary descriptions
- Prevents confusion with excluded or prohibited land

### OSP Documentation

**Required for wild-crop harvesting:**
1. Identification of all harvesting sites
2. Historical land use documentation (3-year verification)
3. Sustainability plan (harvest rates, monitoring, regeneration verification)
4. Maps and site descriptions
5. Evidence of sustainable harvest (monitoring records, environmental assessment)
6. Certifier approval of each site

**Examples of wild crops:**
- Wildcrafted herbs (ginseng, goldenseal, ramp)
- Wild berries (blueberries, mushrooms)
- Nuts (black walnut, hickory)
- Forest products (bark, roots, medicinal plants)

## 8. Allowed and Prohibited Substances

### Reference
See dedicated file: **`ingredient_compliance.md`** for:
- Complete National List of Allowed Substances (7 CFR §205.601–§205.603)
- Synthetic substances allowed for crop production (§205.601)
- Synthetic substances allowed for handling (§205.603)
- Nonsynthetic substances prohibited (§205.602)
- Substance use restrictions, exemptions, and conditions

### General Principles for Substance Allowance (§205.600)

For any substance to be on the National List and allowed:

1. **Safety:** Substance must not be harmful to human health or the environment
   - Risk assessment conducted
   - Toxicity data reviewed
   - Environmental fate evaluated

2. **Necessity:** Substance must be **essential for organic production/handling**
   - No organic alternative available
   - Necessity documented and justified

3. **Consistency:** Use must be **consistent with organic handling/production principles**
   - Aligns with sustainable practices
   - Minimizes synthetic inputs

4. **Compliance with use restrictions:** Substance must be used per National List restrictions
   - Application rate limits
   - Timing restrictions
   - Crop/use restrictions
   - Frequency limits

### Key Prohibited Categories

**Synthetic substances (§205.605(b)):**
- Synthetic substances prohibited unless specifically listed on National List
- "Synthetic" = produced by chemical synthesis; not derived from natural sources without significant modification
- Burden on producer/handler to demonstrate substance is on approved list or nonsynthetic

**Ionizing radiation (§205.105(f)):**
- **Absolutely prohibited for all organic production and handling**
- No exceptions
- Includes: gamma radiation, X-ray, electron beam, irradiation (all types)
- Purpose irrelevant; any irradiation disqualifies product

**Sewage sludge (§205.105(g)):**
- Biosolids or sewage sludge from municipal wastewater treatment prohibited
- Cannot be used as fertilizer or soil amendment
- Nonorganic manure from conventional livestock allowed with restrictions (age/composting)
- Sewage sludge-derived products prohibited

**Genetic engineering / excluded methods (§205.105(e), §205.2):**
- Products of excluded methods prohibited
- "Excluded methods" definition (§205.2): techniques not possible under natural conditions:
  - Recombinant DNA technology / genetic engineering
  - Cell fusion (except traditionally used fusion techniques like hybridization)
  - Microencapsulation and macroencapsulation
- **Does NOT include:** traditional breeding, hybridization, conjugation, fermentation, in vitro fertilization, tissue culture
- Detection: no approved test method; rely on product certification, non-GMO verification, seed source documentation

## 9. Organic Transition

### Producer Transition (Crops)

**Three-year transition requirement (§205.202):**
- Land must have **no prohibited substances applied for 36 months prior to harvest**
- Effective date: 36 months before anticipated harvest of first organic crop
- Transition clock: starts when last prohibited substance was applied

**"Transitional" designation:**
- NOP does NOT recognize "transitional" or "in transition" as a label claim
- Product grown during transition cannot be labeled "organic"
- Some private certification programs (e.g., some state programs) use "transitional" label, but not USDA-approved
- Transitional product typically sells as conventional (lower price)
- Upon completion of 3 years: becomes eligible for organic certification

**Documentation during transition:**
- Field history: previous ownership, crops, uses
- Input records: all substances applied (fertilizers, pesticides, growth regulators)
- Affidavits from previous operators/tenants
- Maps and aerial photos showing field boundaries
- Neighboring land use (potential contamination sources)
- Buffer zone documentation
- All documentation must cover entire 3-year period

**Flexibility in transition:**
- **Field-by-field transition:** Can transition individual fields while other fields remain nonorganic (common in split operations)
- Transition timeline staggered by field
- Different fields can complete transition at different times
- Entire farm does NOT need to transition simultaneously

**Record retention:**
- Transition records must be maintained for 5 years after certification granted
- Helps prove continuous compliance in subsequent years

### Handler Transition

**No transition period required:**
- Handlers can begin producing organic products immediately upon certification
- Handler facility does not need "transition" period
- But: **ALL ingredients must be certified organic** (or compliant with §205.606) before any organic claim made

**Initial audit requirements:**
- Certifier conducts detailed initial inspection
- Reviews all input sources and supplier certifications
- Verifies cleaning/segregation procedures
- Verifies OSP compliance
- May request extended observation period for first production batch
- All first-season documentation reviewed carefully before certification granted

### Livestock Transition

**Dairy cattle (§205.236(a)):**
- **One-time herd transition allowed:**
  - Entire distinct dairy herd must be under organic management for 12 months minimum
  - "Distinct herd" = all dairy animals on operation
  - Milk produced after 12-month transition period becomes organic
  - Subsequent dairy purchases: must already be organically managed (no transition for new purchases)
  - **SOE clarification:** This transition only applies to the initial organic certification; subsequent additions to herd must already be organic

**Poultry and other livestock:**
- No transition period
- Animals must be organic from required date (day 2 for poultry, last third gestation for others)
- No exceptions or transition provisions

## 10. Certification Process (§205.400–§205.406)

### Application (§205.401)

**Submission requirements:**
1. **Apply to USDA-accredited certifying agent** of operation's choice
   - Certifier must be accredited by USDA for operation's category (crop, livestock, handler, multi-category)
   - Certifier must cover operation's geographic location (if applicable)

2. **Include with application:**
   - Completed application form (provided by certifier)
   - Organic System Plan (OSP) — comprehensive document per §205.201
   - Documentation of previous organic certifications (if any)
   - All supporting documentation: field history, input records, livestock records, etc.
   - Applicable fees per certifier's fee schedule

3. **Initial completeness review:**
   - Certifier reviews application for completeness
   - May request additional information within specified timeframe (typically 30 days)
   - Applicant must provide requested information or application may be denied

### Review and Inspection (§205.402–§205.403)

**Certifier's preliminary review (§205.402):**
1. Certifier reviews application and OSP for compliance with NOP standards
2. Identifies any obvious deficiencies or areas requiring clarification
3. Requests additional information if needed (per §205.401(d))
4. Determines necessity of on-site inspection

**Mandatory on-site inspection (§205.403(a)):**
1. Certifier conducts on-site inspection of operation **before certification granted**
2. Inspection must cover: production areas, facilities, equipment, records, storage, field boundaries
3. Inspector must be qualified and trained (certifier responsibility)
4. Unannounced inspections may occur (post-SOE: certifier required to conduct minimum 5% unannounced annually)

**Inspection report (§205.403(b)):**
1. Inspector prepares written inspection report
2. Includes: findings, observations, compliance determinations, recommendations
3. Report submitted to certifier for review
4. Applicant may submit response to inspection findings within specified period

**Certifier's decision process:**
1. Reviews inspection report
2. Compares findings to NOP standards
3. Considers applicant's response to inspection report
4. Makes final determination: certification granted, denied, or conditional

### Granting Certification (§205.404)

**Certification decision (§205.404(a)):**
- If compliant with all NOP standards: **certification granted**
- Certifier issues **Organic Certificate** with:
  - Operation name and address
  - Certification effective date (typically date of initial inspection, or date all conditions met)
  - Categories certified: crop production, livestock, handling, wild-crop harvesting, combinations
  - Specific products or product categories certified
  - Certifying agent name and accreditation number
  - Certificate duration: **valid for one year** from effective date

**Certificate use:**
- Certificate must be displayed at operation and/or made available to buyers
- Copy provided to operation
- Copy maintained in certifier's records
- SOE requirement: Certifier must issue NOP Import Certificates for imported organic products (7 CFR §205.405(e), as amended)

**Conditions on certification:**
- May be issued with corrective action requests (CAR) or minor findings
- Operation must correct within specified timeframe (typically 30–180 days)
- Upon correction verification: full certificate issued
- Failure to correct: certification may be suspended or denied

### Denial of Certification (§205.405)

**Grounds for denial (§205.405(a)):**
- Operation not in compliance with NOP standards
- Operation does not maintain adequate OSP
- Certifier cannot verify compliance with Organic Foods Production Act or NOP regulations
- Applicant willfully violates OFPA

**Denial procedure (§205.405(b)):**
1. Certifier provides **written notice of proposed denial**
2. Notice includes: reason(s) for denial, specific noncompliance findings, proposed corrective actions (if applicable)
3. Applicant has **right to respond** within specified period (minimum 30 days)
4. Applicant may: correct deficiencies, provide rebutting information, request reconsideration
5. Certifier reviews response
6. Certifier makes **final determination:** certification granted (if compliant), or denial final

**Appeal rights:**
- Applicant may appeal denial to USDA (see §205.680–§205.682)
- Appeal must be filed within specified timeframe
- Administrative hearing available per 7 CFR Part 1, Subpart H

### Continuation of Certification (§205.406)

**Annual recertification requirements (§205.406(a)):**

1. **Updated OSP submission (§205.406(d)):**
   - Applicant must submit updated OSP annually
   - Updates must reflect any changes to operation: new fields, new crops, new livestock, new inputs, new facilities, changes to practices
   - Certifier reviews updated OSP for completeness and compliance

2. **Annual on-site inspection:**
   - Mandatory for all certified operations
   - Certifier conducts inspection at operation (announced or unannounced)
   - Same scope as initial inspection: facilities, records, field conditions, livestock, inputs, practices
   - **SOE requirement (§205.406(a)):** Certifier must conduct minimum 5% unannounced inspections annually (increased from previous standards)

3. **Fees:**
   - Annual recertification fees charged by certifier
   - Fees vary by certifier; typically based on operation size, complexity, or flat rate
   - Fees must be paid before certification renewed

4. **Immediate notification requirement (§205.406(b)):**
   - Applicant must notify certifier **immediately** of any changes to OSP
   - Changes triggering notification: new inputs, new fields, new livestock, facility changes, practice changes, ownership changes
   - Changes that affect organic integrity must be approved before implementation
   - Some changes may require inspection before approval

5. **Certificate renewal (§205.406(e)):**
   - If operation remains compliant: certificate renewed for another year
   - New certificate issued with effective date
   - Continuous coverage required (no gap in certification allowed if continuous operations)

**Circumstances that may affect recertification:**
- Compliance issues identified in annual inspection
- Incomplete records or documentation
- Unapproved use of prohibited substances
- Commingling or contamination incidents
- Changes to OSP not approved

## 11. Recordkeeping Requirements (§205.103)

### Record Retention Period (§205.103(a))

- **Minimum 5 years** from date of creation
- Applies to all records documenting compliance with OFPA and NOP regulations
- Certifier may request older records for investigation purposes
- Upon sale of operation: subsequent owner typically inherits record-keeping obligation

### Record Accessibility (§205.103(b))

- Records must be **accessible to:**
  - USDA AMS personnel (NOP oversight)
  - Certifying agent representatives (audits)
  - State organic program representatives (state oversight where applicable)
- Records must be provided upon request
- Access may be remote (digital) or on-site
- Operation may not withhold records based on confidentiality claims (certifier has rights to information for compliance)

### Required Record Categories (§205.103)

**For all operations:**

1. **Purchase and sales records:**
   - Supplier names, addresses, dates, products, quantities
   - Organic certificates of suppliers
   - Customer names, addresses, products sold, quantities, prices
   - Organic claims made on sales documentation
   - Invoices showing organic designation

2. **OSP documentation:**
   - Current OSP (updated annually)
   - Historical OSPs (previous years' plans)
   - Organic certification certificates (own and all suppliers')
   - Certifier correspondence and inspection reports

3. **Input records:**
   - All substances applied to crops, livestock, or facilities
   - Supplier documentation: receipts, labels, certificates of origin
   - Application dates, rates, locations, person applying
   - National List verification documentation
   - Documentation of approved sources (certified organic, approved nonsynthetic)

4. **Production records:**
   - Field/facility identification and maps
   - Crop variety/livestock breed information
   - Planting and harvest dates, yields
   - Livestock birth dates, origins, health records
   - Processing records (date, time, amount, method, ingredients, controls)
   - Production notes and observations

5. **Commingling and contamination prevention records:**
   - Documentation of segregation procedures
   - Equipment cleaning logs and validation
   - Incident reports (if any cross-contact occurred)
   - Scheduling records showing temporal separation (if used)
   - Supplier certification confirmation

6. **Monitoring and inspection records:**
   - Pest/disease monitoring logs
   - Soil testing results
   - Pasture assessment records
   - Feed testing (if applicable)
   - Equipment maintenance records

7. **Certifier correspondence:**
   - Certification applications and copies
   - Certifier inspection reports
   - Copies of organic certificates
   - Corrective action requests (CARs) and responses
   - Surveillance letters or inquiries
   - Appeals or disputes documentation

**For producers specifically:**

8. **Soil and land management:**
   - 3-year land history (transition documentation)
   - Soil testing/analysis results
   - Nutrient management records
   - Crop rotation records and maps
   - Cover crop documentation
   - Composting records (temperature, timing, materials)
   - Manure source documentation and age/composting verification

9. **Seed and planting stock documentation:**
   - Seed source and organic certification
   - Documentation of nonorganic seed use (unavailability justification)
   - Seed treatment verification
   - Propagating material records

10. **Buffer zone documentation:**
    - Maps showing adjacent land and buffer zones
    - Documentation of neighboring pesticide/fertilizer use (inquiries, notifications)
    - Buffer zone maintenance records

**For handlers specifically:**

11. **Supplier certification records:**
    - Copy of each supplier's organic certificate
    - Verification of suppliers' certifier accreditation
    - SOE requirement: documented verification of suppliers' organic status at purchase

12. **Ingredient tracking and mass balance:**
    - Complete ingredient lists with organic status
    - Ingredient quantity records (input vs. output)
    - Processing yield documentation
    - Lot/batch traceability records

13. **Cleaning and sanitation records:**
    - Cleaning procedures for shared equipment
    - Cleaning logs with dates, times, methods, results
    - Validation documentation (residue testing, approved protocols)
    - Sanitation frequency logs

**SOE additions to all record types:**

14. **Supply chain traceability records:**
    - Documentation of all inputs' organic source
    - Certifier identification for all suppliers
    - Import certificate copies (for imported products)
    - Verification activities and results

15. **Fraud prevention and oversight:**
    - Documentation of supplier audits/verifications
    - Investigation records (if discrepancies found)
    - High-risk input monitoring (if applicable)
    - Corrective actions implemented

### Record Format and Organization

- Records may be paper or electronic
- Electronic records must be backed up and protected from unauthorized modification
- Records must be organized and retrievable (labels, indices, cross-references)
- Operation should maintain clear filing system
- Certifier must be able to locate and review records during audit

## 12. Compliance and Enforcement (§205.660–§205.665)

### Noncompliance Procedures (§205.662)

**Identification of noncompliance:**
- Certifier discovers noncompliance through: annual inspection, complaint investigation, document review, unannounced inspection
- Noncompliance = violation of OFPA or NOP regulations; operation not meeting standards

**Noncompliance notice (§205.662(a)):**
1. Certifier provides **written notice** to operation
2. Notice includes:
   - Description of specific noncompliance
   - NOP regulation(s) violated
   - What action(s) caused noncompliance
   - Potential consequence (suspension or revocation)

3. Operation has **right to respond** within specified period (typically 30 days)
   - May provide evidence showing compliance
   - May propose corrective action plan
   - May rebut certifier's findings

**Resolution procedures (§205.662(b)):**

**If operation corrects noncompliance:**
- Certifier verifies correction within specified timeframe
- Certifier may require inspection to confirm
- Certification continues; incident documented in file
- Further violations may lead to escalated enforcement

**If operation fails to correct:**
- Certifier proposes **suspension** or **revocation**
- Issues written notice with specific actions required
- Operation has right to hearing/appeal (see §205.680–§205.682)

### Suspension and Revocation (§205.662(c)–(d))

**Suspension (temporary):**
- Operation loses right to sell/label as organic
- Ongoing production allowed but cannot be sold as organic
- Suspension period specified (typically 30–90 days)
- Operation must correct noncompliance
- Certifier verifies correction
- Upon correction: certification restored
- Products produced during suspension may be relabeled and sold as conventional

**Revocation (permanent for specified period):**
- Operation loses organic certification
- Cannot sell/label any products as organic
- Cannot be recertified for **minimum 5 years** after revocation date
- Reason for revocation: serious or willful violations

**Examples triggering suspension/revocation:**
- Continued use of prohibited substances without correction
- Repeated false records or fraud
- Failure to cooperate with certifier
- Commingling or contamination (severe cases)
- Antibiotic treatment of livestock
- Unapproved changes to operation affecting organic integrity
- Repeated violations of same regulation

### Willful Violation Penalties (OFPA §6519(c))

**Civil penalties:**
- Willful violation: **up to $11,000 per violation**
- Violations may be per incident or per product affected
- Enforced by USDA AMS or law enforcement
- Separate from certification suspension/revocation

**Examples of willful violations:**
- Knowingly applying prohibited substances
- Falsifying records
- Fraudulently selling nonorganic products as organic
- Deliberately misrepresenting certification status
- Importing products without compliance

### Mediation (§205.663)

- **Optional alternative to enforcement proceedings**
- Certifier and operation may request mediation
- Neutral third party facilitates resolution
- Applicable to certain disputes (not all noncompliance)
- If successful: agreement may resolve noncompliance without suspension

## 13. Appeals Process (§205.680–§205.682)

### Right to Appeal (§205.681)

Operations may appeal the following adverse actions:

1. **Denial of certification** (§205.681(a))
   - Appeal of proposed or final denial of organic certification
   - Deadline: within timeframe specified by certifier (minimum 30 days from notice)

2. **Proposed suspension of certification** (§205.681(b))
   - Appeal of proposed suspension for noncompliance
   - Deadline: within timeframe specified by certifier

3. **Proposed revocation of certification** (§205.681(c))
   - Appeal of proposed revocation (permanent loss of certification)
   - Deadline: within timeframe specified by certifier

### Appeal Procedure (§205.680)

**Appeal to certifying agent:**
1. Operation submits written appeal to certifier
2. Appeal must state: reason for appeal, supporting documentation, new information/evidence
3. Certifier reviews appeal
4. Certifier may: sustain appeal (reverse decision), deny appeal (uphold decision), or request additional information

**Appeal to USDA (§205.680(b)):**
- If operation dissatisfied with certifier's appeal decision:
  - May appeal certifier's decision to USDA AMS
  - Appeal must be filed within specified timeframe (typically 30–60 days)
  - USDA reviews certifier's decision for adherence to NOP regulations

### Administrative Hearing (§205.682)

**Hearing availability:**
- Operations may request **administrative hearing** per 7 CFR Part 1, Subpart H
- Hearing held before USDA administrative judge (independent from certifier and USDA decision-maker)
- Operation may present evidence, witnesses, legal representation

**Hearing scope:**
- Review of facts and certifier's legal determinations
- Operation may challenge certifier's findings
- USDA may present evidence supporting certifier
- Hearing transcript becomes part of record

**Hearing decision:**
- Administrative judge issues initial decision
- Parties may file exceptions/appeals
- USDA official issues final decision
- Decision binding on certifier and operation

### Mediation Alternative (§205.663)

- Certifier and operation may agree to mediation instead of formal appeal
- Mediation goals: resolve dispute without formal hearing, maintain relationships
- If successful: mediated agreement may resolve dispute faster than appeals
- If unsuccessful: formal appeal rights remain available

## 14. International Equivalency Arrangements

### Equivalency Framework

**Definition:**
- Equivalency = mutual recognition between USDA NOP and another country's organic standards
- Allows organic products certified under one standard to be sold in the other country
- **Does NOT mean identical standards**; each arrangement has specific scope and exceptions

### Current Equivalency Arrangements

**Status note:** Equivalency arrangements evolve and should be verified with USDA AMS for current details. Below reflects arrangements as of 2024–2025.

#### Canada (CFIA Canadian Organic Regime)

**Scope:**
- Full equivalency arrangement (oldest and most comprehensive)
- Covers organic crops, livestock products, processed products

**Key points:**
- Products certified as organic under NOP may be sold as organic in Canada
- Canadian-certified products may be sold as organic in the US
- Equivalency includes livestock standards, handling, and labeling

**Exceptions:**
- Some substances allowed under NOP are not permitted under Canadian standards
- Importers must ensure compliance with both standards (use most stringent)
- Separate Canadian organic certificate may be required for export

#### European Union

**Scope:**
- Recognition arrangement (not full equivalency)
- Covers: unprocessed plant products, processed agricultural products for food use, vegetative propagating material, seeds
- Limited to specific product categories

**Key points:**
- EU recognizes NOP as equivalent for listed categories
- US-certified products can be exported to EU as organic
- Restrictions: some inputs allowed in US not recognized in EU (e.g., certain additives, processing aids)
- Products must be dual-compliant if using substances allowed in NOP but not EU

**Certificate requirements:**
- US certifier must be approved for EU exports
- May require separate EU organic certificate (Certificate of Inspection) in addition to NOP certificate
- Documentation requirements for exports include traceability and substance verification

#### Japan (JAS — Japanese Agricultural Standards)

**Scope:**
- Equivalency arrangement with specified organic products
- Covers crops and some processed products

**Key points:**
- NOP-certified products may be imported to Japan as organic
- Japan-certified products may be imported to US as organic
- Scope limited to specific product categories

**Requirements:**
- Certifier must be approved for Japan exports
- Some JAS requirements may differ from NOP; dual compliance may be needed
- Import documentation and certificates required

#### South Korea (NAQS — National Agricultural Products Quality Management Service)

**Scope:**
- Equivalency arrangement for organic processed foods
- Covers specific categories of processed products

**Requirements:**
- Certifier approval required for Korean exports
- Documentation and traceability requirements

#### Switzerland

**Scope:**
- Separate equivalency arrangement
- Covers organic products meeting both NOP and Swiss organic standards

#### Taiwan

**Scope:**
- Equivalency arrangement for specified organic products
- Imports and exports covered

#### United Kingdom (Post-Brexit)

**Scope:**
- Separate arrangement following UK departure from EU
- Covers organic products

### Export and Import Considerations

**For US exporters (sending organic products abroad):**

1. **Certifier approval:**
   - Certifier must be accredited by USDA for international scope
   - Certifier must be approved in destination country's system (if applicable)
   - Some certifiers specialize in specific country/international exports

2. **Equivalency scope:**
   - Not all products covered by equivalency; verify product eligibility
   - Some countries recognize only specific product categories
   - Certifier advises on destination country requirements

3. **Dual compliance:**
   - May need to comply with BOTH NOP and destination country standards
   - Use more stringent requirement (e.g., if US allows substance X but destination doesn't, don't use it)
   - Substance verification critical

4. **Export documentation:**
   - NOP organic certificate (standard)
   - SOE requirement: NOP Import Certificate (for destination country receipt)
   - Country-specific certificates (if required)
   - Traceability documentation
   - Ingredient verification documentation

5. **Labeling:**
   - Must comply with destination country labeling laws
   - NOP label claims must not conflict with destination country label requirements
   - Some countries require local language labeling
   - Organic claim substantiation required

**For US importers (bringing organic products into US):**

1. **SOE requirement — NOP Import Certificate (§205.405(e)):**
   - **All organic products imported into US must have NOP Import Certificate**
   - Effective March 20, 2023 (SOE Rule)
   - Foreign certifier or customs broker obtains certificate
   - Certificate confirms product compliance with NOP standards
   - Certificate accompanies import documentation

2. **Certifier equivalency review:**
   - US certifier for importer must verify foreign certifier's accreditation
   - If foreign certifier is in equivalency country: accreditation presumed valid
   - If foreign certifier in non-equivalency country: requires USDA AMS review and approval

3. **Documentation required:**
   - NOP Import Certificate
   - Foreign organic certificate
   - Importer certifier's assessment of foreign certifier equivalency (if needed)
   - Traceability documentation showing product authenticity
   - Ingredient verification (for processed products)

4. **Non-equivalency country imports:**
   - Products from countries without equivalency arrangement may be imported as organic
   - Requires special review and approval process
   - Foreign certifier must be USDA-accredited (or accreditation request process)
   - More stringent documentation required
   - Increased inspection and verification burden

### Key Export/Import Principles

**Equivalency scope limitations:**
- Equivalency applies only to listed product categories
- Not all products qualify; certifier confirms eligibility
- Substances on one country's approved list may not be recognized in other

**Dual certification:**
- Some operations obtain certification from both NOP certifier AND destination country certifier
- Ensures unambiguous compliance
- Typical for products exported to multiple countries with different standards

**Substance verification is critical:**
- Input review: ensure all inputs (fertilizers, pesticides, additives, processing aids) comply with BOTH standards
- Destination country restrictions may be more stringent than NOP
- Processor/handler must document that all inputs are acceptable in destination country

**Export certificate documentation:**
- NOP certificate alone insufficient for international export
- Destination country-specific certificates often required
- Importer/buyer requests specific documentation per destination country requirements
- Customs clearance requires proper documentation
