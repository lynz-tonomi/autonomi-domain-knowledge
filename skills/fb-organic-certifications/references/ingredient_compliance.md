## 1. National List Overview

The National List of Allowed and Prohibited Substances (7 CFR §§205.600–205.607) is the regulatory mechanism that determines which non-organic substances may be used in organic handling and production. It is maintained by the USDA National Organic Program, with recommendations from the National Organic Standards Board (NOSB).

### Structure
- **§205.600:** General evaluation criteria for substances on the National List
- **§205.601:** Synthetic substances allowed for use in organic crop production
- **§205.602:** Nonsynthetic substances prohibited in organic crop production
- **§205.603:** Synthetic substances allowed for use in organic livestock production
- **§205.604:** Nonsynthetic substances prohibited in organic livestock production
- **§205.605:** Nonagricultural (nonorganic) substances allowed as ingredients in or on processed products labeled as "organic" or "made with organic"
  - (a) Nonsynthetics allowed
  - (b) Synthetics allowed
- **§205.606:** Nonorganically produced agricultural products allowed as ingredients in products labeled as "organic" (only when commercially unavailable in organic form)

### Key Principles
- Organic products must contain at least 95% organic ingredients (by weight, excluding water and salt) to bear the "organic" label
- The remaining 5% must be either:
  - Certified organic, OR
  - On the National List (§205.605 or §205.606), OR
  - A nonagricultural substance not on the National List that is not prohibited (very limited application)
- Processing aids must also comply with the National List
- Water and salt are excluded from the organic percentage calculation

- Citric acid appears in §205.605(a) (nonsynthetic, produced by microbial fermentation) — this is the most commonly used form. Synthetic citric acid is NOT listed and therefore NOT allowed.

- Xanthan gum is synthetic but allowed without restriction — very common in beverages and sauces.
- Carrageenan is nonsynthetic under §205.605(a) — NOSB has reviewed it multiple times and it remains on the list (controversial in consumer perception but currently NOP-compliant).

## 5. OMRI (Organic Materials Review Institute)

### What OMRI Is
- Independent nonprofit organization (Eugene, OR) that reviews products against NOP standards
- Maintains the OMRI Products List — a directory of products (brand name products, not generic substances) evaluated for compliance with organic standards
- OMRI reviews include analysis of ingredients, manufacturing processes, and product labels

### What OMRI Listing Means
- The product has been independently reviewed and found to comply with USDA NOP standards for the listed use category
- OMRI listing is NOT a guarantee of NOP compliance — the certifying agent makes the final compliance determination
- OMRI listing is voluntary — many NOP-compliant products are not OMRI-listed (especially commodity chemicals)

### What OMRI Listing Does NOT Mean
- It is NOT a certification of the product
- It does NOT replace the certifying agent's review
- It does NOT mean the substance is on the National List (OMRI may list products that contain National List substances in compliant formulations)
- OMRI-listed products can still be rejected by a certifying agent if they determine the specific use is non-compliant

### How to Use OMRI in Practice
1. Check OMRI Products List (omri.org) for brand-name products you plan to use
2. If the product is OMRI-listed for your use category, include the OMRI listing in your OSP documentation
3. If the product is NOT OMRI-listed, you need to conduct your own compliance determination (review the ingredients against the National List) and submit to your certifying agent for review
4. OMRI listing streamlines the certifier review process but does not eliminate it

### OMRI Categories
- Crop Inputs (fertilizers, pest management, etc.)
- Livestock Inputs
- Processing/Handling Inputs (most relevant for F&B manufacturers)

## 6. Commercial Unavailability Determinations (§205.606 context)

### When Commercial Unavailability Applies
If you want to use a nonorganic agricultural ingredient in an "organic" product (the ≤5% slot), AND that ingredient appears on §205.606, you must demonstrate that the organic form is commercially unavailable.

### What "Commercially Unavailable" Means (§205.2)
The condition of not being able to obtain a production input in an appropriate form, quality, or quantity to fulfill an essential function in a system of organic production or handling, as determined by the certifying agent.

### How to Document Commercial Unavailability
1. Contact a minimum of 3 organic suppliers (some certifiers require more)
2. Document each contact: date, supplier name, contact person, result (unavailable, insufficient quality, insufficient quantity, wrong form, price not a valid reason for unavailability)
3. Retain documentation: emails, letters, quotes, purchase orders showing attempts
4. Submit to certifying agent as part of OSP
5. Must be re-verified each year (or more frequently, per certifying agent)

### What Does NOT Constitute Commercial Unavailability
- **Price:** The organic version is more expensive — this is NOT grounds for unavailability. (Controversial and debated, but NOP guidance is clear: price alone does not constitute unavailability.)
- **Convenience:** The organic version requires more effort to source
- **Preference:** The nonorganic version has preferred qualities (unless truly a quality issue that makes the organic version non-functional)

### Common Commercial Unavailability Scenarios in F&B
- Certain specialty colors (organic versions truly not available in sufficient quantity/quality)
- Specific gum or hydrocolloid forms/grades
- Certain starches in specific functional forms
- Certain flavoring ingredients where organic supply chain is immature

## 7. Substance Evaluation Process and Petitions

### How Substances Get on (or Off) the National List
1. **Petition:** Any person may petition NOSB to add, amend, or remove a substance
2. **NOSB Review:** NOSB's Handling Subcommittee (or relevant subcommittee) evaluates the petition using criteria in §205.600:
   - Effect on human health
   - Effect on the environment
   - Essentiality (is it necessary for organic handling?)
   - Compatibility with organic handling practices
   - Alternatives available
3. **Technical Review (TR):** NOSB requests a Technical Review from NOP — a detailed scientific assessment of the substance
4. **Public Comment:** NOSB opens public comment period
5. **NOSB Vote:** Two-thirds majority required to recommend addition/removal
6. **USDA Rulemaking:** If NOSB recommends, USDA/AMS initiates rulemaking (proposed rule, public comment, final rule)
7. **Publication:** Final rule published in Federal Register; CFR updated

### Sunset Review Process
- Every substance on the National List undergoes "sunset review" every 5 years
- NOSB reviews whether the substance should remain on the list
- Two-thirds vote of NOSB required to REMOVE a substance (previously required to renew — changed in 2013)
- This means substances stay on the list by default unless NOSB actively votes to remove

### Timeline
- Petition to final rule: typically 2-5 years (sometimes longer)
- Sunset review cycle: 5 years for each substance

## 8. Common Compliance Challenges for F&B Manufacturers

### Challenge 1: Natural Flavors
- Natural flavors must be nonsynthetic per 21 CFR 101.22 definition
- Flavor carriers and solvents must also comply — propylene glycol (synthetic, NOT on National List) is a common non-compliant carrier
- Compliant carriers: ethanol (organic or conventional), water, organic oil bases
- Organic flavors preferred; conventional natural flavors allowed in the ≤5% if no synthetic components
- Documentation: full ingredient breakdown from flavor supplier required (this is often resisted by flavor houses citing proprietary formulations — certifier may require it)

### Challenge 2: Processing Aids
- Processing aids that contact organic product must comply with National List
- Common problem: cleaning agents used on food-contact surfaces — must comply with §205.605 (e.g., chlorine materials must be below Safe Drinking Water Act residual levels)
- Boiler water additives, lubricants on equipment, and sanitizers all need review
- "Incidental additives" (21 CFR 101.100) are a gray area — certifiers vary in interpretation

### Challenge 3: Vitamins and Minerals for Fortification
- §205.605(b) allows nutrient vitamins and minerals in accordance with 21 CFR 104.20
- This limits fortification to amounts consistent with FDA nutritional quality guidelines
- Cannot add vitamins/minerals beyond what 21 CFR 104.20 allows and still claim organic
- Common confusion: companies want to fortify beyond FDA guidelines — this may disqualify organic claim

### Challenge 4: Enzymes and Microbial Cultures
- Enzymes from plants, fungi, or bacteria: allowed under §205.605(a) if non-GE
- The GE issue is critical: many commercial enzymes are produced via genetically engineered microorganisms
- Must verify non-GE status with enzyme supplier
- Cultures: food-grade microorganisms allowed, must be non-GE

### Challenge 5: Co-Packing and Ingredient Provenance
- If your co-packer handles both organic and conventional products, risk of commingling is elevated
- Co-packer must have their own organic certification (or operate under your certification scope — complex arrangement)
- Ingredient provenance: every organic ingredient must have traceable chain of custody back to a certified organic operation
- SOE rule strengthened these requirements significantly — import fraud prevention measures, supply chain traceability

### Challenge 6: Label Claims vs. Certification Category
- Companies frequently miscalculate their organic percentage or misunderstand the label categories
- Common error: including water and salt in the organic percentage calculation (they should be excluded)
- Common error: claiming "organic" when actual organic content is 70-94% (should be "made with organic")
- Common error: using USDA seal on "made with organic" products (seal only allowed on "100% organic" and "organic" categories)

Use this format for ingredient-by-ingredient compliance review:

| # | Ingredient | Source/Supplier | Organic? | If Non-Organic: Basis | National List Section | OMRI Listed? | Supplier Cert Status | Expiry Date | Risk Flag |
|---|-----------|----------------|----------|----------------------|----------------------|-------------|---------------------|-------------|-----------|
| 1 | Organic cane sugar | [Supplier] | Yes | N/A | N/A | N/A | Valid | [Date] | Low |
| 2 | Citric acid | [Supplier] | No (non-ag) | §205.605(a) — nonsynthetic, produced by microbial fermentation | §205.605(a) | Check OMRI | N/A (non-ag) | N/A | Medium — verify fermentation source |
| 3 | Xanthan gum | [Supplier] | No (non-ag) | §205.605(b) — synthetic allowed | §205.605(b) | Check OMRI | N/A (non-ag) | N/A | Low |
| 4 | Natural vanilla flavor | [Supplier] | No (ag) | §205.606 + commercial unavailability | §205.606 | N/A | Requires CU documentation | Annual | High — verify carrier compliance, document CU |
| 5 | Water | [Municipal] | N/A | Excluded from % calc | N/A | N/A | N/A | N/A | Low — verify treatment methods (chlorine levels) |
| 6 | Ascorbic acid | [Supplier] | No (non-ag) | §205.605(b) — synthetic allowed | §205.605(b) | Check OMRI | N/A (non-ag) | N/A | Low |

### Column Definitions
- **Organic?** Yes/No/N/A (non-agricultural substances are N/A)
- **If Non-Organic: Basis:** Which National List section authorizes use?
- **OMRI Listed?** Is the specific brand-name product OMRI-listed for handling use?
- **Supplier Cert Status:** Valid / Expired / Pending / N/A
- **Risk Flag:** Low / Medium / High — based on compliance complexity, supply risk, or annotation restrictions
