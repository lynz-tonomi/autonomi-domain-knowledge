## 1. NOP Label Categories Overview (7 CFR §§205.300-205.311)

The NOP defines four distinct labeling categories based on organic ingredient content. These categories determine what claims can be made on the label, whether the USDA seal can be used, and what certifier identification is required.

| Category | Organic Content | "Organic" in Product Name? | USDA Seal? | Certifier ID Required? |
|----------|----------------|---------------------------|------------|----------------------|
| 100% Organic | 100% organic ingredients (excl. water & salt) | Yes | Yes (optional) | Yes |
| Organic | ≥95% organic ingredients (excl. water & salt) | Yes | Yes (optional) | Yes |
| Made With Organic | ≥70% organic ingredients (excl. water & salt) | No (but "Made With Organic [X]" allowed) | NO | Yes |
| <70% Organic | <70% organic ingredients | No | NO | NO |

## 2. "100% Organic" Label Requirements (§205.301)

### Who Qualifies
Products composed entirely of organic ingredients. Every ingredient (excluding water and salt) must be certified organic.

### Principal Display Panel (PDP)
- May use the term "100% Organic" to describe the product
- Example: "100% Organic Apple Juice"

### Information Panel
- Must display certifying agent name and/or seal
- Ingredient statement: All ingredients identified as "organic" (e.g., "Organic apples, organic cane sugar")
- Statement of handling: "Certified organic by [certifying agent name]" or equivalent

### USDA Seal
- May display the USDA Organic seal on the PDP and/or information panel
- Seal usage is optional, not required

### Processing Aids
- Must comply with §205.605 (National List)
- Processing aids do not need to be organic, but must be NOP-compliant

### Additional Notes
- Water added as an ingredient: excluded from percentage calculation per §205.302(a)
- Salt: excluded from percentage calculation per §205.302(a)
- The product is NOT required to display the USDA seal — it's optional. Some brands choose not to use it.

## 3. "Organic" Label Requirements (§205.301)

### Who Qualifies
Products with at least 95% organic ingredients (by weight, excluding water and salt). The remaining ≤5% must consist of:
- Nonagricultural substances on the National List §205.605, OR
- Nonorganically produced agricultural products on §205.606 (with commercial unavailability documented), OR
- Organic agricultural products

### Principal Display Panel
- May use the word "Organic" to modify the product name
- Example: "Organic Granola Bars"

### Information Panel
- Must display certifying agent name and/or seal
- Ingredient statement: Organic ingredients identified with "organic" preceding the ingredient name
- Non-organic ingredients listed without "organic" prefix
- Statement of handling

### USDA Seal
- May display the USDA Organic seal (optional)

### The 5% Non-Organic Allowance
This 5% is the most commonly misunderstood part of NOP labeling:
- It is 5% by WEIGHT of the finished product, excluding water and salt
- Each non-organic ingredient must have a legitimate compliance basis:
  - If nonagricultural: must be on §205.605 (allowed nonsynthetic or synthetic)
  - If agricultural: must be on §205.606 AND commercially unavailable in organic form
- Non-organic agricultural products NOT on §205.606 may NOT be used in the 5%, even if organic version is unavailable
- Non-organic ingredients must not be produced using excluded methods (genetic engineering), ionizing radiation, or sewage sludge

## 4. "Made With Organic [Specified Ingredients]" Label Requirements (§205.304)

### Who Qualifies
Products with at least 70% organic ingredients (by weight, excluding water and salt).

### Principal Display Panel
- May state "Made With Organic [specific ingredient or food group]"
- Up to three ingredients or food groups may be listed
- Example: "Made With Organic Oats, Honey, and Blueberries"
- Cannot use the word "organic" in the product name itself
  - WRONG: "Organic Trail Mix — Made With Organic Nuts"
  - RIGHT: "Trail Mix — Made With Organic Almonds, Cashews, and Raisins"

### Information Panel
- Must display certifying agent name and/or seal
- Ingredient statement: Organic ingredients identified with "organic" prefix
- Statement of handling

### USDA Seal
- **NOT PERMITTED** — the USDA Organic seal cannot appear anywhere on the label
- Certifier's own seal/logo may appear (per certifier's label use policy)

### Non-Organic Ingredients (30%)
- NOT required to be on the National List
- BUT: must not be produced using excluded methods (genetic engineering), ionizing radiation, or sewage sludge
- This is a key distinction from the "organic" category — more flexibility on which non-organic ingredients can be used

## 5. Products with Less Than 70% Organic Ingredients (§205.305)

### What's Allowed
- May identify certified organic ingredients in the ingredient statement ONLY
- Example: In the ingredient list — "sugar, flour, organic butter, salt"
- The word "organic" may appear ONLY in the ingredient statement, not on the PDP or anywhere else on the label

### What's NOT Allowed
- No "organic" claims on PDP or anywhere else on the package (other than ingredient statement)
- No USDA Organic seal
- No certifying agent identification (because the product itself is not certified)
- No "made with organic" claim
- No implication that the product is organic

## 6. USDA Organic Seal Rules (§205.311)

### Seal Specifications
- The USDA Organic seal is a circular design with "USDA ORGANIC" text
- Available in: full-color (green and brown/white), black and white, or single-color variations
- Must be reproduced per USDA guidelines (available at USDA AMS website)
- Minimum size not specified by NOP, but must be legible

### Where the Seal May Appear
- **"100% Organic" products:** Anywhere on label (PDP, information panel, cap, case)
- **"Organic" products:** Anywhere on label
- **"Made With Organic" products:** NOWHERE — seal is prohibited
- **<70% organic products:** NOWHERE — seal is prohibited

### Seal Usage Rights
- No license fee from USDA for using the seal
- Operation must be certified organic by a USDA-accredited certifying agent
- Seal usage is voluntary — certified operations are NOT required to use it

### Common Seal Errors
- Using the USDA seal on "made with organic" products (violation)
- Using the USDA seal with the wrong colors or proportions (technically non-compliant but often not enforced unless egregious)
- Using the USDA seal on promotional materials for non-certified products

## 7. Certifying Agent Identification (§205.303)

### Requirement
All products in the "100% Organic," "Organic," and "Made With Organic" categories must display:
- The statement "Certified organic by [certifying agent name]" or equivalent
- OR the certifying agent's seal/logo

### Placement
- Information panel (preferred) or elsewhere on the package
- Must be in a size and format that is legible to the consumer

### Multiple Certifying Agents
- If ingredients are certified by different agents, only the handler's (final product's) certifying agent needs to be identified on the label
- Private label products: the brand owner's certifying agent appears on the label (not the co-packer's, though the co-packer must also be certified)

## 8. Calculating Organic Percentage (§205.302)

### Formula
Organic % = (Weight of organic ingredients / Total weight of all ingredients MINUS water and salt) × 100

### Key Rules
- **Water:** Excluded from BOTH numerator and denominator, regardless of whether it's organic
- **Salt:** Excluded from BOTH numerator and denominator
- **Weight basis:** Use formulation weight (not volume) at the time of formulation (before cooking, baking, or other processing that may change weight)
- **Processing aids:** Not included in the percentage calculation (they are not ingredients under FDA definition)
- **Organic ingredients:** Must be certified organic (with valid certificate from supplier)

### Worked Example
Product: Organic Lemonade
- Water: 900g — EXCLUDED from calculation
- Organic lemon juice: 50g
- Organic cane sugar: 45g
- Citric acid (non-ag, §205.605(a)): 3g
- Natural lemon flavor (non-organic ag): 2g
- Salt: 0.5g — EXCLUDED from calculation

Calculation:
- Total ingredients minus water and salt: 50 + 45 + 3 + 2 = 100g
- Organic ingredients: 50 + 45 = 95g
- Organic percentage: 95/100 = 95%
- Result: Qualifies for "Organic" label category

## 9. Ingredient Statement Rules

### General FDA Requirements (21 CFR §101.4)
- Ingredients listed in descending order of predominance by weight
- Common/usual names required
- Sub-ingredients of multi-component ingredients must be declared (with limited exceptions)

### Organic-Specific Requirements
- **"100% Organic" and "Organic" products:** Each organic ingredient preceded by "organic" — e.g., "organic cane sugar, organic cocoa, organic vanilla extract"
- **"Made With Organic" products:** Each organic ingredient preceded by "organic"
- **<70% products:** May (but not required to) identify organic ingredients with "organic" prefix in ingredient statement

### Flavors
- Natural flavors in organic products: declared as "natural [type] flavor" or "natural flavor"
- If organic: "organic natural flavor"
- Flavor solvents/carriers must be NOP-compliant for "100% organic" and "organic" categories
- 21 CFR 101.22 definitions apply for "natural" classification

## 10. Multi-Ingredient Products — Worked Examples

### Example 1: Organic Fruit Snacks
Ingredients:
- Organic tapioca syrup: 40g
- Organic cane sugar: 25g
- Organic fruit juice concentrates (apple, grape, strawberry): 20g
- Pectin (§205.605(b) — low methoxy): 5g
- Citric acid (§205.605(a)): 3g
- Natural flavors (non-organic): 2g
- Organic sunflower oil: 2g
- Carnauba wax (§205.605(a)): 1g
- Ascorbic acid (§205.605(b)): 0.5g
- Color from organic fruit and vegetable juice: 1.5g

Total excl. water & salt: 100g
Organic: 40 + 25 + 20 + 2 + 1.5 = 88.5g
Non-organic: pectin (5) + citric acid (3) + natural flavors (2) + carnauba wax (1) + ascorbic acid (0.5) = 11.5g
Organic %: 88.5%

**Label category:** "Made With Organic [Tapioca Syrup, Cane Sugar, and Fruit Juice Concentrates]"
**USDA seal:** NOT permitted

### Example 2: Organic Cold Brew Coffee
Ingredients:
- Water: 940g (excluded)
- Organic coffee extract: 58g
- Organic cane sugar: 2g

Total excl. water & salt: 60g
Organic: 60g
Organic %: 100%

**Label category:** "100% Organic Cold Brew Coffee"
**USDA seal:** Permitted

### Example 3: Granola with Organic Oats
Ingredients:
- Organic rolled oats: 35g
- Brown sugar (conventional): 15g
- Canola oil (conventional): 10g
- Organic honey: 8g
- Almonds (conventional): 7g
- Organic coconut: 5g
- Organic dried cranberries: 5g
- Salt: 1g (excluded)
- Natural flavor: 1g
- Organic vanilla extract: 0.5g

Total excl. salt: 86.5g
Organic: 35 + 8 + 5 + 5 + 0.5 = 53.5g
Organic %: 53.5 / 86.5 = 61.8%

**Label category:** <70% — organic claims only in ingredient statement
**USDA seal:** NOT permitted
**"Made with organic":** NOT permitted (below 70%)

## 11. Private Label / Contract Manufacturing (§205.101)

### Brand Owner Responsibilities
- Brand owner must be certified organic if the product is sold under their label as organic
- Brand owner's certifying agent appears on the label
- Brand owner is responsible for label compliance

### Co-Packer / Contract Manufacturer Responsibilities
- Co-packer/toll manufacturer must also be certified organic (or operate under the brand owner's certification — a complex arrangement requiring certifier approval)
- Both operations are subject to inspection
- Co-packer's certificate does NOT appear on the product label (brand owner's does)

### Common Issue
- Brand owner assumes co-packer's certification covers their products — it does not
- Brand owner MUST have their own organic certification to make organic claims
- If co-packer is NOT certified organic, the brand owner CANNOT make organic claims on products manufactured at that facility (even if the brand owner is certified)

## 12. Export Label Requirements

### US to Canada
- Under US-Canada Equivalency Arrangement
- NOP organic products can be sold as organic in Canada and vice versa
- Must comply with Canadian labeling requirements for Canadian market
- May need bilingual (English/French) labels for Canadian market
- "Canada Organic" logo usage: subject to Canadian Organic Regime rules

### US to EU
- Under US-EU Organic Equivalency Arrangement (verify current status — this arrangement has been modified over time)
- Certain products require Certificate of Inspection (COI) for import into EU
- EU organic label requirements differ from US:
  - EU organic logo (green leaf) mandatory on pre-packaged EU organic products
  - For non-EU origin products: "non-EU Agriculture" designation required
  - Code number of certifying agent required
- Some NOP-allowed substances may not be recognized under EU organic rules — verify product-by-product

### US to Japan (JAS)
- Japan Agricultural Standards (JAS) organic
- Requires JAS-registered certifier
- JAS organic mark required on products sold as organic in Japan
- Labeling requirements differ (Japanese language, specific format)

### General Export Labeling Principles
- Domestic NOP label compliance is necessary but not sufficient for export
- Each destination country has its own organic labeling requirements
- Work with certifier who has experience with target export market
- Consider dual-labeling (NOP compliance + destination country compliance)

## 13. State Organic Program Overlaps

### State Organic Programs (SOPs)
- Several US states have their own organic programs that operate in conjunction with or under the NOP
- State programs must be at least as restrictive as NOP (can be more restrictive)
- Examples:
  - **California:** California Organic Products Act (COPA) — administered by CDFA
    - Requires state organic registration in addition to NOP certification for California operations
    - California organic producers and handlers must register with CDFA Organic Program
    - Additional enforcement mechanisms
  - **Texas:** Texas Department of Agriculture Organic Program
  - **Washington:** Washington State Department of Agriculture Organic Program

### Practical Implications
- If operating in a state with a State Organic Program, you may need BOTH NOP certification AND state registration
- State programs may have additional labeling requirements
- State programs may conduct their own inspections in addition to NOP certifier inspections
- Check with your state's Department of Agriculture for requirements

## 14. Common Label Errors and How to Fix Them

| Error | Why It's Wrong | How to Fix |
|-------|---------------|------------|
| USDA seal on "Made With Organic" product | Seal only allowed on "100% Organic" and "Organic" (§205.311) | Remove USDA seal. Certifier seal may remain. |
| "Organic" in product name at 70-94% organic | "Organic" in product name requires ≥95% organic (§205.301) | Change to "Made With Organic [ingredients]" or reformulate to ≥95% |
| No certifying agent on "Organic" product | Certifying agent ID required (§205.303) | Add "Certified organic by [name]" to information panel |
| Including water in organic % calculation | Water excluded per §205.302(a) | Recalculate excluding water (and salt) |
| Using "organic" in product name for "Made With" category | "Made With Organic" cannot use "organic" as standalone descriptor of the product | Restructure: "Trail Mix — Made With Organic Almonds and Cashews" not "Organic Trail Mix" |
| Non-organic ingredient not on National List in "Organic" product | The ≤5% non-organic portion must be on §205.605 (non-ag) or §205.606 (ag) | Reformulate: source organic version, or find National List-compliant alternative |
| "Organic" claim on product with <70% organic | No organic claims on PDP or package allowed below 70% (§205.305) | Remove all organic claims except in ingredient statement |
| Listing "organic water" as an ingredient | Water is not certifiable as organic and is excluded from organic calculations | Remove "organic" from water in ingredient statement |
| Claiming certifier on <70% organic product | Certifier ID is only for certified products (§205.303) | Remove certifier identification from label |
| "No pesticides" claim on organic product | Organic doesn't mean "no pesticides" — certain pesticides are allowed | Remove misleading claim or work with legal counsel on compliant wording |

## 15. Label Review Process

### Internal Label Review Workflow
1. **Draft label created** by marketing/design team
2. **QA/Regulatory review:**
   - Calculate organic percentage
   - Verify correct label category
   - Check USDA seal usage (allowed? removed if not?)
   - Verify certifying agent identification
   - Check ingredient statement for correct "organic" prefix usage
   - Verify non-organic ingredients have National List basis (for "Organic" category)
   - Check all FDA-required elements (Nutrition Facts, allergens, net weight, etc.)
3. **Certifier review** (many certifiers require or offer label review as part of certification):
   - Submit label to certifying agent for approval
   - Address any certifier comments
   - Receive certifier approval
4. **Final proof review** — verify print proof matches approved version
5. **Label approval record** — document the approval chain (who reviewed, when, which version)
6. **Label inventory control** — obsolete labels destroyed or quarantined; only approved labels in production

### When to Re-Review Labels
- Any formulation change (may change organic percentage)
- New ingredient added or ingredient source changed
- Certifying agent changed
- Change in organic status of any ingredient
- Annual review recommended (coincide with OSP update)

### Label Retention
- Retain approved labels (or images) for minimum 5 years per NOP recordkeeping requirement (§205.103)
- Retain label review records and certifier approval correspondence
