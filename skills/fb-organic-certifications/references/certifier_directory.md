## 1. USDA-Accredited Organic Certifying Agents

The USDA maintains a list of accredited certifying agents at the USDA Organic Integrity Database. There are approximately 80 USDA-accredited certifying agents worldwide. Below are the most commonly used agents for US food and beverage manufacturers.

### Major US-Based Certifying Agents

**CCOF (California Certified Organic Farmers)**
- Headquarters: Santa Cruz, CA
- Scope: Crops, livestock, handlers/processors, wild-crop, private label/retail
- Geography: Nationwide, particularly strong in Western US. International scope (Mexico, Central America).
- Strengths: Largest US organic certifier by number of operations. Excellent handler/processor expertise. Well-recognized brand in retail. Strong educational resources.
- Cost: Application fees start ~$750; annual certification fees based on gross organic sales (sliding scale, typically $750–$10,000+ for handlers). Inspection fees additional.
- Timeline: Application review typically 4–8 weeks. Inspection scheduling 2–6 weeks after review. Total application-to-certificate: 3–6 months (varies by season and complexity).
- International: Accredited for NOP, Canada Organic Regime, Mexico organic.
- Website: ccof.org

**Oregon Tilth Certified Organic (OTCO)**
- Headquarters: Portland, OR
- Scope: Crops, livestock, handlers/processors, wild-crop
- Geography: Nationwide. Strong in Pacific Northwest, growing nationally.
- Strengths: Reputation for technical rigor. Excellent with complex handler operations (multi-ingredient products, co-packing). Good international equivalency experience (EU, Japan, Canada).
- Cost: Application fees ~$500–$1,000; annual fees based on gross organic sales (typically $800–$8,000+ for handlers). Inspection fees additional.
- Timeline: Similar to CCOF. Application-to-certificate typically 3–6 months.
- International: NOP, Canada, EU, JAS, Korea equivalency.
- Website: tilth.org

**QAI (Quality Assurance International)**
- Headquarters: San Diego, CA (now part of NSF International)
- Scope: Handlers/processors, crops, livestock, personal care, textiles
- Geography: Nationwide and international. Strong in California, Southwest.
- Strengths: Integrated with NSF International, allowing bundled certifications (organic + gluten-free + Non-GMO through NSF). Strong with supplement and personal care manufacturers.
- Cost: Fees similar to CCOF/OTCO range. NSF bundling may offer cost advantages for multi-certification.
- Timeline: 3–6 months typical.
- International: NOP, EU, Canada, JAS, other equivalencies.
- Website: nsf.org/qai

**OCIA International (Organic Crop Improvement Association)**
- Headquarters: Lincoln, NE
- Scope: Crops, livestock, handlers, wild-crop
- Geography: Strong in Midwest, Plains states. International (Latin America, Asia).
- Strengths: Strong crop/producer expertise. Deep experience with grain, oilseed, and feed operations. Good international sourcing certification.
- Cost: Competitive pricing, particularly for producers. Application + annual fees typically lower than coastal certifiers.
- Timeline: 3–6 months.
- International: NOP, Canada, EU, JAS, Mexico.
- Website: ocia.org

**Vermont Organic Farmers (VOF) / NOFA-VT**
- Headquarters: Richmond, VT
- Scope: Crops, livestock, handlers, maple
- Geography: Vermont and New England primary. Some national handler certification.
- Strengths: Deep expertise in maple syrup, dairy, small-scale diversified farms. Strong community and education approach.
- Cost: Generally lower fees, reflecting smaller scale. Good option for small producers and handlers.
- Timeline: 3–5 months.
- Note: Primarily Northeast focus; not the best choice for large national handler operations.

**Baystate Organic Certifiers**
- Headquarters: Deerfield, MA
- Scope: Crops, livestock, handlers
- Geography: Northeast US
- Strengths: New England focus, expertise with small-to-medium operations and specialty crops
- Cost: Competitive regional pricing
- Timeline: 3–6 months

**PCO (Pennsylvania Certified Organic)**
- Headquarters: Centre Hall, PA
- Scope: Crops, livestock, handlers
- Geography: Mid-Atlantic and Northeast
- Strengths: Strong with dairy, produce, and handler operations in the region

**MOSA (Midwest Organic Services Association)**
- Headquarters: Viroqua, WI
- Scope: Crops, livestock, handlers
- Geography: Midwest
- Strengths: Excellent for Midwest producers and handlers. Deep dairy and grain expertise.

**SCS Global Services**
- Headquarters: Emeryville, CA
- Scope: Crops, livestock, handlers, wild-crop, international
- Strengths: Global audit infrastructure. Good for companies needing organic + other certifications (Rainforest Alliance, Non-GMO, sustainability certifications).
- Cost: Mid-range to premium pricing; value in multi-certification bundling
- Timeline: 3–6 months
- International: Broad equivalency recognition

**International Certifiers Operating in US**
- **Ecocert** (France-based, US office): Strong EU equivalency, international ingredient sourcing
- **Control Union** (Netherlands-based): Broad scope including EU Organic, GLOBALG.A.P., multiple certifications
- **CERES** (Germany-based): EU Organic, JAS, global scope
- **Kiwa BCS Öko-Garantie** (Germany-based): EU Organic, Latin American sourcing

### Certifier Selection for Export
If you need to export organic products:
- **To EU:** Choose a certifier recognized by the EU for equivalency. CCOF, Oregon Tilth, QAI/NSF, OCIA all have EU recognition. Verify current status as EU recognition arrangements change.
- **To Japan (JAS):** Certifier must be JAS-registered. Oregon Tilth, OCIA, QAI/NSF, Ecocert, Control Union are common options.
- **To Canada:** Almost all US certifiers have Canada equivalency (bilateral arrangement is broad). Verify for specific products.
- **To Korea:** Limited certifiers with NAQS recognition. Check USDA Organic Integrity Database.

## 2. GFSI Certification Bodies

GFSI certification bodies (CBs) are accredited by accreditation bodies (e.g., ANAB in the US, UKAS in the UK) and approved by the scheme owner. Major GFSI schemes include SQF, BRC, FSSC 22000, and IFS.

### Certification Body Selection Criteria
- **Scheme accreditation:** Verify the CB is accredited for YOUR specific scheme (SQF, BRC, FSSC 22000, IFS) and scope category
- **Auditor competency:** Request auditor CVs — industry experience in your specific product category matters enormously
- **Lead times:** Some CBs book 2–3 months out. Others can schedule within 4–6 weeks. Confirm current capacity.
- **Pricing:** Varies 20–40% between CBs for equivalent scope. Get 3 quotes minimum.
- **Multi-site capability:** If you have multiple facilities, a single CB may offer efficiencies (volume discounts, consistent audit standards across sites)
- **Additional services:** Some CBs offer training, pre-assessment, gap assessment (note: cannot provide consulting AND certification simultaneously — conflict of interest rules apply)
- **Geographic coverage:** If your facilities are in remote areas, ensure the CB has auditors who can travel there efficiently (travel costs can be significant)
- **Reciprocal recognition:** Some retail customers prefer specific CBs; confirm your major customers will accept the CB you choose

## 3. Non-GMO Project Technical Administrators

Technical Administrators (TAs) are licensed by the Non-GMO Project to manage the verification process. Non-GMO Project certification requires <0.9% GMO content per ingredient and finished product testing.

| Technical Administrator | Headquarters | Scope | Notes |
|------------------------|-------------|-------|-------|
| FoodChain ID | Fairfield, IA | Crops, ingredients, handlers, private label | Largest TA by volume. Also handles EU Novel Food, organic, labeling compliance. |
| NSF International | Ann Arbor, MI | Crops, ingredients, handlers, multi-ingredient | Major multi-service provider. Bundles with organic (QAI), GFSI, gluten-free. |
| SCS Global Services | Emeryville, CA | Crops, ingredients, handlers, sustainability | Multi-certification provider. Organic + Non-GMO + sustainability auditing. |
| Where Food Comes From | Castle Rock, CO | Crops, ingredients, handlers | Also handles USDA Process Verified, source verification programs. |
| International Certification Services (ICS) | Medina, ND | Crops, ingredients, handlers | Smaller, organic-focused, competitive pricing for small-to-medium operations. |
| Global Organic Alliance | Bellefontaine, OH | Crops, ingredients, handlers | Organic + Non-GMO bundle. Strong in Midwest. |

### TA Selection Criteria
- **Testing laboratory partnerships:** TAs use specific labs — turnaround times for GMO testing vary (typically 2–4 weeks)
- **Number of verifications managed:** Experience with your specific product category (e.g., processed foods, ingredients, beverages) matters
- **Bundling opportunity:** If also pursuing organic, choosing a TA that's also an organic certifier (e.g., NSF/QAI, SCS, ICS) streamlines documentation
- **Documentation review speed:** Varies from 2–6 weeks for Non-GMO Project application review
- **Responsiveness:** Some TAs have faster review cycles and more accessible coordinators than others
- **Cost:** Initial evaluation fee (~$300–$800) + annual verification fees (typically $1,000–$5,000 depending on number of ingredients). Get quotes.
- **Ingredient sourcing:** If you use imported ingredients, verify the TA has experience with international supply chain documentation

## 4. Gluten-Free Certifiers

| Organization | Program Name | Threshold | Annual Requirements | Notes |
|-------------|--------------|-----------|--------------------:|-------|
| GFCO (Gluten-Free Certification Organization) | GFCO Certified | ≤10 ppm | Annual audit + product testing (3+ times/year) | Most recognized GF certification mark in North America. Stringent requirements. |
| NSF International | NSF Gluten-Free | <20 ppm | Audit + testing | Aligned with FDA rule. Well-recognized mark. Often bundled with organic/GFSI. |
| Canadian Celiac Association (CCA) | Gluten-Free Certification Program | <20 ppm | Annual audit + testing | Primarily Canadian market and companies exporting to Canada. |
| Allergen Control Group (ACG) | Gluten-Free Certification | Varies by product | Audit-based | Allergen management focused. May be cost-effective for simple products. |

### Gluten-Free Selection Criteria
- **Market recognition:** GFCO is most recognized. If product is for mass market US retail, GFCO is the preferred choice.
- **Threshold:** GFCO's ≤10 ppm is more stringent than FDA's <20 ppm. Consider your target market.
- **Cost:** GFCO is typically more expensive due to higher frequency testing. NSF may be lower cost.
- **Facility shared-use:** If your facility also processes gluten-containing products, GFCO has strict requirements. NSF is somewhat more flexible.
- **Testing frequency:** GFCO requires testing 3+ times annually; NSF typically 1–2 times.

## 5. Kosher Certifying Agencies

### Major US Kosher Certifiers

**Orthodox Union (OU)**
- Headquarters: New York, NY
- Scope: All food product categories
- Market recognition: Most widely recognized kosher symbol in North America (on ~60% of kosher-certified US products)
- Strengths: Highest market recognition; deep expertise across all product categories; extensive auditor network
- Cost: Premium pricing (application ~$1,000–$2,000; annual fees $2,000–$15,000+ depending on complexity and plant visits)
- Timeline: 2–4 weeks for review; 2–12 weeks for first audit scheduling (varies by season)
- Passover: Yes, separate Passover certification program available
- Website: ou.org

**OK (Organized Kashrus Laboratories)**
- Headquarters: New York, NY
- Scope: All food categories
- Market recognition: Second most widely recognized mark (on ~20% of kosher products)
- Strengths: Strong in ingredients, industrial foods, international recognition (especially in Israel)
- Cost: Competitive with OU; may be slightly lower. Annual fees typically $1,500–$12,000
- Timeline: Similar to OU
- Passover: Yes
- Website: ok.org

**Star-K Kosher Certification**
- Headquarters: Baltimore, MD
- Scope: All categories; special expertise in industrial ingredients, gelatin, enzymes
- Market recognition: Well-recognized, particularly in ingredients and specialty food markets
- Strengths: Deep technical expertise in challenging ingredient categories (enzymes, emulsifiers, modified starches); good for industrial/ingredient manufacturers
- Cost: Mid-range. Annual fees typically $1,000–$10,000
- Timeline: 2–4 weeks review
- Passover: Yes
- Website: star-k.org

**Kof-K Kosher Supervision**
- Headquarters: Teaneck, NJ
- Scope: All categories
- Market recognition: Well-recognized, strong in beverage industry
- Strengths: Good beverage/ingredient expertise; responsive customer service
- Cost: Mid-to-lower range. Annual fees typically $1,000–$8,000
- Timeline: 2–4 weeks
- Passover: Yes
- Website: kof-k.org

**CRC (Certified Kosher Supervision)**
- Headquarters: Chicago, IL
- Scope: All categories
- Market recognition: Well-recognized regionally (strong in Midwest); growing national presence
- Strengths: Good expertise in dairy, meat, grains; strong community relationships in Chicago region
- Cost: Generally lower than OU/OK; annual fees typically $800–$7,000
- Timeline: 2–4 weeks
- Passover: Yes
- Website: crcweb.org

### Kosher Selection Criteria
- **Market recognition:** OU is the default for mass market. If product is for Jewish consumers or upscale/premium market, OU is highly preferred. OK and Star-K are excellent second choices.
- **Product category expertise:** Star-K excels with difficult ingredients (enzymes, emulsifiers). Kof-K is strong with beverages.
- **Cost:** OU is typically the most expensive. Regional certifiers (CRC, Star-K) may offer lower fees.
- **Speed:** All major certifiers have similar turnaround for initial review (2–4 weeks).
- **International recognition:** OU and OK are most recognized internationally. Critical for export to Israel or other countries with kosher requirements.
- **Passover certification:** If needed, verify the agency offers Passover certification. Passover requires additional oversight and ingredient pre-approval.
- **Plant visits:** Some certifiers require annual plant visits; others may do inspections every other year. Confirm frequency and travel costs.

## 6. Halal Certifying Bodies

### Major US Halal Certifiers

**IFANCA (Islamic Food and Nutrition Council of America)**
- Headquarters: Chicago, IL
- Scope: Food, ingredients, supplements, pharmaceuticals
- Market recognition: Highest international recognition (IFANCA-certified products recognized in 50+ Muslim-majority countries)
- Strengths: Deep expertise in food ingredient halal status (animal-derived enzymes, emulsifiers, additives); broad international recognition; established relationships with Middle Eastern importers
- Cost: Application ~$500–$1,000; annual fees $1,500–$8,000 depending on complexity
- Timeline: 2–4 weeks for initial review; audit scheduling 4–8 weeks
- International: Recognized by OIC (Organization of Islamic Cooperation)
- Website: ifanca.org

**ISA (Islamic Society of the Animal and Plant Health)**
- Headquarters: Cedar Rapids, IA
- Scope: Food, ingredients, supplements
- Market recognition: Growing international recognition (Canada, parts of Europe, Asia)
- Strengths: Good ingredient expertise; strong in North American production
- Cost: Generally competitive with IFANCA; annual fees $1,200–$6,000
- Timeline: 2–4 weeks
- International: Expanding recognition
- Website: isahalal.org

**ISNA Halal (Islamic Society of North America)**
- Headquarters: Plainfield, IN
- Scope: Food, ingredients
- Market recognition: Recognized primarily in North America
- Strengths: Community-focused; reasonable fees; good for small-to-medium producers
- Cost: Lower than IFANCA; annual fees $800–$4,000
- Timeline: 2–4 weeks
- Note: Less international recognition than IFANCA; recommend IFANCA if export is priority
- Website: isnahalalfood.com

**HTO (Halal Transactions Oversight)**
- Headquarters: New Jersey
- Scope: Food, ingredients, supplements
- Market recognition: Smaller certifier; recognition primarily in North America
- Strengths: Responsive to small producers; flexible inspection scheduling
- Cost: Lower fees; $600–$3,000 annually
- Timeline: 2–4 weeks
- Note: Limited international recognition

### Halal Selection Criteria
- **Export market:** If exporting to Muslim-majority countries (Middle East, Malaysia, Indonesia, etc.), IFANCA is the preferred certifier due to broad international recognition. Verify the specific country's halal authority recognizes your certifier.
- **Ingredient expertise:** IFANCA has the deepest expertise with challenging halal determinations (enzymes, modified starches, flavorings, emulsifiers)
- **Cost:** ISNA and HTO are lower cost; IFANCA is mid-range
- **Dual certification (Kosher + Halal):** Some companies pursue both. IFANCA and OU/OK are willing to coordinate inspections. Confirm auditor availability.
- **Speed:** All major certifiers similar (2–4 weeks review)
- **Turnaround for ingredient pre-approval:** If using imported ingredients or new suppliers, some certifiers provide faster pre-approval than others. Confirm timeline.

## 7. Fair Trade Certifiers

### Fair Trade International (FLO) System

**Standard:** Fair Trade International (formerly Fair Trade Labeling Organization)
- Certifying body: FLOCERT (Bonn, Germany)
- Scope: Coffee, cocoa, bananas, tea, sugar, spices, flowers, cotton, and increasing number of products
- Requirements: Supplier standards (labor practices, environmental, democratic governance), pricing floor, premium payments
- Cost: Inspection fees + certification fees (typically $500–$3,000+ annually depending on scope and complexity)
- Timeline: Initial audit typically 4–8 weeks after application; ongoing annual audits
- Market recognition: Very high; recognized by consumers globally
- Website: fairtradeintl.org

### Fair Trade USA

**Standard:** Fair Trade Certified (US-specific system)
- Certifying body: Fair Trade USA; audits conducted by various accredited auditors (list on Fair Trade USA website)
- Scope: Similar to FLO plus some US-specific products
- Requirements: Similar to FLO but pricing and standards may differ
- Cost: Similar fee structure; varies by auditor
- Timeline: Similar to FLO
- Market recognition: Strong in North America (60% of US "fair trade" labeled products use Fair Trade USA)
- Website: fairtradecertified.org

### Fair Trade USA Auditors (Common)

Fair Trade USA does not employ auditors directly; it licenses third-party auditors. Common auditors include:
- Certifications International
- Global Organic Alliance
- Oregon Tilth (organic + fair trade)
- SCS Global Services
- NSF International (for specific product categories)

### Fair Trade Selection Criteria
- **Market presence:** Fair Trade International (FLO) has broader global recognition; Fair Trade USA has stronger US market presence
- **Product category:** Not all products can be certified fair trade. Verify your product category is eligible.
- **Supply chain transparency:** Fair Trade requires documented supplier relationships and labor practices. Ensure your supply chain can meet requirements.
- **Cost:** Fair Trade certification is moderate cost compared to organic or GFSI, but requires ongoing compliance
- **Bundling:** Some certifiers (Oregon Tilth, SCS, Global Organic Alliance) offer organic + fair trade bundling
- **Premium pricing impact:** Fair Trade certification typically supports premium pricing in retail; confirm your market positioning supports the certification cost

## 8. Selection Criteria Guide — Universal Principles

Regardless of certification type, evaluate certifiers on:

### 1. Scope Match
Does this certifier/CB cover your specific products, operation type, and scope?
- Product category (crops, handlers, processors, ingredients, supplements, beverages, etc.)
- Operation size (small-scale, artisanal vs. industrial, co-packing vs. dedicated facility)
- Ingredient complexity (single-ingredient vs. multi-ingredient, imported vs. domestic)

### 2. Accreditation Status
Is the certifier properly accredited?
- **Organic:** Verify on USDA Organic Integrity Database (search by certifier name)
- **GFSI:** Confirm on individual scheme websites (SQF, BRC, FSSC 22000, IFS) for specific accreditation
- **Non-GMO:** Non-GMO Project website lists licensed TAs
- **Specialty:** Verify with the program (OU, GFCO, IFANCA, Fair Trade, etc.)

### 3. Cost
Get minimum 3 quotes. Include all fees:
- Application/initial evaluation fee
- Annual certification fee
- Inspection/audit day rates and estimated number of days
- Travel expenses (often significant for remote facilities)
- Product testing or sampling fees
- Corrective action/follow-up audit fees
- Report/certificate fees

Calculate both Year 1 (higher due to application) and Year 2+ costs.

### 4. Timeline
Confirm current lead times from application to inspection/certificate:
- Application review period
- Inspection/audit scheduling lead time
- Total time from application to certificate issuance
- Seasonal variations (e.g., organic inspections may slow in winter)

Ask for realistic timelines, not marketing estimates. Confirm auditor availability for your preferred inspection dates.

### 5. Auditor/Inspector Quality
Request auditor/inspector qualifications:
- Industry experience in YOUR specific product category
- Years of experience
- Language capabilities (if relevant)
- Product-specific certifications or training
- Client references from similar operations

Industry-specific experience is more valuable than a generalist auditor.

### 6. Multi-Certification Bundling
If pursuing multiple certifications, evaluate:
- Can this provider handle more than one certification type?
- Will they coordinate multi-certification audits (reduces facility disruption)?
- Are there bundled pricing discounts?
- Does bundling reduce documentation duplication?

Examples: NSF (organic + GFSI + gluten-free + Non-GMO); SCS (organic + GFSI + Non-GMO + sustainability); Oregon Tilth (organic + fair trade).

### 7. International Scope & Equivalency
If you export, does this certifier have international recognition?
- **EU:** Certifier must be approved for EU Organic equivalency (check EU Commission list)
- **Japan:** JAS registration required
- **Canada:** Most US organic certifiers have mutual recognition agreements
- **Korea, China, other markets:** Verify specific recognition arrangements

### 8. Responsiveness & Support
Evaluate:
- Response time to questions (email, phone availability)
- Accessibility of auditors/reviewers
- Documentation submission process (portal, email, paper)
- Availability for pre-audit consultations or gap assessments
- Flexibility in audit scheduling

Poor responsiveness can delay certification and create frustration.

### 9. Client References
Ask for references from similar operations:
- Same product type (e.g., if you make plant-based beverages, find references from other beverage makers)
- Similar operation size
- Similar complexity level
- Recent certifications (within last 2 years)

Ask references:
- Was the audit fair and technically sound?
- Were corrective actions reasonable?
- How responsive was the certifier?
- Would they re-certify?

### 10. Corrective Action Process
Understand how the certifier handles non-conformances:
- What is their process for reviewing corrective action requests?
- How collaborative vs. strictly transactional is their approach?
- What is the timeline for follow-up audits?
- Are minor vs. major non-conformances handled differently?
- What is the pass/fail threshold?

Some certifiers are more consultative (within ethical boundaries); others are strictly transactional.
