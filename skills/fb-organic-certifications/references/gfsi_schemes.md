## 1. GFSI Overview

### What is GFSI?
The Global Food Safety Initiative is a private, business-driven initiative managed by The Consumer Goods Forum (CGF). GFSI does NOT certify companies — it benchmarks food safety management schemes. When a scheme is "GFSI-recognized" or "GFSI-benchmarked," it means GFSI has evaluated the scheme and determined it meets GFSI's Benchmarking Requirements.

### Why GFSI Matters
- Major retailers and food companies require GFSI-benchmarked certification from their suppliers
- "Certified once, accepted everywhere" principle (though individual retailers may still have preferences)
- Key retail requirements: Walmart, Costco, Kroger, Target, Whole Foods, McDonald's, Nestlé, and most major retailers and food service companies require GFSI-benchmarked certification
- GFSI benchmarking version: current is v2020 (Benchmarking Requirements v2020)

### Currently GFSI-Recognized Schemes (food manufacturing)
- SQF (Safe Quality Food Institute / FMI)
- BRCGS (Brand Reputation Compliance Global Standards)
- FSSC 22000 (Foundation for Food Safety System Certification)
- IFS Food (International Featured Standards)
- GLOBALG.A.P. (primarily primary production, but Integrated Farm Assurance covers some processing)
- Others: Canada GAP, GRMS, PrimusGFS (fresh produce focused), etc.

## 2. SQF (Safe Quality Food)

### Governing Body
Safe Quality Food Institute (SQFI), a division of FMI — The Food Industry Association (Arlington, VA).

### SQF Code Structure (Edition 9, current)
Three certification levels:
- **SQF Fundamentals (formerly Level 1):** Basic food safety. Based on Good Manufacturing Practices (GMPs). No HACCP required. Suitable for small/developing businesses. NOT GFSI-benchmarked.
- **SQF Food Safety Code (formerly Level 2):** HACCP-based food safety management system. GFSI-benchmarked. Most common level required by retailers.
- **SQF Quality Code (formerly Level 3):** Food safety + quality management. GFSI-benchmarked. Includes SQF Quality Code elements (SPC, quality objectives, continuous improvement). Required by some buyers.

### SQF Code Modules (Ed. 9)
- **Module 2:** SQF System Elements (management commitment, document control, SQF system, training, etc.)
  - 2.1 Management Commitment
  - 2.2 Document Control and Records
  - 2.3 Food Safety and Quality Management System (SQF system scope, food safety plan, food quality plan)
  - 2.4 Management Review (including SQF Practitioner role, management review meetings, complaint management, food safety planning)
  - 2.5 Supplier Approval and Management
  - 2.6 SQF System Verification (validation, verification, product identification, trace and recall)
  - 2.7 Product Identification, Trace, Withdrawal and Recall
  - 2.8 Food Defense and Food Fraud
  - 2.9 Allergen Management
  - 2.10 Training
- **Modules 3-15:** Sector-specific Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) modules
  - Module 3: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Agricultural Practices
  - Module 4: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Aquaculture Practices
  - Module 5: Food Safety Fundamentals — Animal Production
  - Module 6: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Manufacturing Practices for Pre-Processing of Animal Products
  - Module 7: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Manufacturing Practices for Processing of Food Products (MOST COMMON FOR MANUFACTURERS)
  - Module 8: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Manufacturing Practices for Pet Food Manufacturing
  - Module 9: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Manufacturing Practices for Animal Feed Manufacturing
  - Module 10: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Manufacturing Practices for Dietary Supplements
  - Module 11: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Distribution Practices (Storage and Distribution)
  - Module 12: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Distribution Practices (Transport/Broker)
  - Module 13: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Retail Practices
  - Module 14: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Catering Practices
  - Module 15: Food Safety Fundamentals — Good Manufacturing Practices for Primary Packaging Materials

### SQF Practitioner
- Required role for Level 2 and Level 3 certification
- Must complete HACCP training (equivalent to Codex Alimentarius-based HACCP course)
- Responsible for preparation, implementation, review, and maintenance of the SQF System
- Must be a company employee (cannot be outsourced entirely — consultant can assist but employee must own it)
- Must complete SQF Practitioner training (SQFI-registered course)

### Finding Classifications
- **Critical:** Immediate food safety threat or absence of a fundamental element. Certification not granted or suspended if critical found.
- **Major:** Substantial failure to meet SQF Code requirement. Systemic breakdown or pattern of non-compliance. Corrective action required within 30 days (or as specified). Too many majors = certification not granted.
- **Minor:** Isolated or non-systemic departure from SQF Code. Corrective action required by next audit.
- **Observation/Opportunity for Improvement:** Not a non-conformity, but a recommendation.

### Audit Types
- **Initial certification audit:** Full system audit. Two-stage audit process (document review + on-site audit).
- **Surveillance audit (recertification):** Annual. Must be completed within the certification period.
- **Unannounced audit option:** Some certification bodies offer. Can improve buyer confidence.
- **Follow-up audit:** After critical or major findings.

### Scoring/Rating
- SQF uses a rating system:
  - E (Excellent): ≥96% compliance, no critical or major findings
  - G (Good): 86-95% compliance
  - C (Compliance Needs Improvement): 76-85%
  - F (Fail): <76% or critical finding
- Rating appears on certificate and SQF Assessment Database

### Timeline (New Certification)
- Pre-assessment (optional but recommended): 1-2 months
- System development: 3-6 months (if starting from scratch)
- Implementation and records generation: 2-3 months (need operating records)
- Internal audit and management review: 1 month
- Certification body selection and scheduling: 1-2 months (lead times vary)
- Certification audit: 1-3 days on-site (varies by facility size/scope)
- Corrective actions: 30 days if needed
- Total: 8-14 months from start to certificate

### Cost Ranges
- SQF registration fee: ~$600-$1,500/year (to SQFI, based on company size)
- Certification body audit fees: $3,000-$15,000+ per audit (varies by CB, facility size, scope, travel)
- Consultant (if used): $5,000-$30,000+ for full system build
- SQF Practitioner training: $500-$1,500 per person
- HACCP training: $300-$800 per person
- Total first-year cost: $15,000-$60,000+ (highly variable)

## 3. BRCGS (BRC Global Standard for Food Safety)

### Governing Body
BRCGS (Brand Reputation Compliance Global Standards), part of LGC Group (London, UK). Formerly known as BRC Global Standards.

### Current Standard
- **Global Standard for Food Safety, Issue 9** (effective February 2023)
- 12 clauses, covering management systems through product control

### Clause Structure (Issue 9)
1. Senior Management Commitment
2. Food Safety and Quality Plan — HACCP (Codex Alimentarius-based)
3. Food Safety and Quality Management System (documentation, record-keeping)
4. Site Standards (external standards, security, layout, utilities, equipment)
5. Product Control (product design/development, allergen management, provenance/claims, packaging)
6. Process Control (control of operations, labeling, quantity control, calibration)
7. Personnel (training, personal hygiene, medical screening, protective clothing)
8. Production Risk Zones (high risk, high care, ambient high care — zone requirements based on product risk)
9. Requirements for Traded Products (products not manufactured on site but sold under the site's scope)
10. Rejection and Corrective Action
11. Food Defense
12. Management of Incidents, Product Withdrawal and Recall

### Fundamental Requirements (Issue 9)
These clauses are so critical that any Major non-conformity against them results in automatic failure regardless of overall score:
- 1.1 Senior Management Commitment and Continual Improvement
- 2 Food Safety and Quality Plan — HACCP
- 3.4 Internal Audits
- 3.5.1 & 3.5.2 Supplier Raw Material Approval and Monitoring
- 3.9 Traceability
- 4.2 Food Safety and Quality — Security
- 5.3 Allergen Management
- 5.6 Product Inspection and Laboratory Testing
- 6.1 Control of Operations
- 7.1 Training
- 8 Production Risk Zones (for applicable sites)

### Grading System
| Grade | Criteria | Audit Type |
|-------|---------|------------|
| AA | <5 minors, 0 majors, 0 critical. Announced audit. | Announced |
| AA+ | Same as AA. Unannounced audit. | Unannounced |
| A | 5-10 minors, 0 majors, 0 critical. Announced. | Announced |
| A+ | Same as A. Unannounced. | Unannounced |
| B | 11-20 minors, 0 majors, 0 critical. OR ≤10 minors + 1 major. Announced. | Announced |
| B+ | Same as B. Unannounced. | Unannounced |
| C | 21-30 minors, 0 critical. OR 11-20 minors + 1 major. OR ≤10 minors + 2 majors. Announced. | Announced |
| C+ | Same as C. Unannounced. | Unannounced |
| D | >30 minors. OR >20 minors + any major. OR >2 majors. OR any critical. | Announced |
| D+ | Same as D. Unannounced. | Unannounced |

**Note:** D grade = certification NOT granted. Must re-audit.
**Note:** Many retailers require minimum Grade A or B. Grade AA is the gold standard.

### Announced vs. Unannounced
- **Announced:** Audit date agreed in advance. Grades AA, A, B, C, D.
- **Unannounced (blended):** Two-part audit — Part 1 unannounced (GMP/facility focus), Part 2 announced (systems/records). Grades get "+" suffix. Most retailers prefer unannounced.
- **Fully unannounced:** Entire audit unannounced. Same "+" grading.

### BRCGS Directory
- All certified sites listed on BRCGS Compliance Database (online portal)
- Buyers can verify certification status, grade, scope, and audit date
- Certificate validity: 12 months (6 months if Grade C/D requiring re-audit)

### Timeline (New Certification)
- Gap assessment: 1-2 months
- System development and implementation: 4-8 months
- Operating records: minimum 3 months recommended
- Internal audit: 1 month
- Certification body selection/scheduling: 1-2 months
- Certification audit: 1-4 days on-site
- Total: 9-16 months from start to certificate

### Cost Ranges
- BRCGS registration fee: ~$1,100-$2,500/year (site registration on BRCGS directory)
- Certification body audit fees: $4,000-$20,000+ per audit
- Consultant: $8,000-$40,000+ for full system build
- Total first-year: $20,000-$75,000+ (highly variable)

## 4. FSSC 22000

### Governing Body
Foundation for Food Safety System Certification (FSSC), based in Gorinchem, The Netherlands.

### Standard Structure
FSSC 22000 is a composite standard built from three layers:
1. **ISO 22000:2018** — Food safety management system requirements (base standard)
2. **Sector-specific Prerequisite Programs (PRPs):**
   - ISO/TS 22002-1: Food manufacturing (most common)
   - ISO/TS 22002-2: Catering
   - ISO/TS 22002-3: Farming
   - ISO/TS 22002-4: Food packaging manufacturing
   - ISO/TS 22002-6: Feed and animal food
   - NEN/NTA 8059: Transport and storage
3. **FSSC 22000 Additional Requirements** — scheme-specific requirements covering:
   - Management of services and purchased materials
   - Product labeling
   - Food defense
   - Food fraud prevention
   - Logo use
   - Allergen management
   - Environmental monitoring
   - Product formulation management
   - Multi-site certification
   - Unannounced audits

### ISO 22000:2018 Key Elements
Based on ISO High Level Structure (Annex SL):
- Clause 4: Context of the Organization
- Clause 5: Leadership
- Clause 6: Planning
- Clause 7: Support (resources, competence, communication, documented information)
- Clause 8: Operation (PRPs, traceability, emergency preparedness, HACCP — called "hazard analysis" in ISO terms, operational prerequisite programs — oPRPs)
- Clause 9: Performance Evaluation (monitoring, internal audit, management review)
- Clause 10: Improvement (nonconformity, continual improvement, system update)

### Key Difference from SQF/BRC
FSSC 22000 uses **ISO management system audit methodology**, which is different from prescriptive checklist-based auditing:
- Focus on process approach and PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act)
- Auditors trace processes rather than audit clause-by-clause
- More emphasis on management commitment, objectives, and continual improvement
- Organizations already ISO-certified (ISO 9001, ISO 14001) find the management system approach familiar

### Certification Categories
- Category C (food processing) — most common for F&B manufacturers
- Category CI (food processing — perishable animal products)
- Category CII (food processing — perishable plant products)
- Category CIII (food processing — products with long shelf life at ambient temperature)
- Category CIV (food processing — (bio)chemical products)
- Category D (animal feed)
- Category E (catering)
- Category FI (retail/wholesale)
- Category G (transport/storage)
- Category I (food packaging)
- Category K (food broker/agent)

### Audit/Finding Classifications
- **Critical:** Direct and immediate food safety hazard. Certificate not issued or suspended.
- **Major:** Significant failure to meet requirement. Systemic issue.
- **Minor:** Isolated non-conformity. Not systemic.

### Certification Cycle
- Stage 1 audit: Documentation review (can be off-site or on-site)
- Stage 2 audit: Full on-site audit
- Certificate valid for 3 years
- Annual surveillance audits (Years 1 and 2)
- Recertification audit in Year 3
- Unannounced audits: FSSC 22000 requires at least 1 unannounced audit per 3-year cycle

### Timeline
- System development: 4-8 months (longer if no ISO experience)
- Implementation and records: 3-6 months
- Stage 1 audit: 1 day
- Stage 2 audit: 2-5 days (based on scope/size)
- Total: 10-18 months from start to certificate

### Cost Ranges
- FSSC 22000 registration: ~$600-$1,800/year
- Certification body fees: $5,000-$25,000+ per audit cycle
- ISO 22000 standard purchase: ~$150
- Consultant: $10,000-$50,000+ for full build
- Total first-year: $25,000-$80,000+

## 5. IFS Food

### Governing Body
IFS Management GmbH (Berlin, Germany). Originally developed by German and French retailers.

### Current Standard
IFS Food Standard, Version 8 (effective October 2023)

### Structure
- Part 1: Requirements for governance/management system (KO and Major/Minor scoring)
- Key chapters:
  1. Governance and Commitment
  2. Food Safety and Quality Management System
  3. Resource Management
  4. Operational Processes
  5. Measurements, Analysis, Improvement
  6. Food Defense

### Scoring System
Each requirement scored:
- **A:** Full compliance (20 points)
- **B:** Almost full compliance — minor deviation (15 points)
- **C:** Small part of requirement implemented (5 points — deduction of 15)
- **D:** Not implemented (-20 points — treated as Major)
- **KO (Knock Out):** 10 KO requirements. Score of D on any KO = automatic failure.

### KO Requirements (Version 8)
1. Senior management commitment
2. Monitoring system for each CCP
3. Personal hygiene
4. Raw material specifications
5. Product formulation/recipe compliance
6. Foreign body management
7. Traceability system
8. Internal audits
9. Product recall/withdrawal procedure
10. Corrective action management

### Scoring Threshold
- Must achieve ≥75% of total possible points to be certified
- Foundation Level: 75-95%
- Higher Level: >95%

### Certification Cycle
- Annual announced or unannounced audit
- Certificate validity: 12 months
- IFS Database: all certified sites listed publicly

### Cost
- Similar range to BRC: $15,000-$60,000+ first year including development and audit

## 6. GLOBALG.A.P.

### Governing Body
GLOBALG.A.P. (Cologne, Germany). Primarily focused on primary production (agriculture, aquaculture) rather than processing.

### Scope
- **Integrated Farm Assurance (IFA):** Crops, livestock, aquaculture
- **Chain of Custody (CoC):** For supply chain traceability
- **Harmonized Produce Safety Standard (HPSS):** Harmonized with FSMA Produce Safety Rule for US market

### Relevance to F&B Manufacturers
- Most relevant for companies sourcing raw agricultural products
- If a manufacturer also farms/grows (vertically integrated), GLOBALG.A.P. may be required for the primary production side
- Some retailers require GLOBALG.A.P. for fresh produce suppliers (especially European retailers)
- Less commonly required for processing facilities (SQF/BRC/FSSC more common for manufacturing)

### Certification
- Third-party audit by GLOBALG.A.P.-approved Certification Bodies
- Certificate validity: 12 months
- Control points scored: Major Musts (100% compliance required), Minor Musts (95% compliance required), Recommendations

## 7. Scheme Comparison

| Feature | SQF | BRCGS | FSSC 22000 | IFS |
|---------|-----|-------|------------|-----|
| Origin | Australia (now US-based) | UK | Netherlands | Germany/France |
| Base standard | SQF Code | BRC Global Standard | ISO 22000 + PRPs | IFS Standard |
| Audit approach | Prescriptive/checklist | Prescriptive/checklist | Process-based (ISO) | Prescriptive/scoring |
| Rating/grade | E, G, C, F | AA to D (with +) | Pass/Fail (with severity) | Foundation/Higher Level |
| Certification cycle | 12 months | 12 months (6 for C/D) | 3 years (annual surveillance) | 12 months |
| Quality component | Level 3 only | Not separate | Separate (not GFSI) | Integrated |
| Retailer preference (US) | Strong | Strong | Growing | Less common in US |
| Retailer preference (EU) | Less common | Strong | Strong | Strong |
| Unannounced option | Available | Available (+ grades) | Required 1 per cycle | Available |
| Cost range (first year) | $15K-$60K | $20K-$75K | $25K-$80K | $15K-$60K |

### Choosing a Scheme
Key decision factors:
1. **Customer/retailer requirement:** If your buyer specifies a scheme, that's your answer.
2. **Geography:** SQF and BRC dominant in North America. BRC, IFS, and FSSC 22000 dominant in Europe. FSSC 22000 growing globally.
3. **Existing management systems:** If you already have ISO 9001 or ISO 14001, FSSC 22000's ISO-based approach is a natural fit.
4. **Quality management needs:** If you need food safety + quality in one certification, SQF Level 3 or IFS are options.
5. **Cost and complexity:** SQF and IFS tend to have lower entry costs. FSSC 22000 can be higher due to ISO requirements.

## 8. Common Audit Deficiencies by Scheme Section

### SQF Common Findings
- 2.4.2 Management Review: Incomplete or infrequent management reviews. Missing action items follow-up.
- 2.5 Supplier Approval: Incomplete supplier approval program, missing risk assessments, no monitoring of approved suppliers.
- 2.6 Verification: Insufficient verification activities for food safety plan. Missing CCP trend analysis.
- 2.7 Traceability: Mock recall exercises incomplete or not conducted. Traceability gaps between receiving and shipping.
- Module 7 (GMP): Environmental sanitation issues, inadequate pest control documentation, equipment maintenance gaps, employee hygiene practices.

### BRC Common Findings
- Clause 3.5 (Supplier Management): Inadequate supplier approval procedures, missing risk assessments for raw materials.
- Clause 4.4 (Building Fabric): Facility maintenance gaps, roof/wall/floor integrity issues.
- Clause 4.11 (Housekeeping/Hygiene): Cleaning schedules not followed, inadequate cleaning verification.
- Clause 5.3 (Allergen Management): Incomplete allergen risk assessment, labeling errors, cross-contact controls insufficient.
- Clause 7.1 (Training): Insufficient training records, no competency verification, temporary staff not trained.

### FSSC 22000 Common Findings
- ISO 22000 Clause 7.1 (Resources): Competence gaps, insufficient training.
- ISO 22000 Clause 8.5.1 (Hazard Analysis): Incomplete hazard assessment, missing validation of control measures.
- ISO/TS 22002-1 Clause 10 (Cross-contamination): Environmental monitoring gaps, allergen controls.
- ISO/TS 22002-1 Clause 11 (Cleaning): Cleaning validation insufficient, inadequate master sanitation schedule.
- FSSC Additional Requirements: Food fraud vulnerability assessment incomplete, food defense plan gaps.
