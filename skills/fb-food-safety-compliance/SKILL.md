# F&B Food Safety & Regulatory Compliance

## When to Use

Activate this knowledge base when conversations involve:
- HACCP plan development, validation, or CCP troubleshooting for beverage or food manufacturing
- FDA/FSMA regulatory questions (Preventive Controls, Traceability Rule 204, FSVP, IA Rule)
- Allergen management programs, cross-contact risk assessment, cleaning validation
- GFSI audit preparation (SQF, BRC, FSSC 22000), organic/kosher/halal certification
- Environmental monitoring programs, pathogen controls, sanitation verification
- Regulatory submissions (FDA registration, acidified food filings, state cannery licenses)
- Recall planning, mock recall exercises, traceability system design
- Supplier approval programs, ingredient hazard analysis, COA review

## Key Concepts

### HACCP Advanced Principles
The seven HACCP principles (hazard analysis, CCP identification, critical limits, monitoring, corrective actions, verification, recordkeeping) form the mandatory foundation for juice processing (21 CFR 120) and low-acid/acidified canned foods (21 CFR 113/114). Beyond the basics, effective HACCP demands process-specific CCP identification (hot fill vs. aseptic vs. HPP vs. cold fill acidified), scientifically justified critical limits grounded in thermal lethality data (D-values, z-values, F0/P-values), statistically valid monitoring frequencies, and robust verification programs including environmental monitoring across four facility zones. Prerequisite programs (cGMP, SSOP, allergen control, pest management, water safety, supplier approval) must be fully operational before the HACCP plan can function. Validation requires scientific evidence — published literature, challenge studies, or process authority letters — demonstrating that critical limits achieve the required pathogen reduction (e.g., 5-log for juice HACCP).

### FSMA Regulatory Framework
The Food Safety Modernization Act shifted FDA from reactive enforcement to preventive controls. Key rules include: Preventive Controls for Human Food (21 CFR 117) requiring written food safety plans with hazard analysis, preventive controls, monitoring, corrective actions, verification, and recall plans — all overseen by a PCQI. The Food Traceability Rule (FSMA 204) mandates Key Data Elements at Critical Tracking Events for foods on the Food Traceability List, with full compliance required by January 20, 2026. FSVP governs imported ingredient verification. The Intentional Adulteration rule requires vulnerability assessments and mitigation strategies at actionable process steps. Facilities must maintain current FDA registration (biennial renewal in even years) and comply with inspection schedules risk-ranked by FDA.

### Allergen Management Programs
U.S. law (FALCPA as amended by FASTER Act 2021) mandates declaration of nine major allergens: milk, eggs, fish, crustacean shellfish, tree nuts, peanuts, wheat, soybeans, and sesame. Effective allergen programs go beyond labeling to include cross-contact prevention through dedicated lines, production sequencing, and validated cleaning procedures. Cleaning validation uses quantitative ELISA swab testing with action limits typically at 10 ppm of allergenic protein. Programs encompass supplier allergen questionnaires, ingredient change notification protocols, label review/approval workflows, and recall procedures for undeclared allergens — the single largest cause of FDA Class I recalls.

### Audit & Certification Systems
GFSI-benchmarked certification schemes (SQF, BRC, FSSC 22000) are effectively mandatory for suppliers to major retail and foodservice customers. Each scheme requires a functioning food safety management system, HACCP plan, prerequisite programs, and management commitment. SQF (Ed. 9) uses a three-level certification model with letter grading (A/B/C/Critical). BRC (Issue 9) employs fundamental requirements with announced or unannounced audit options and AA-through-D grading. FSSC 22000 builds on ISO 22000 with sector-specific PRPs. Additional certifications — USDA Organic (NOP), Kosher, Halal — layer ingredient sourcing, process supervision, and audit trail requirements on top of food safety fundamentals.
