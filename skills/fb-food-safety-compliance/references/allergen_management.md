# Allergen Management Program — Comprehensive Reference

## The FDA Big 9 Allergens

The Food Allergen Labeling and Consumer Protection Act of 2004 (FALCPA), as amended by the FASTER Act of 2021 (effective January 1, 2023), identifies nine major food allergens:

| Allergen | Scientific/Regulatory Name | Common Beverage Sources |
|----------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Milk | Milk, including casein, whey, lactalbumin, lactoglobulin | Dairy beverages, protein shakes, creamers, milk-based smoothies |
| Eggs | Eggs (all species) | Eggnog, some protein beverages, emulsifiers (lecithin if egg-derived) |
| Fish | Finfish (species must be declared) | Fish oil supplements in functional beverages, Worcestershire-based drinks |
| Crustacean shellfish | Crustacean shellfish (species must be declared) | Rare in beverages; possible in savory/broth drinks |
| Tree nuts | Tree nuts (specific nut must be declared): almonds, cashews, walnuts, pecans, pistachios, macadamia, hazelnuts, Brazil nuts, coconut, etc. | Almond milk, cashew milk, hazelnut beverages, nut-based protein drinks |
| Peanuts | Peanuts | Peanut butter smoothies, peanut protein beverages |
| Wheat | Wheat (includes spelt, kamut, emmer, einkorn) | Wheat grass shots, some malt beverages, grain-based drinks |
| Soybeans | Soybeans | Soy milk, soy protein isolate in beverages, soy lecithin |
| Sesame | Sesame | Sesame milk (emerging), tahini-based beverages, flavored beverages |

## FALCPA Labeling Requirements

### Mandatory Declaration
- All packaged foods regulated by FDA must declare the presence of any Big 9 allergen in the ingredient statement or in a separate "Contains" statement immediately following the ingredient list
- **"Contains" statement method:** "Contains: milk, soy, tree nuts (almond, coconut)"
- **Parenthetical method:** Allergen source declared in parentheses within the ingredient list, e.g., "whey protein concentrate (milk), soy lecithin (soy)"
- Both methods are acceptable; using both simultaneously is common practice and recommended for clarity
- The allergen source must use the common name of the food allergen (e.g., "milk" not "casein" alone)

### Declaration Scope
- Applies to food-contact incidental additives and processing aids if they contain allergens and are present in the finished product
- Highly refined oils (e.g., soybean oil, peanut oil): exempt from allergen declaration per FDA guidance if protein has been fully removed during refining — but the exemption applies only to highly refined oils, not cold-pressed or expeller-pressed oils
- Natural flavors and spice blends: must declare if they contain Big 9 allergens; "natural flavor" alone does not satisfy the requirement

### Labeling for Specific Allergen Situations
- **Coconut:** Classified as a tree nut by FDA. Must be declared as "tree nut (coconut)" even though it is botanically a drupe.
- **Soy lecithin:** Must be declared as "soy" even in small quantities as an emulsifier
- **Wheat starch:** Must be declared even if "gluten-free" claim is also made (gluten-free is separate from allergen declaration)

## Precautionary Allergen Labeling (PAL)

### "May Contain" Statements
- FDA does not regulate or require precautionary allergen labeling ("may contain traces of...", "produced in a facility that also processes...")
- PAL is **voluntary** and must not be used as a substitute for cGMP allergen controls
- FDA has stated that PAL should only be used when, after implementing good manufacturing practices, a quantifiable risk of cross-contact remains
- Overuse of PAL unnecessarily restricts food choices for allergic consumers and may trigger FDA scrutiny

### Best Practice for PAL
- Conduct a formal cross-contact risk assessment before applying PAL
- Use consistent language: "May contain [allergen]" or "Produced on shared equipment with [allergen]"
- Never use PAL for ingredients that are intentionally added (that requires declaration, not PAL)
- Document the justification for each PAL statement and review annually

## Allergen Control Plans for Shared Equipment

### Approach 1: Dedicated Lines
- Highest level of allergen control — separate equipment, separate room preferred
- No cross-contact risk; no cleaning validation needed for allergen removal
- Required by some customers for severe allergens (peanut, tree nut) or when Clean Label/allergen-free claims are made
- Equipment must be clearly labeled "DEDICATED — [product type]" with restricted access

### Approach 2: Allergen Sequencing (Production Scheduling)
- Schedule production from allergen-free products to increasingly allergenic products
- Example sequence: water-based beverages first, then soy-based, then dairy-based, then nut-based
- Run allergen-containing products last in the production day to minimize changeover risk
- At end of allergen production: full CIP/COP cleaning cycle with validated allergen removal
- Sequencing matrices should be documented and controlled by production scheduling team

### Approach 3: Cleaning Validation (Wet and Dry Methods)

**Wet Cleaning (CIP — Clean In Place):**
- Standard for beverage manufacturing — pipes, tanks, fillers, pasteurizers
- CIP cycle: pre-rinse (ambient water, 5 min) -> caustic wash (2-3% NaOH, 160-180 degF, 15-20 min) -> intermediate rinse -> acid wash (1-2% phosphoric or nitric acid, 140-160 degF, 10-15 min) -> final rinse -> sanitize (peracetic acid 150-200 ppm or hot water at 180 degF for 15 min)
- Allergen validation: post-CIP, pre-production swabs of food-contact surfaces

**Dry Cleaning:**
- Used for dry ingredient handling (powder blending, dry storage areas)
- Vacuuming, scraping, compressed air blowdown (with containment to prevent aerosolization)
- Physical removal must be followed by visual inspection under adequate lighting
- Dry cleaning alone is less effective for allergen removal than wet cleaning — validate carefully

## Cleaning Validation Methods

### ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay) Testing

**Quantitative ELISA (laboratory-based):**
- Gold standard for allergen cleaning validation
- Detects specific allergenic proteins (e.g., casein for milk, Ara h 1 for peanut, Cor a 9 for hazelnut)
- Sensitivity: LOD typically 1-5 ppm; LOQ typically 5-10 ppm depending on analyte and kit
- Results in 2-4 hours (lab turnaround: 1-3 business days for external labs)
- Used for initial validation and periodic re-validation (quarterly or annually)

**Lateral Flow Devices (LFD) — Rapid Screening:**
- Immunochromatographic strips for on-site rapid testing (5-10 minutes)
- Qualitative (positive/negative) or semi-quantitative results
- Typical sensitivity: 10-20 ppm (surface swab kits) or 10 ppm (rinse water kits)
- Useful for real-time production decisions; confirmed by quantitative ELISA if positive
- Must use allergen-specific kits; no universal allergen test exists

### Validation Protocol
1. **Establish the cleaning procedure** (documented SOP with chemical, concentration, time, temperature, mechanical action)
2. **Apply known allergen load** to equipment surface (representative of worst-case production conditions)
3. **Execute the cleaning procedure** exactly as written in the SOP
4. **Sample food-contact surfaces** using swab kits designed for the target allergen
   - Minimum 3 sampling points per piece of equipment (hardest to clean areas: dead legs, gaskets, valves, recesses)
   - Swab area: typically 10 cm x 10 cm (100 cm2) or 4 in x 4 in
5. **Analyze swabs** by quantitative ELISA
6. **Acceptance criteria:** All swabs < 10 ppm allergenic protein (some programs use 2.5 ppm or 5 ppm)
7. **Document results** and repeat for 3 consecutive successful cleanings to validate
8. **Revalidation triggers:** equipment modification, cleaning chemical change, new allergen introduced, complaint or positive result

### Action Limits
| Level | Swab Result | Action |
|-------|------------|--------|
| Pass | < 10 ppm | Release for production |
| Marginal | 10-20 ppm | Re-clean and re-swab; investigate root cause |
| Fail | > 20 ppm | Do not release; full re-clean, re-swab, corrective action |

Some GFSI schemes and customers require lower action limits (2.5 ppm or 5 ppm). Align limits with customer specifications and risk assessment.

## Supplier Allergen Management

### Supplier Allergen Questionnaires
Every ingredient supplier must complete an allergen questionnaire covering:
- Complete allergen profile of the ingredient (all Big 9 present in the product or as cross-contact)
- Facility allergen profile (all allergens handled in the facility, on shared lines, in the same room)
- Allergen control measures in place (dedicated lines, cleaning validation, sequencing)
- Allergen change notification commitment (written agreement to notify within 24-48 hours of any formulation or facility allergen change)
- Third-party audit status (GFSI or equivalent)

### Allergen Certificates (Certificate of Conformance)
- Lot-specific or annual allergen statement from supplier
- Must specify: allergens intentionally present and potential cross-contact allergens
- Must be signed by a quality-authorized representative
- Archived and cross-referenced with incoming lot records

### Ingredient Change Notification
- Suppliers must contractually agree to provide advance written notice of any change that could affect allergen status:
  - Reformulation or ingredient substitution
  - New allergen introduced to the facility
  - Change in supplier's supplier (sub-tier changes)
  - Change in production line or equipment sharing
- Notification must trigger a re-evaluation of the receiving facility's allergen risk assessment and label review

## Label Review and Approval Process

### New Product / Reformulation
1. **Formulation review:** R&D confirms complete allergen profile of all ingredients (primary ingredients, processing aids, incidental additives, carryover ingredients)
2. **Cross-contact assessment:** QA/Food Safety evaluates production line allergen map to identify cross-contact risks from shared equipment
3. **Label draft:** Regulatory affairs prepares label with allergen declaration (both "Contains" statement and parenthetical in ingredient list)
4. **QA review:** Verify label against formulation, cross-contact assessment, and regulatory requirements
5. **Legal review:** Confirm compliance with FALCPA, state requirements (e.g., California Prop 65 if applicable), and export market requirements (EU FIC Regulation 1169/2011 declares 14 allergens)
6. **Final approval:** Signed off by QA Manager, Regulatory Affairs, and Plant Manager before print
7. **Label reconciliation:** After production, verify correct labels used; count and reconcile unused labels

### Ongoing Label Verification
- **Pre-production check:** Operator verifies label matches production schedule and product code — documented on the line clearance checklist
- **First-article inspection:** QA verifies first labeled unit against approved proof
- **In-process checks:** Label correctness verified at defined intervals (e.g., every 30 minutes or per pallet)
- **Label storage:** Allergen-containing product labels stored separately from non-allergen labels; access controlled

## Undeclared Allergen Recall Procedures

Undeclared allergens are the single most common reason for FDA Class I recalls (highest health risk category).

### Recall Initiation Triggers
- Consumer complaint reporting allergic reaction linked to product
- Internal discovery during label review or production record audit
- Supplier notification of allergen change not captured on current label
- Positive allergen test result on finished product that contradicts label declaration
- FDA or state agency notification

### Recall Process
1. **Immediate assessment:** QA and management evaluate health hazard severity, affected lot codes, distribution scope
2. **Health Hazard Evaluation (HHE):** FDA classifies recalls — undeclared major allergens are almost always Class I
3. **Notification:** Directly notify all consignees (distributors, retailers) within 24 hours. Include: product name, lot codes, UPC, description of the issue, instructions for return/destruction
4. **Public notification:** FDA Recall notice, press release, social media if warranted
5. **Effectiveness checks:** Verify all consignees received notice and are taking action (FDA expects Level A effectiveness checks for Class I — 100% of consignees contacted)
6. **Product disposition:** Collect, segregate, and destroy or relabel (relabeling only if feasible and approved by FDA)
7. **Root cause investigation:** Trace back through production records, ingredient COAs, supplier communications, line clearance records
8. **Corrective and preventive actions (CAPA):** Implement controls to prevent recurrence — may include supplier changes, label approval process improvements, production scheduling changes, additional cleaning validation

### Mock Recall for Allergen Readiness
- Conduct at least annually (SQF, BRC require documented mock recalls)
- Select a random lot of an allergen-containing product
- Exercise: trace all ingredients to suppliers and all finished product to first customers within 4 hours
- Achieve >= 95% product recovery rate (by volume) as target
- Document findings, gaps, and corrective actions

## Cross-Contact Risk Assessment Matrix

### Risk Scoring Model
For each production line and allergen, assign scores:

**Likelihood of cross-contact (L):**
| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Dedicated line, no allergen present in facility zone |
| 2 | Allergen in facility but not on shared equipment; effective physical separation |
| 3 | Allergen on shared equipment with validated cleaning between runs |
| 4 | Allergen on adjacent line with potential airborne or splash transfer |
| 5 | Allergen on same line with unvalidated or manual-only cleaning |

**Severity (S):** All Big 9 allergens = 5 (potentially life-threatening anaphylaxis)

**Detectability (D):**
| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 1 | Quantitative testing every lot + visual verification |
| 2 | Rapid LFD testing every changeover + CIP verification |
| 3 | Visual inspection + ATP testing (no allergen-specific test) |
| 4 | Visual inspection only |
| 5 | No verification procedure |

**Risk Priority Number (RPN) = L x S x D**
- RPN > 50: Unacceptable — implement additional controls immediately
- RPN 25-50: High — improve controls, increase testing frequency
- RPN 10-24: Moderate — maintain current controls, verify effectiveness
- RPN < 10: Low — monitor during routine verification

## Allergen Mapping of Production Lines

### Purpose
Create a visual map showing every point in the production process where each allergen is present, introduced, transferred, or potentially cross-contacted.

### Mapping Procedure
1. List all products manufactured on each line with their allergen profiles
2. Walk the production line and identify: ingredient staging areas, addition points, transfer points, shared equipment, CIP circuits, waste streams, rework points
3. For each point, identify which allergens could be present and the route of potential transfer (direct contact, splash, airborne, rework)
4. Mark zones: allergen-free zones, controlled allergen zones, high-allergen zones
5. Document with annotated floor plans and process flow diagrams
6. Review and update whenever new products, new allergens, equipment changes, or layout changes occur

## Training Program Requirements

### All Production Personnel
- Big 9 allergen identification and seriousness of allergic reactions (annual refresher)
- Cross-contact prevention in their specific work area
- Reading and understanding production schedules for allergen sequencing
- Handwashing and protective clothing procedures between allergen changeovers
- What to do if a potential cross-contact event occurs (stop, notify, hold product)

### QA / Food Safety Team
- ELISA and LFD testing procedures, interpretation, and documentation
- Cleaning validation protocols and corrective actions for failed swabs
- Supplier allergen questionnaire review and ingredient assessment
- Label review and allergen regulatory requirements (FALCPA, FASTER Act)
- Recall procedures specific to undeclared allergens

### Management
- Allergen risk assessment methodology and review
- Regulatory obligations and recall liability
- Resource allocation for allergen control infrastructure (dedicated lines, testing equipment, training)
- Customer and certification body requirements for allergen management
