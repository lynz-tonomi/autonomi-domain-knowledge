# Audit & Certification Systems — Deep Reference

## GFSI-Benchmarked Schemes Overview

The Global Food Safety Initiative (GFSI) does not certify facilities directly. It benchmarks certification schemes against its requirements. Major retailers (Walmart, Costco, Kroger, Target, Whole Foods) and foodservice companies require GFSI-benchmarked certification from their suppliers. The three dominant schemes for beverage manufacturing facilities are SQF, BRC, and FSSC 22000.

## SQF (Safe Quality Food) — SQF Code Edition 9

### Certification Levels
| Level | Name | Scope | Typical Use |
|-------|------|-------|-------------|
| Level 1 | Food Safety Fundamentals | Basic food safety controls, pre-HACCP | Small/low-risk operations, primary producers |
| Level 2 | HACCP-Based Food Safety Plan | Certified food safety management system with HACCP | Required minimum for most retail/foodservice supplier approval |
| Level 3 | Comprehensive Food Safety and Quality Management | Food safety + food quality plan | Full quality management; covers product quality, consistency, legality |

Most beverage manufacturers targeting major retail or foodservice accounts need **SQF Level 2** minimum; Level 3 is increasingly expected by premium buyers.

### SQF Code Structure (Ed. 9)
- **Module 2:** SQF System Elements (applicable to all facilities)
  - 2.1 Management commitment, food safety policy, management review
  - 2.2 Document control and records
  - 2.3 Specification and product development (ingredient specs, finished product specs, supplier approval)
  - 2.4 Food safety system — hazard analysis, food safety plan, prerequisite programs
  - 2.5 SQF system verification (internal audits, corrective/preventive action, product release, product tracing)
  - 2.6 Product identification, trace, withdrawal, and recall
  - 2.7 Food defense and food fraud (vulnerability assessment, mitigation, food fraud vulnerability assessment per GFSI v2020+)
  - 2.8 Training
  - 2.9 Allergen management
- **Module 11:** Good Manufacturing Practices for Processing of Food Products (sector-specific GMP module for beverage manufacturers)
  - Site and building requirements, utilities (water, ice, air, steam), waste disposal, exterior grounds
  - Food-contact equipment design (3-A Sanitary Standards for dairy/beverage equipment)
  - Cleaning and sanitation, pest management, personal hygiene, transportation and storage

### Audit Scoring and Grading
SQF uses a numerical scoring system:
- Each element rated: **Compliant (C), Minor Non-Conformance (Mi), Major Non-Conformance (Ma), Critical Non-Conformance (Cr)**

**Grade Calculation:**
| Grade | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **A (Excellent)** | Score >= 96%, no critical or major non-conformances |
| **B (Good)** | Score 86-95%, no more than 1 major, no critical |
| **C (Needs Improvement)** | Score 76-85%, or more than 1 major non-conformance |
| **Critical Failure** | Any critical non-conformance, or score < 76% — certification denied or suspended |

**Critical Non-Conformance examples:**
- Absence of a food safety plan or HACCP plan
- Failure in CCP management that could result in production and release of unsafe product
- Evidence of food fraud or intentional adulteration
- Obstruction of the audit process

### Audit Protocol
- **Certification audit:** Full system audit by a licensed SQF Certification Body (CB) — typically 1.5-3 days depending on facility size and scope
- **Surveillance audit:** Unannounced or announced interim audit between annual certification audits — performed by the CB
- **Recertification audit:** Annual, on the anniversary of the initial certification (within a 30-day window)
- **Unannounced option:** Facilities may opt-in to the SQFI unannounced audit program; increasingly encouraged
- SQF practitioner must be designated on-site (equivalent to a food safety team leader)

## BRC Global Standard for Food Safety (Issue 9)

### Standard Structure
BRC Issue 9 (effective February 2023) contains 7 sections:

1. **Senior Management Commitment** — food safety culture, policy, objectives, management review, organizational structure
2. **The Food Safety Plan — HACCP** — Codex Alimentarius-based, prerequisite programs, hazard analysis, CCPs
3. **Food Safety and Quality Management System** — food safety/quality policy, document control, specifications, internal audit, purchasing and supplier approval, corrective/preventive action, traceability, complaint handling, recall/withdrawal
4. **Site Standards** — external standards, building fabric and services, utilities, equipment, housekeeping, contamination control (foreign body, chemical, allergen), waste, pest management
5. **Product Control** — product development, allergen management, provenance/origin/sourcing claims, product packaging, product inspection/testing, product release, pet food (if applicable)
6. **Process Control** — control of operations, labeling, weight/volume control, calibration, product tracing
7. **Personnel** — training, personal hygiene, medical screening, protective clothing, facilities for personnel

### Fundamental Requirements
BRC designates certain clauses as **"fundamental"** — failure on any fundamental clause automatically results in non-certification or certification withdrawal. Fundamental requirements include:
- Senior management commitment and continual improvement (1.1)
- The food safety plan — HACCP (2)
- Internal audits (3.4)
- Corrective and preventive actions (3.7)
- Traceability (3.9)
- Housekeeping and hygiene (4.11)
- Allergen management (5.3)
- Control of operations (6.1)
- Training (7.1)

### Audit Protocol
**Announced audit program:**
- Planned date agreed between site and CB
- Full audit of all requirements
- Re-audit within 28 days if major non-conformance raised

**Unannounced audit program (two options):**
- **Option 1:** Single unannounced full audit replacing the announced audit — site does not know the exact date (within the audit due window)
- **Option 2:** Two-part audit — Part 1 unannounced (GMP/site standards focus), Part 2 announced (documentation/systems focus)
- Unannounced audits earn a grade prefix "+" (e.g., AA+ is the highest possible grade)

### Grading System
| Grade | Non-Conformances | Certificate Duration |
|-------|-----------------|---------------------|
| **AA** | <= 5 minor, no major, no critical | 12 months |
| **A** | 6-10 minor, no major, no critical | 12 months |
| **B** | 11-16 minor, OR 1 major + <= 10 minor, no critical | 12 months |
| **C** | 17-24 minor, OR 1 major + 11-16 minor, no critical | 6 months (re-audit required) |
| **D** | 25+ minor, OR 2+ majors, OR 1 critical, no critical on fundamental | 6 months (re-audit required) |
| **Non-certified** | Critical on a fundamental requirement, or other defined failure criteria | No certificate issued |

### Non-Conformance Definitions
- **Critical:** Direct failure in food safety or legal compliance; an immediate or serious risk
- **Major:** Substantial failure to meet the intent of a clause; a significant doubt about product conformity or safety
- **Minor:** Clause not fully met but product safety is not in doubt based on objective evidence; a lapse in management system compliance

## FSSC 22000 (Food Safety System Certification)

### Framework
FSSC 22000 is built on three pillars:
1. **ISO 22000:2018** — Food Safety Management System requirements (management system structure, hazard analysis, operational PRPs, HACCP plan)
2. **Sector-specific PRPs:**
   - **ISO/TS 22002-1:2009** — for food manufacturing (including beverage)
   - ISO/TS 22002-4 — for food packaging manufacturing
3. **Additional FSSC 22000 requirements** — food defense, food fraud mitigation, allergen management, environmental monitoring, labeling, logo use, multi-site management

### ISO 22000:2018 Key Elements
- **Context of the organization:** Internal/external issues, interested party requirements, scope definition
- **Leadership:** Food safety policy, roles and responsibilities, food safety culture (added emphasis in v5.1)
- **Planning:** Hazard analysis, risk and opportunity assessment, food safety objectives
- **Support:** Resources, competence, awareness, communication, documented information
- **Operation:** Prerequisite programs, traceability, emergency preparedness, hazard analysis (per Codex steps), control measure validation, monitoring/measurement, nonconformity handling
- **Performance evaluation:** Internal audit, management review, analysis of results
- **Improvement:** Nonconformity, corrective action, continual improvement, food safety management system updating

### ISO/TS 22002-1 PRP Topics (Beverage Manufacturing)
1. Construction and layout of buildings
2. Layout of premises including workspace and employee facilities
3. Utilities — air, water, energy
4. Waste disposal
5. Equipment suitability, cleaning, and maintenance
6. Management of purchased materials (supplier approval, incoming material specifications)
7. Measures for prevention of cross-contamination (physical, allergen, microbiological)
8. Cleaning and sanitizing
9. Pest control
10. Personnel hygiene and employee facilities
11. Rework
12. Product recall procedures
13. Warehousing
14. Product information / consumer awareness
15. Food defense, biovigilance, and bioterrorism

### FSSC 22000 Certification Process
- Stage 1 audit: documentation review, readiness assessment (on-site or remote)
- Stage 2 audit: full implementation assessment (on-site, 2-5 days depending on scope and size)
- Surveillance audits: annual (within 12 months of initial certification)
- Recertification audit: every 3 years
- Unannounced audits: FSSC 22000 requires CBs to conduct at least one unannounced audit per certification cycle

## USDA Organic Certification (National Organic Program — NOP)

### Applicability to Beverage Manufacturers
Any operation that handles, processes, or packages organic products and sells them as "organic" must be certified by a USDA-accredited certifying agent — unless exempt (operations with <= $5,000 in annual organic sales may be exempt from certification but must still comply with NOP standards).

### Labeling Categories
| Label Claim | Requirement |
|-------------|-------------|
| **"100% Organic"** | All ingredients must be certified organic (excluding water and salt) |
| **"Organic"** | >= 95% organic ingredients (by weight, excluding water and salt); remaining 5% must be from the National List or non-agricultural |
| **"Made with Organic [ingredients]"** | >= 70% organic ingredients; cannot use USDA Organic seal |
| **< 70% organic ingredients** | May list organic ingredients in ingredient statement only; no organic claim on principal display panel |

### Handling Requirements for Beverage Manufacturers
- **Organic System Plan (OSP):** Written plan describing practices and procedures, including ingredient sourcing, pest management, cleaning, commingling prevention, and record keeping
- **Commingling and contamination prevention:** Organic and non-organic products must not be commingled. If shared equipment is used, cleaning procedure must be documented and effective. Dedicated lines preferred but not required.
- **Processing aids and ingredients:** Only those on the National List of Allowed and Prohibited Substances (7 CFR 205.605-606) are permitted in organic processing. Common allowed non-organic ingredients: citric acid, ascorbic acid, pectin (non-amidated), potassium hydroxide.
- **Prohibited substances:** Synthetic processing aids not on the National List, ionizing radiation, sewage sludge (not applicable to beverage), genetic engineering (GMO ingredients prohibited)
- **Pest management:** Preventive practices first; if pests found, mechanical/physical methods, then biological methods. Synthetic pesticides inside the facility prohibited. Approved substances list maintained by OMRI (Organic Materials Review Institute).

### Audit Trail and Records
- Organic certificates for all organic ingredient suppliers (current, verified against certifying agent database)
- Transaction certificates for each lot of organic ingredients received
- Mass balance records demonstrating organic input >= organic output + shrinkage/waste
- Lot traceability: organic ingredients traced from receiving through finished product shipment
- Cleaning records between organic and non-organic production runs (if shared equipment)
- Records retained for minimum 5 years

### Annual Inspection
- Unannounced or announced on-site inspection by certifying agent
- Full audit of OSP implementation, record review, mass balance, facility tour
- Residue testing: certifying agents may collect samples for pesticide residue testing (selected based on risk)

## Kosher Certification for Beverages

### Overview
Kosher certification is granted by a rabbinical agency that supervises production to ensure compliance with Jewish dietary law (halacha). Major agencies: OU (Orthodox Union), OK, Star-K, Kof-K, CRC.

### Key Requirements for Beverages
- **Ingredient review:** All ingredients, processing aids, release agents, and incidental additives must be kosher-approved. Most fruit juices, water, sugar, citric acid are inherently kosher. Problematic ingredients: grape juice/wine (must be produced under rabbinical supervision), gelatin (animal-derived — pork-derived is prohibited), carmine/cochineal (insect-derived — prohibited), some emulsifiers, flavors, and vitamins.
- **Equipment:** If equipment was previously used for non-kosher products, it must be "koshered" (cleaned according to specific halachic procedures — often involving hot water at boiling temperature, called "hag'ala"). Dedicated equipment simplifies compliance.
- **Dairy vs. Pareve:** Products containing dairy ingredients are classified "Dairy" (OU-D). Products without any dairy or meat derivatives are "Pareve." Dairy and Pareve products should ideally be produced on separate equipment or with kosherization between runs.
- **Grape products:** Grape juice and wine require special supervision from grape crushing through bottling. Kosher grape juice concentrate is available from certified sources.
- **Supervision level:** Ranges from initial ingredient and facility approval with periodic audits (mashgiach visits) to full-time on-site rabbinical supervision (required for grape wine/juice and some other sensitive categories).

## Halal Certification for Beverages

### Overview
Halal certification verifies that products comply with Islamic dietary law. Major U.S. agencies: IFANCA (Islamic Food and Nutrition Council of America), ISWA (Islamic Services of America), HFA (Halal Food Authority).

### Key Requirements
- **Prohibited (Haram) ingredients:** Alcohol (ethanol — even as a processing solvent; threshold varies by certifying body, typically < 0.5% naturally occurring is permitted in some interpretations), pork-derived ingredients (gelatin, certain emulsifiers), blood, carnivorous animal derivatives, insects (carmine/shellac)
- **Questionable (Mashbooh) ingredients:** Flavors extracted with alcohol, enzymes of unknown animal origin, L-cysteine (can be animal or synthetic), mono- and diglycerides (can be plant or animal), glycerin (can be plant or animal)
- **Equipment:** Must not be shared with haram products unless properly cleaned (requirements vary by certifying body)
- **Audit:** Initial facility and ingredient audit, followed by periodic inspections (quarterly to annually depending on risk and certifying body)
- **Alcohol in beverages:** Beverages marketed as Halal must not contain alcohol as an ingredient. Natural fermentation producing trace ethanol (e.g., kombucha) is a gray area — many Halal certifiers will not certify kombucha, or require < 0.5% ABV confirmed by testing.

## FDA Inspection Readiness

### Mock Recall Procedures
Per FSMA Preventive Controls (21 CFR 117.139) and GFSI scheme requirements:
1. **Frequency:** At least annually; some schemes and customers require semi-annually
2. **Selection:** Randomly select a finished product lot code (preferably one involving an allergen-containing product and an FTL ingredient for combined traceability testing)
3. **Forward trace (downstream):** From the selected lot code, identify all direct consignees, quantities shipped, and ship dates. Target: 100% of distributed product identified within 4 hours.
4. **Backward trace (upstream):** From the selected lot code, identify all raw material suppliers, lot codes, and receiving dates. Target: 100% of ingredients traced within 4 hours.
5. **Mass balance:** Calculate total input ingredient quantity vs. total finished product output + waste. Reconciliation should account for >= 95% of material.
6. **Documentation review:** Verify production records, CCP logs, allergen checks, label verification records are complete and retrievable.
7. **Communication test:** Simulate notification of direct consignees — test contact information accuracy.
8. **Report:** Document results, time to complete, percentage recovered, gaps identified, corrective actions.

### Traceability Exercise Best Practices
- Maintain a current customer contact list (verified quarterly)
- Electronic traceability systems (ERP with lot tracking) dramatically reduce trace time — target < 2 hours for both directions
- Include co-manufacturers, 3PL warehouses, and broker/distributor chains in the exercise
- Test both product-to-ingredient and ingredient-to-product traces
- Include a product that has been reworked to test rework traceability

### Record Availability for FDA Inspectors
- All records required by 21 CFR 117 must be available within **24 hours** of an FDA request (or sooner for electronic records)
- HACCP/Food Safety Plan must be available immediately upon request
- CCP monitoring records, corrective action records, verification records: retained for 2 years minimum
- Supplier approval records, COAs, allergen documentation: readily retrievable
- Training records: current and past employees, documented by date, topic, attendee signature
- Calibration records: instrument-specific, with reference standards documented
- Environmental monitoring data: trending reports, positive findings, corrective actions

### Common FDA Inspection Observations (Form 483)
Top observations for beverage facilities in recent years:
1. Failure to conduct a hazard analysis or document the food safety plan
2. Failure to implement preventive controls for identified hazards
3. Inadequate environmental monitoring program for *Listeria* in RTE facilities
4. Failure to verify preventive controls are effective (no validation, no record review)
5. Inadequate supplier approval program for high-risk imported ingredients
6. Allergen labeling deficiencies or inadequate cross-contact controls
7. Insanitary conditions (equipment design, facility maintenance, pest evidence)
8. Failure to file acidified food or LACF process with FDA (Form 2541 series)
9. Inadequate employee training documentation
10. Failure to maintain required records or make them available during inspection
