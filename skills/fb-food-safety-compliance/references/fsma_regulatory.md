# FSMA Regulatory Framework — Deep Reference

## Preventive Controls for Human Food (PCHF) — 21 CFR Part 117

The PCHF rule replaced the older cGMP-only framework with a mandatory requirement for a written Food Safety Plan. All facilities that manufacture, process, pack, or hold human food must comply unless a specific exemption applies.

### Exemptions and Modified Requirements
- Facilities solely engaged in storage of packaged foods not exposed to the environment: exempt from food safety plan but must comply with cGMPs
- Qualified facilities (averaging < $1M in total food sales over prior 3 years, adjusted for inflation): modified requirements — no food safety plan but must submit attestation to FDA
- Farms as defined in 21 CFR 1.227: exempt (on-farm manufacturing/processing may qualify for qualified facility or farm mixed-type facility exemptions)
- Juice processors complying with 21 CFR 120 (Juice HACCP): exempt from preventive controls requirements but must comply with cGMPs in Part 117 Subpart B
- Low-acid canned food processors under 21 CFR 113: exempt from preventive controls for thermal processing hazards but must comply with cGMPs

### Hazard Analysis (Subpart C, 117.130)
- Must identify and evaluate known or reasonably foreseeable biological, chemical (including radiological), and physical hazards for each type of food manufactured
- Evaluation considers severity of illness/injury and probability of occurrence in the absence of preventive controls
- Hazard analysis must be written and conducted by or reviewed by a PCQI

### Preventive Controls (117.135-117.145)

**Process Controls:**
- Parameters and minimum/maximum values for the process (e.g., time, temperature, pH, aw, pressure)
- Example for hot-fill juice: pasteurization at 185 degF (85 degC) for 15 seconds minimum

**Allergen Controls:**
- Cross-contact prevention procedures (dedicated lines, sequencing, validated cleaning between allergen and non-allergen runs)
- Label verification: every production run must verify correct label applied to correct product

**Sanitation Controls:**
- Required when environmental pathogens are a hazard (e.g., *Listeria monocytogenes* in RTE exposed-to-environment products)
- Environmental monitoring program per FDA draft guidance

**Supply Chain Controls (Subpart G):**
- Required when a hazard is controlled by a supplier rather than the receiving facility
- Receiving facility must: approve suppliers based on hazard analysis, verify supplier controls (audit, sampling, review of supplier's food safety records), document the supply chain program
- Supply chain-applied controls may include: supplier audit (on-site, by qualified auditor), lot-by-lot COA review, periodic testing

### Monitoring (117.145)
- Written procedures specifying what is monitored, how, frequency, and who performs
- Monitoring must be adequate to assure preventive controls are consistently performed

### Corrective Actions and Corrections (117.150)
- **Corrections** (minor deviations, not requiring evaluation): isolated incidents, no product safety impact
- **Corrective actions** (when preventive control is not properly implemented): identify problem, reduce likelihood of recurrence, evaluate affected food for safety, prevent adulterated food from entering commerce

### Verification (117.155-117.165)
- Validation: scientific evidence that preventive controls effectively control the identified hazard (must occur within 90 days of production or within a reasonable timeframe for gathering data)
- Verification activities: calibration of monitoring instruments, review of monitoring and corrective action records (within 7 working days or reasonable timeframe), product testing, environmental monitoring
- Reanalysis of food safety plan: at least every 3 years or whenever a significant change occurs

### Recall Plan (117.139(a))
- Required for every food for which a hazard requiring a preventive control has been identified
- Must include: procedures for notification of direct consignees, instructions for how to return/dispose of product, procedures to notify the public (if appropriate), effectiveness checks
- Mock recall: not explicitly required by rule but expected by auditors and recommended by FDA guidance — test traceability system upstream and downstream within 4 hours

### PCQI (Preventive Controls Qualified Individual) — 117.180
- Must have successfully completed training in preventive controls equivalent to the FDA-recognized standardized curriculum (currently: FSPCA Preventive Controls for Human Food course)
- Or have job experience sufficient to develop and apply a food safety system
- PCQI responsibilities: oversee or perform hazard analysis and food safety plan development, validation, record review, reanalysis
- A facility may use a consultant PCQI but must have on-site personnel who understand and can implement the plan

## FSMA 204 — Food Traceability Rule (21 CFR Part 1, Subpart S)

### Overview
Published November 15, 2022. Compliance date: **January 20, 2026** for all covered entities. Applies to foods on the FDA Food Traceability List (FTL), which was developed based on risk-ranking model considering food/hazard pairs.

### Food Traceability List — Beverages Covered
- Fresh-cut fruits and vegetables (including those used in smoothie/juice bar operations)
- Shell eggs (if used in beverages)
- Nut butters (if used as an ingredient in beverages)
- Fresh herbs (basil, cilantro, parsley — if used in functional beverages)
- Leafy greens (spinach, kale — relevant for green juices and smoothies)
- Fresh/frozen fruits: soft fruits used in juice manufacturing (not citrus or apples)
- Finfish (not typically in beverages, but relevant if producing fish-based products)
- Ready-to-eat deli salads: not directly relevant but indicates the risk-based approach

**Note:** Shelf-stable beverages that have received a kill step (pasteurized, retorted, aseptically processed) and are not produced from FTL ingredients do not trigger FSMA 204 requirements. Fresh juices, cold-pressed juices, and HPP juices made from FTL produce are covered.

### Key Data Elements (KDEs) and Critical Tracking Events (CTEs)

**CTEs with Required KDEs:**

**Growing (for farms supplying FTL produce to juice operations):**
- Traceability lot code, commodity, quantity, unit of measure, location of growth, harvest date, cooling location (if applicable), TLC source reference

**Receiving:**
- Traceability lot code of incoming food, quantity, unit of measure, commodity, date received, location of receipt, location identifier of sender, reference document (PO, BOL), TLC source

**Transformation (applies to juice/beverage manufacturers blending or processing FTL ingredients):**
- New traceability lot code assigned to output, commodity of output, quantity of output, all input TLCs and their quantities, date of transformation, location of transformation

**Shipping:**
- Traceability lot code, quantity, commodity, date shipped, location shipping from, location shipping to, reference document (BOL, invoice)

### Recordkeeping Requirements
- Records must be maintained for 2 years
- Records must be available within 24 hours of FDA request (electronic or paper)
- Traceability plan must be established describing: procedures for assigning TLCs, identifying FTL foods, maintaining required records
- Records must include a reference document (invoice, BOL, receipt) linking each CTE

### Exemptions
- Farms (except those with a farm mixed-type facility that packs or holds FTL food)
- Restaurants and retail food establishments (unless they manufacture/process FTL foods for sale to other businesses)
- Facilities that only manufacture shelf-stable products from fully processed (non-FTL) ingredients
- Very small businesses (< $1M in food sales averaged over 3 years): partial exemption — must keep records but modified requirements
- Commingled raw agricultural commodities: partial exemption with modified recordkeeping

## Foreign Supplier Verification Program (FSVP) — 21 CFR Part 1, Subpart L

### Applicability
Every importer of food (the U.S. owner or consignee at time of entry) must comply. Importers must verify that food from foreign suppliers meets U.S. safety standards.

### Requirements
1. **Hazard analysis** for each imported food
2. **Supplier evaluation and approval:** evaluate supplier's compliance history, food safety practices, and results of testing/audits
3. **Verification activities** — risk-based, may include:
   - On-site audit by a qualified auditor (required when hazard is controlled by the supplier and the hazard requires preventive controls per 21 CFR 117)
   - Sampling and testing of the food
   - Review of supplier's food safety records
4. **Corrective actions** when verification reveals a problem
5. **Periodic reassessment** of supplier performance and verification activities (at least every 3 years, or whenever new information becomes available)
6. **Importer identification** at entry: must be identified as the FSVP importer in import entry records

### Common Import Categories for Beverage Manufacturers
- Fruit concentrates (citrus, tropical — often from Brazil, Mexico, China)
- Botanical extracts and functional ingredients (adaptogens, nootropics)
- Organic ingredients (verified through organic certificates plus FSVP)
- Acidulants and processing aids (citric acid, ascorbic acid)

## Intentional Adulteration (IA) Rule — 21 CFR Part 121

### Applicability
Facilities required to register under 21 CFR 1.225 with activities involving the manufacture, processing, packing, or holding of food — unless exempt (e.g., facilities with < $10M in total annual sales plus their affiliated entities, warehousing and holding facilities, farms).

### Vulnerability Assessment
Must identify significant vulnerabilities at each point, step, or procedure (actionable process steps) by evaluating:
1. **Potential public health impact** — severity and scale
2. **Degree of physical access** to the product
3. **Ability of an attacker to contaminate** the product successfully
4. FDA has identified four key activity types: bulk liquid receiving and loading, liquid storage and handling, secondary ingredient handling, mixing and similar activities

### Mitigation Strategies
- Each actionable process step must have a mitigation strategy
- Examples: tamper-evident seals on ingredient containers, restricted access via locks/badges, electronic monitoring (cameras, alarms), buddy system in sensitive areas, seal verification on tanker trucks
- Mitigation strategies must be monitored, corrective actions established, and verification performed

### Food Defense Plan
- Written plan covering vulnerability assessment, mitigation strategies, monitoring, corrective actions, verification
- Must be reanalyzed at least every 3 years
- Personnel with food defense roles must complete training (FSPCA IA training curriculum)

## Produce Safety Rule — 21 CFR Part 112

### Applicability to Juice Ingredients
Applies to farms growing produce that is a raw agricultural commodity (RAC) normally consumed raw or that will be used in products where the produce is not subject to a kill step. For juice operations:
- If the farm grows produce that is sold directly to a juice processor who applies a validated kill step: farm may be exempt under the commercial processing exemption (112.2(b)) — but farm must obtain written assurance from the buyer
- If produce is consumed without a kill step (fresh juice, smoothie bar): Produce Safety Rule applies fully to the growing farm

### Key Requirements When Applicable
- Agricultural water quality standards (generic *E. coli* testing: geometric mean <= 126 CFU/100 mL, STV <= 410 CFU/100 mL)
- Biological soil amendments (composting standards, application intervals)
- Worker health and hygiene
- Equipment, tools, and buildings sanitation
- Domesticated and wild animal intrusion prevention

## FDA Registration and Facility Inspections

### Registration (21 CFR Part 1, Subpart H)
- All domestic and foreign facilities that manufacture, process, pack, or hold food for U.S. consumption must register with FDA
- Biennial renewal required in even-numbered years (October 1 - December 31)
- Failure to renew: FDA may consider facility registration expired — food from unregistered facility is subject to refusal at import or hold at domestic facility
- Registration number is facility-specific, not product-specific

### Inspection Frequency
- FDA risk-ranks facilities: high-risk facilities inspected every 3 years minimum, non-high-risk every 5 years minimum (FSMA mandate — 21 U.S.C. 350j)
- Juice HACCP processors and LACF/acidified food processors are typically classified as high-risk
- Foreign facility inspections: FDA has expanded foreign inspection capacity but frequency varies; registered foreign facilities must permit FDA inspection

### Inspection Types
- Routine surveillance inspections (announced or unannounced)
- For-cause inspections (triggered by complaints, illness, positive sample)
- Compliance follow-up inspections (after warning letter or import alert)

## State-Level Requirements

### California CDPH — Processed Food Registration (PFR)
- All facilities manufacturing, processing, or packing food in California must register with CDPH Food and Drug Branch
- Application: Form FDB-612, annual renewal fee based on gross food sales (tiered fee schedule)
- Facility must pass initial inspection before registration is granted

### Acidified Food and Low-Acid Canned Food Registration
- Facilities must register with FDA via **Form FDA 2541** (Food Canning Establishment Registration)
- Each process must be filed via **Form FDA 2541a** (Process Filing for Acidified Method) or **2541c** (for LACF)
- Filing must be done before commercial distribution of any new product or process
- Process filings must be supported by a Process Authority letter documenting scheduled process adequacy
- California additionally requires a **Cannery License** from CDPH for any facility producing canned (hermetically sealed) food

### FDA Form 2541 Series Summary
| Form | Use | When |
|------|-----|------|
| 2541 | Food Canning Establishment Registration | One-time, before first production |
| 2541a | Acidified Food Process Filing | Per product/process, before commercial distribution |
| 2541c | Low-Acid Canned Food Process Filing | Per product/process, before commercial distribution |
| 2541d | Process Amendment | When changing an existing filed process |
| 2541e | Process Filing for Aseptic Processing | For aseptic systems, before commercial distribution |
| 2541g | Supplemental Listing (additional products) | When adding products to existing registration |

### Other State Requirements
- **New York:** Article 20-C registration for food processing establishments
- **Texas:** DSHS Food Manufacturer Registration
- **Florida:** DACS Food Permit — annual registration with the Division of Food Safety
- Each state may impose additional labeling, licensing, or inspection requirements beyond federal standards
- Beverage manufacturers distributing nationally should maintain a state compliance matrix and renew all licenses proactively
